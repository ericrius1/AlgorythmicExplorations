The Reach

The bear can bend — part two wired every vertex to a skeleton — but so far someone has to dial each joint by hand. This article inverts the arrangement: stop choosing angles, start choosing places. A firefly is drifting around the bear right now; the gaze tracking it and the paw that reaches when it strays close are both solutions to the same small problem, solved sixty times a second.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part two ended with a confession: forward kinematics is a wonderful machine pointed the wrong way. Give it joint angles and it produces world positions — one matrix pass, parents first, no ambiguity, no failure. But nobody directing a character thinks in shoulder quaternions. Direction happens in the world: put the paw on the orb. Look at the firefly. The animator names the destination; the angles are the machine's problem. Computing angles from places is inverse kinematics, and it is harder than the forward kind for an honest reason: running a function backwards inherits none of its guarantees.

A target can be out of reach — then there is no answer. A target can be close — then there are infinitely many, a whole circle of elbows that all put the paw on the same spot (your own elbow can trace that circle while your fingertip stays put on the table). A good IK solver has to manufacture the missing guarantees: clamp the impossible, and choose among the infinite. Both, it turns out, fit in a page of code.

Step 1 · One triangleThe law of cosines, employed

Strip the arm to its skeleton: an upper bone of length L1 (shoulder to elbow), a lower bone of length L2 (elbow to wrist), and a target at distance d from the shoulder. The shoulder, the elbow, and the wrist-on-target form a triangle with side lengths L1, L2, d — and a triangle with three known sides has no secrets. The law of cosines, the one from school, hands over the angle at the shoulder directly: 

// angle at the shoulder between "toward the target" and the upper bone
const cosA = (d * d + L1 * L1 - L2 * L2) / (2 * d * L1);
const a = Math.acos(clamp(cosA, -1, 1));

No iteration, no convergence test, no solver in sight — for a two-bone limb the inverse problem is closed form. This is why nearly every arm and leg in games is driven by exactly this formula. The impossible cases are handled before the trigonometry ever runs, by clamping d into the only range where a triangle exists: 

// no triangle past full extension (L1 + L2) or full fold (|L1 - L2|)
const d = clamp(dist(shoulder, target), Math.abs(L1 - L2) + ε, L1 + L2 - ε);

Past full extension the arm simply goes straight and points — which is also what you do when the jar is on the top shelf. The bone lengths themselves are not new measurements: they are read straight out of the same capsule table that sculpted the bear in part one and rigged it in part two. One table, three articles of mileage.

Aside · Born on the assembly line

Inverse kinematics is a refugee from robotics. Factory arms had to place welders and grippers at world coordinates years before computer characters existed, and the robotics literature — Denavit and Hartenberg's joint parameters in 1955, Whitney's resolved motion rate control in 1969 — built the mathematics. Computer animation imported it in 1985, when Girard and Maciejewski used IK to plant the feet of walking figures, ending the era of legs that swam past the floor. The bear's ambitions are more modest than a six-axis welder's, and it is rewarded for that modesty with a closed-form answer.

Step 2 · The circle of elbowsA pole vector picks the plane

The triangle fixes the elbow's angle but not its position: the whole triangle is free to spin around the shoulder→target line, sweeping the elbow along a circle of equally correct solutions. Something has to break the tie. Riggers call that something the pole vector: a point in space the bend leans toward. Elbows want a pole behind and below; knees want one out front. It is less a mathematical necessity than a statement of taste — which is exactly why it is exposed as a control.

Below, the full solver with its hands visible. Drag the amber orb anywhere; the elbow swing slider walks the pole around the circle of solutions; spine assist leans the torso a fraction of the way toward the goal before the arm solves, the way you reach with your whole body when something is barely in range.

The layering order matters and is worth saying plainly: breathing, spine lean, and gaze are forward kinematics — they write joint rotations from sine waves and aim heuristics. The two-bone solver runs after them, reads where the shoulder actually ended up, and writes better numbers into the same three joints FK owns. IK never replaces the rig. It is just a smarter way of choosing the numbers the dials would have chosen — which means everything from part two (the weights, the skin matrices, the GPU vertex stage) works unchanged.

Deep dive: shortest-arc rotations and the free twist

The solver converts each desired bone direction into a joint rotation with a shortest-arc quaternion — the smallest rotation taking the bone's rest direction to the new one. A direction pins only two of a rotation's three degrees of freedom; the third, twist about the bone's own axis, is left wherever the shortest arc puts it. For a bear in fur this is free money: the twist is invisible, so the cheapest choice is the right one. On a human wrist it is not — skin shows twist — which is why production rigs thread dedicated twist bones along the forearm and distribute the rotation across them. Same triangle, more bookkeeping.

Step 3 · The gazeAll "look at" ever is

The bear's eyes never leave the firefly, and that behavior costs almost nothing: aiming a joint is one shortest-arc rotation — from the joint's rest direction to "toward the target" — clamped and blended so the neck does not behave like a turret: 

// clamp: never rotate further than maxDeg off the rest direction
const angle = 2 * Math.acos(Math.min(1, Math.abs(q.w)));
if (angle > max) q.slerp(IDENTITY, 1 - max / angle);   // shrink toward rest
joint.rotation.slerp(q, weight);                        // ease, don't snap

The craft is in the split: the neck gets a 26° cone and answers slowly; the head gets 44° and answers faster. Stacked, they produce the natural recruitment order — head first, neck following — and the cones stop the bear from owling its head backwards when the firefly passes behind it. Two clamped rotations, layered on top of breathing FK, and the statue starts to seem interested.

Step 4 · Longer chainsReach and re-root

The law of cosines is a two-bone trick — a triangle has three sides, no more. Tails, spines, tentacles, and anything with more joints than an arm need a different idea, and the prettiest one in the toolbox is FABRIK — Forward And Backward Reaching Inverse Kinematics. No matrices, no trigonometry; just two passes of "snap and re-impose the lengths". Backward: teleport the tip onto the target and walk toward the root, dragging each joint to its neighbor at bone-length distance. That satisfies the target but uproots the chain. Forward: teleport the root back home and walk to the tip, re-imposing lengths the other way. Each round trip leaves the tip closer; a handful of iterations and it has effectively converged.

Aside · A 2011 algorithm that feels older

FABRIK is young by graphics standards — Aristidou and Lasenby published it in 2011 — but it has the flavor of a folk algorithm: two loops, no linear algebra, embarrassingly easy to implement from the description alone. It spread through game engines almost immediately, the way quicksort spread through everything. Before it, long chains were mostly handled by CCD (cyclic coordinate descent, 1991): rotate one joint at a time to swing the tip toward the target, loop until tired. CCD converges too, but it works angle by angle and tends to curl chains unnaturally near the root; FABRIK's position-space passes distribute the motion more evenly — which is why the demo above looks like a creature and not like a crane.

CodaWanting, on demand

Take stock of the bear's vocabulary. Part one: a body. Part two: joints under the skin, and dials that bend it. This article: wants — put the paw there, look at that — compiled into the same dials by a triangle, a tiebreaker, and a clamp. What is still missing is composition: a whole-body shape held with intent, and a way to move from one such shape to the next so the journey looks like practice rather than interpolation. Next time the dials get a memory: poses as targets, springs as blends — and the bear finally begins its qi gong form.
