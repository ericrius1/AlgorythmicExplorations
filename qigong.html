The Form

The bear above is practicing right now — Lifting the Sky, Drawing the Bow, Swaying the Head and Wagging the Tail — and none of it is a recording. There are no animation clips in this series. There are tables of joint angles, and springs that chase them. This final article combines poses as targets, springs as blends, and user-controlled sequencing.

Scroll down to read ↓

The previous three articles produced a body lofted around a table of bones, a GPU-skinned skeleton, and target-based inverse kinematics. What is still missing is the thing audiences actually call animation: whole-body intent, sustained over seconds, one shape flowing into the next.

The industrial answer is the clip: record curves for every joint, play them back, and when you need to go from clip A to clip B, crossfade. Clips are wonderful right up until someone interactive touches them — interrupt a crossfade with a new request and you need a blend of blends, then a tree of those, and the tree grows arbitration logic of its own. This article instead stores target poses and uses dynamics to move between them.

Step 1 · The vocabularyPose targets

A pose here is nothing but a table: joint name → three Euler angles, plus an optional whole-body lift. The qi gong stances are all small edits of one base — wu ji, "empty standing": knees soft, spine tall, arms just off the body. Composing a stance is a spread operator: 

const liftSky = over(WUJI, {
  upperArmL: [-152, 0, -10], upperArmR: [-152, 0, 10],  // both arms overhead
  spine: [-3, 0, 0], chest: [-6, 0, 0],
  head: [-12, 0, 0],                                    // eyes follow the hands up
});

A move is a handful of poses with normalized timestamps — Lifting the Sky is six keyposes: stand, arms forward, arms overhead, the stretch (up on the toes, a 4-centimeter lift), sweep down through a T, stand again. Sampling a move at time t finds the two bracketing keyposes and eases between their tables with a smoothstep. That is the entire file format. No curves per joint, no exported takes — the whole nine-move form is a page of readable tables, and editing a stance is editing a number.

Aside · The bear was already in the curriculum

Qi gong and bears go back further than either does with computers. The Wu Qin Xi — Five Animal Frolics, attributed to the physician Hua Tuo around the second century — is one of the oldest documented exercise systems, and its five animals are the tiger, the deer, the monkey, the crane, and the bear: heavy, rooted, swaying from the waist. The routine this article's bear performs is adapted from the Ba Duan Jin, the Eight Pieces of Brocade, a Song-dynasty standing form whose move names — "Two Hands Hold Up the Heavens", "Wise Owl Gazes Backwards", "Sway the Head and Wag the Tail" — needed no adaptation at all to describe what a stylized bear should do in a browser.

Step 2 · Temporal blendingSprings as blends

The move player never rotates a joint. It only writes targets — and every joint chases its target with a critically damped spring: acceleration proportional to the remaining error, damping tuned to the exact value where the joint arrives as fast as possible without overshooting. The spring owns two numbers per channel — position and velocity. Preserving velocity is the important part. When a new move interrupts an old one, the spring does not restart; it carries its momentum toward the new target. This avoids a visible restart when a move is interrupted.

Watch the three philosophies race. The grey line is a hard cut — what switching clips does. The violet line is a fixed-duration crossfade — what blend trees do, and it looks fine until you interrupt it: click mid-fade and it visibly restarts from wherever it was, with a kink. The blue spring just keeps flowing:

Rotations need one extra courtesy. A joint's orientation is a quaternion, and "the remaining error" between two quaternions is not a subtraction — the animator measures it as a rotation vector (axis times angle, the log map), runs the spring in that space, and integrates the angular velocity back onto the quaternion each frame: 

err = log(target · current⁻¹)            // rotation vector: axis × angle
vel += k·err·dt − c·vel·dt               // k from the stiffness, c = 2√k (ζ = 1)
current = exp(vel·dt) · current          // physics that refuses to teleport

One control follows from the math: the spring frequency, in hertz — the bear's response speed. Around 1.3 Hz it moves like a large, calm animal. At 3 Hz it snaps quickly to each stance; at half a hertz it moves slowly through the same form. The same tables, same schedule — one number changes the character's apparent mass.

Aside · The games industry converged here too

Springs as the connective tissue of character motion is now mainstream practice. David Bollo's GDC talk on inertialization made the case that transitions should be physical decays rather than crossfades — Gears of War shipped on it — and Daniel Holden's writing catalogued the critically damped spring as a broadly useful tool for gameplay animation, including cameras, aim offsets, and IK targets. The animator here uses the same method.

Step 3 · The layersLayered procedural motion

Springs chasing keyposes already look like motion, but not yet like a body. Three layers, applied in order each frame, close the distance — and each is a tool this series already owns:

Breath. A slow sine — six breaths a minute, qi gong tempo — added onto the chest, spine, neck, and shoulder targets before the springs see them. It never stops, so the bear is never a statue, even standing "still". Some moves breathe deeper: Embracing the Tree is mostly breath.

Ground. The pose tables bend knees, but forward kinematics alone would leave the feet swinging through the floor like a marionette's. So after the springs run, part 3's two-bone solver re-plants each ankle exactly where it stood at rest — and there is one subtlety worth the italics: pinning the feet is what turns bent knees into a crouch. The animator measures how much the pose moved the ankles relative to the hips and sinks the hips by the same amount; the IK then keeps the feet planted. Horse stance comes out as an actual descent, not a levitation with folded legs.

Gaze. The clamped-cone aim from part 3, eased so glances feel intentional. Each move can name a point worth watching — the archer's distant hawk, the owl's over-the-shoulder look — and the neck and head recruit toward it at their own rates while everything else carries on.

The order — pose targets, breath, springs, feet, gaze — is a pipeline. Each stage modifies the output of the previous one without requiring direct coupling between stages.

Step 4 · InteractionInteractive controls

The demo combines nine moves from the Brocade. They can be selected in any order or interrupted mid-motion. There is no transition-specific code; the springs simply receive new targets. Turning feet: planted off exposes the foot-sliding problem, while turning gaze off shows the contribution of the aim layer.

The muscle-speed slider is worth a slow minute: at 0.6 Hz the same nine tables read as tai chi; at 3 Hz they read as calisthenics. Tempo scales the schedule without touching the physics, so a slow form still arrives because targets change late while the springs preserve motion.

CodaThe complete animation system

One table of twenty-one bones — two points and two radii each — was, in order: a body (the lofted tubes of part one), a paint job (color zones stamped per part), a skeleton (the deform rows became joints), skinning data (the distances to those same bones became weights), a set of bone lengths for the IK triangle of part three, and finally the address space the qi gong tables write their angles into. Every later system uses the same twenty-four rows defined in the first article.

The general result is that storing destinations is more composable than storing journeys. Clips are journeys; they fight when combined. Targets plus dynamics compose by construction — which is why the same method keeps surfacing as camera smoothing, cursor inertia, UI transitions, and interruptible procedural animation.

The bear at the top of the page continues to vary because its transitions are generated dynamically rather than stored as clips.
