The Form

The bear above is practicing right now — Lifting the Sky, Drawing the Bow, Swaying the Head and Wagging the Tail — and none of it is a recording. There is no animation clip anywhere in this series. There are tables of joint angles, and springs that chase them. This final article builds the whole performance: poses as targets, springs as blends, and a form you can conduct.

Scroll down to read ↓

Take stock of what three articles bought. A body lofted, ring by ring, around a table of bones; a skeleton with skin weights, bending on the GPU; and wants — put the paw there, watch the firefly — compiled into joint angles by a triangle and a clamp. What is still missing is the thing audiences actually call animation: whole-body intent, sustained over seconds, one shape flowing into the next.

The industrial answer is the clip: record curves for every joint, play them back, and when you need to go from clip A to clip B, crossfade. Clips are wonderful right up until someone interactive touches them — interrupt a crossfade with a new request and you need a blend of blends, then a tree of those, and the tree grows arbitration logic of its own. This article takes the other road, the one this site always takes, and it turns out to dissolve the blending problem entirely: never store motion at all. Store destinations. Let physics do the traveling.

Step 1 · The vocabularyPoses as places

A pose here is nothing but a table: joint name → three Euler angles, plus an optional whole-body lift. The qi gong stances are all small edits of one base — wu ji, "empty standing": knees soft, spine tall, arms just off the body. Composing a stance is a spread operator: 

const liftSky = over(WUJI, {
  upperArmL: [-152, 0, -10], upperArmR: [-152, 0, 10],  // both arms overhead
  spine: [-3, 0, 0], chest: [-6, 0, 0],
  head: [-12, 0, 0],                                    // eyes follow the hands up
});

A move is a handful of poses with normalized timestamps — Lifting the Sky is six keyposes: stand, arms forward, arms overhead, the stretch (up on the toes, a 4-centimeter lift), sweep down through a T, stand again. Sampling a move at time t finds the two bracketing keyposes and eases between their tables with a smoothstep. That is the entire file format. No curves per joint, no exported takes — the whole nine-move form is a page of readable tables, and editing a stance is editing a number.

Aside · The bear was already in the curriculum

Qi gong and bears go back further than either does with computers. The Wu Qin Xi — Five Animal Frolics, attributed to the physician Hua Tuo around the second century — is one of the oldest documented exercise systems, and its five animals are the tiger, the deer, the monkey, the crane, and the bear: heavy, rooted, swaying from the waist. The routine this article's bear performs is adapted from the Ba Duan Jin, the Eight Pieces of Brocade, a Song-dynasty standing form whose move names — "Two Hands Hold Up the Heavens", "Wise Owl Gazes Backwards", "Sway the Head and Wag the Tail" — needed no adaptation at all to describe what a stylized bear should do in a browser.

Step 2 · The time machineSprings as blends

Here is the idea the whole article stands on. The move player never rotates a joint. It only writes targets — and every joint chases its target with a critically damped spring: acceleration proportional to the remaining error, damping tuned to the exact value where the joint arrives as fast as possible without overshooting. The spring owns two numbers per channel — position and velocity — and the velocity is the magic. When a new move interrupts an old one, the spring does not restart; it carries its momentum into the new chase. The result reads as a performer changing their mind, not a video changing its frame.

Watch the three philosophies race. The grey line is a hard cut — what switching clips does. The violet line is a fixed-duration crossfade — what blend trees do, and it looks fine until you interrupt it: click mid-fade and it visibly restarts from wherever it was, with a kink. The blue spring just keeps flowing:

Rotations need one extra courtesy. A joint's orientation is a quaternion, and "the remaining error" between two quaternions is not a subtraction — the animator measures it as a rotation vector (axis times angle, the log map), runs the spring in that space, and integrates the angular velocity back onto the quaternion each frame: 

err = log(target · current⁻¹)            // rotation vector: axis × angle
vel += k·err·dt − c·vel·dt               // k from the stiffness, c = 2√k (ζ = 1)
current = exp(vel·dt) · current          // physics that refuses to teleport

One knob falls out of the math: the spring frequency, in honest hertz — the bear's muscle speed. Around 1.3 Hz it moves like a large, calm animal. Crank it to 3 Hz and it snaps to its stances like a drill sergeant; drop it to half a hertz and it underwater-dreams through the same form, every keypose arriving late and soft. Same tables, same schedule — one number changes the character's apparent mass.

Aside · The games industry converged here too

Springs as the connective tissue of character motion is now mainstream practice. David Bollo's GDC talk on inertialization made the case that transitions should be physical decays rather than crossfades — Gears of War shipped on it — and Daniel Holden's writing catalogued the critically damped spring as the single most useful object in a gameplay animator's toolbox (cameras, aim offsets, IK targets, everything). The thirty-line version in this article's animator is the same animal, just with the fur visible.

Step 3 · The layersBreath, ground, gaze

Springs chasing keyposes already look like motion, but not yet like a body. Three layers, applied in order each frame, close the distance — and each is a tool this series already owns:

Breath. A slow sine — six breaths a minute, qi gong tempo — added onto the chest, spine, neck, and shoulder targets before the springs see them. It never stops, so the bear is never a statue, even standing "still". Some moves breathe deeper: Embracing the Tree is mostly breath.

Ground. The pose tables bend knees, but forward kinematics alone would leave the feet swinging through the floor like a marionette's. So after the springs run, part 3's two-bone solver re-plants each ankle exactly where it stood at rest — and there is one subtlety worth the italics: pinning the feet is what turns bent knees into a crouch. The animator measures how much the pose moved the ankles relative to the hips and sinks the hips by the same amount; the IK then makes the legs honest. Horse stance comes out as an actual descent, not a levitation with folded legs.

Gaze. The clamped-cone aim from part 3, eased so glances feel intentional. Each move can name a point worth watching — the archer's distant hawk, the owl's over-the-shoulder look — and the neck and head recruit toward it at their own rates while everything else carries on.

The order — pose targets, breath, springs, feet, gaze — is a pipeline, and like the GPU pipelines earlier in this site's history, each stage only ever improves the numbers the previous stage wrote. No stage knows the others exist. That ignorance is the architecture.

Step 4 · The toyConduct the form

Everything assembled. Nine moves from the Brocade, bear edition — call them in any order, as rudely as you like. Interrupt the archer mid-draw with a bounce; demand the owl while the bear is still deep in horse stance. There is no transition code to confuse, because there are no transitions — only springs discovering they have somewhere new to be. Then turn feet: planted off and watch the marionette problem come back; turn the gaze off and watch the practice go vacant.

The muscle-speed slider is worth a slow minute: at 0.6 Hz the same nine tables read as tai chi; at 3 Hz they read as calisthenics. Tempo scales the schedule without touching the physics, so a slow form still arrives — late targets, honest travel.

CodaOne table, four articles

The series is over, so audit the spend. One table of twenty-one bones — two points and two radii each — was, in order: a body (the lofted tubes of part one), a paint job (color zones stamped per part), a skeleton (the deform rows became joints), a skinning (the distances to those same bones became weights), a set of bone lengths for the IK triangle of part three, and finally the address space the qi gong tables write their angles into. Every later system leaned on the same twenty-four rows the first article typed in.

And the moral that generalizes, the one worth taking to systems that have nothing to do with bears: storing destinations is more composable than storing journeys. Clips are journeys; they fight when combined. Targets plus dynamics compose by construction — which is why the same trick keeps surfacing as camera smoothing, cursor inertia, UI transitions, and a bear that can be interrupted mid-brocade without ever once looking embarrassed.

The bear at the top of this page is still practicing. It will not repeat itself exactly, ever — and nobody taught it any of those in-betweens.
