Five posts ago, a note was dissected into three numbers. Since then: intervals from colliding harmonic ladders, twelve notes from a number theory accident, scales as lopsided rings, and chords as stacked thirds. One question remains: is that machinery descriptive — a way of talking about music humans make — or is it generative, a recipe an algorithm can follow? Build the algorithm and listen.

Two parts are still missing from the engine: something to decide when (rhythm) and something to decide what, exactly (melody). Both can be represented with relatively compact algorithms.

Step 1 · WhenEuclid, drummer

Rhythm starts with a grid in time — a bar of sixteen steps, the temporal cousin of part three's twelve-note octave. A drum pattern is a subset of the grid, and the good ones obey a strange rule discovered by accident: take k hits and spread them over n steps as evenly as possible. When k divides n the result is a metronome. When it doesn't, the leftover steps must be distributed — and the distribution algorithm is literally Euclid's GCD construction, the oldest algorithm on record, running on remainders of remainders.

Set the kick to E(3,16) and you get the tresillo — the Cuban/New Orleans heartbeat, also the spine of a century of pop. E(5,16) is the bossa nova's cousin. Computer scientist Godfried Toussaint catalogued dozens of traditional rhythms worldwide and found an absurd fraction of them are exactly Euclidean: necessity (spread the strokes, keep the pulse) converging on the same arithmetic everywhere. Note what the sliders teach: a rhythm's character is barely about how many hits — it's about how the inevitable unevenness syncopates against the grid your ear infers. Like part four's scales: the lopsidedness is the information.

Aside · An algorithm for neutron timing

The connection was found in 2004, when Toussaint noticed that Bjorklund's algorithm — written for spallation neutron source hardware, to space accelerator timing pulses as evenly as possible — generated, rhythm after rhythm, the patterns ethnomusicologists had spent a century transcribing. Particle physics and Afro-Cuban percussion had independently arrived at the same code. His paper states the result in its title: "The Euclidean Algorithm Generates Traditional Musical Rhythms."

Step 2 · WhatA melody-biased random walk

Melody is harder to automate convincingly. Analyze large melody corpora and two simple statistics dominate: motion is overwhelmingly stepwise (small moves on the scale, with occasional leaps that then tend to fill back in), and notes on strong beats are overwhelmingly chord tones. Deep dive: dice music, 1787 — and what's actually new

Generative music is older than the United States. Musikalisches Würfelspiel — dice games assembling minuets from precomposed bars, one popular edition attributed to Mozart — sold briskly in the 1780s; Hiller and Isaacson's Illiac Suite (1957) moved the dice into a computer, and Xenakis, Eno, and every ambient generator since refined the idea. The important part is not the dice but the distribution: the weights, the gravity, and the grammar. What this page automates is sampling from choices made in advance. (Modern neural music models change the vocabulary — they learn the distribution from corpora instead of taking it from a theory series like this one — but not the framing: still a distribution, still sampled.)

Step 3 · AssemblyThe jukebox

Assemble the whole series. Pick a tonic and a mode (part four). Generate a four-chord progression with a functional grammar — start home, wander, set up a return (part five) — and voice it lazily. Bass on the roots. Euclidean kick, snare on the backbeat, hats by energy (step 1). A gravity-guided walker for the melody (step 2), through a delay because taste is also a parameter. Tune everything in twelve equal steps (part three) so any tonic works; let every simultaneous interval be the harmonic relationships from part two; synthesize every voice as a recipe of partials, per part one. One seed number decides it all.

Press new song a few times. Each seed produces a new arrangement; the nebula is the spectrum of the live audio, with low harmonics at the core, cymbals at the rim, the kick drum arriving as a pulse. Cycle the modes and hear part four's brightness ordering applied to the whole band.

And press new song once more, attentively. Some seeds are better than others. You can hear that — instantly, reliably, without being able to say why. The machinery on this page can keep generating plausible music; it does not evaluate the results. That judgement remains with the listener.

EpilogueThe complete system

a note     = a repetition rate, a size, and a recipe of harmonics     (1)
an interval = two recipes interleaving; consonance = aligned partials  (2)
the grid    = 12 equal steps, because (3/2)¹² ≈ 2⁷, almost             (3)
a scale     = 7 of 12, lopsided on purpose, so the ear knows home      (4)
a chord     = 3 of 7; geometry of thirds = feeling; V7 = bowstring     (5)
a song      = chords in time + Euclid's drums + a walker with gravity  (6)
