Five posts ago, a note was dissected into three numbers. Since then: intervals from colliding harmonic ladders, twelve notes from a number theory accident, scales as lopsided rings, chords as geometry with feelings. One question remains, and it's the one this series has been quietly building toward: is that machinery descriptive — a way of talking about music humans make — or is it generative, a recipe an algorithm can follow? There's an honest way to find out. Build the algorithm and listen.

Two parts are still missing from the engine: something to decide when (rhythm) and something to decide what, exactly (melody). Both turn out to be the most algorithm-shaped objects in all of music.

Step 1 · WhenEuclid, drummer

Rhythm starts with a grid in time — a bar of sixteen steps, the temporal cousin of part three's twelve-note octave. A drum pattern is a subset of the grid, and the good ones obey a strange rule discovered by accident: take k hits and spread them over n steps as evenly as possible. When k divides n the result is a metronome. When it doesn't, the leftover steps must be distributed — and the distribution algorithm is literally Euclid's GCD construction, the oldest algorithm on record, running on remainders of remainders.

Set the kick to E(3,16) and you get the tresillo — the Cuban/New Orleans heartbeat, also the spine of a century of pop. E(5,16) is the bossa nova's cousin. Computer scientist Godfried Toussaint catalogued dozens of traditional rhythms worldwide and found an absurd fraction of them are exactly Euclidean: necessity (spread the strokes, keep the pulse) converging on the same arithmetic everywhere. Note what the sliders teach: a rhythm's character is barely about how many hits — it's about how the inevitable unevenness syncopates against the grid your ear infers. Like part four's scales: the lopsidedness is the information.

Aside · An algorithm for neutron timing

The connection was found in 2004, when Toussaint noticed that Bjorklund's algorithm — written for spallation neutron source hardware, to space accelerator timing pulses as evenly as possible — generated, rhythm after rhythm, the patterns ethnomusicologists had spent a century transcribing. Particle physics and Afro-Cuban percussion had independently arrived at the same code. (His paper's title is the best abstract ever written: "The Euclidean Algorithm Generates Traditional Musical Rhythms.")

Step 2 · WhatThe drunken singer with a homing instinct

Melody looks like the unautomatable part — surely tunes are where the human lives? Mostly, no. Analyze big melody corpora and two boring statistics dominate: motion is overwhelmingly stepwise (small moves on the scale, with occasional leaps that then tend to fill back in), and notes on strong beats are overwhelmingly chord tones. The two sliders are the entire aesthetic. Gravity at maximum: every strong beat snaps to the chord and you've built an arpeggiator — pleasant, obedient, dead. Gravity at zero: the walk ignores the harmony and goes free-jazz; interesting for about eight seconds. Smoothness low: leaps everywhere, bebop dice. The musical zone is the middle — enough gravity that the line means something against the chords, enough freedom that it surprises. Randomness isn't the opposite of music; unweighted randomness is.

Deep dive: dice music, 1787 — and what's actually new

Generative music is older than the United States. Musikalisches Würfelspiel — dice games assembling minuets from precomposed bars, one popular edition attributed to Mozart — sold briskly in the 1780s; Hiller and Isaacson's Illiac Suite (1957) moved the dice into a computer, and Xenakis, Eno, and every ambient generator since refined the idea. The honest framing: the dice were never the composer. The distribution is the composer — the weights, the gravity, the grammar. What this page automates is the performance of taste, not its construction. (Modern neural music models change the vocabulary — they learn the distribution from corpora instead of taking it from a theory series like this one — but not the framing: still a distribution, still sampled.)

Step 3 · Everything, everywhere, all at onceThe jukebox

Assemble the whole series. Pick a tonic and a mode (part four). Generate a four-chord progression with a functional grammar — start home, wander, set up a return (part five) — and voice it lazily. Bass on the roots. Euclidean kick, snare on the backbeat, hats by energy (step 1). A gravity-guided walker for the melody (step 2), through a delay because taste is also a parameter. Tune everything in twelve equal steps (part three) so any tonic works; let every simultaneous interval be the handshake physics of part two; synthesize every voice as a recipe of partials, per part one. One seed number decides it all.

Press new song a few times. Each seed is a song nobody has heard before and nobody will hear again — not a recording, a performance; the nebula you're watching is the actual spectrum of the actual audio, low harmonics at the core, cymbals at the rim, the kick drum arriving as a shockwave. Cycle the modes and hear part four's brightness ladder operate on a whole band at once: mixolydian grins, dorian hopes, aeolian rains.

And press new song once more, attentively. Some seeds are better than others. You can hear that — instantly, reliably, without being able to say why. The machinery on this page generates competent music forever; what it cannot do is notice which of its songs matters. That noticing — taste, the weighting of the dice — is the part of music theory nobody has written down, because it lives in the listener. The series hands you the engine; you remain the judge.

Where to climb next, if you want to keep going: voice leading in four parts and real counterpoint (the lazy-voice search, made contrapuntal); extended and borrowed harmony (ninths, tritone substitutions — the handshake math still explains them); microtonality and adaptive tuning (un-round part three's rounding in real time); or Sethares' road — redesign timbre itself so that brand-new scales become consonant, and build music theory for instruments that have never existed. The grid was always a choice. Now you know whose.
