The Grass

Three hundred thousand blades, and not one of them is being managed. The CPU planted the meadow once and walked away; every gust you see is the GPU bending seven vertices at a time, a hundred million times a second. The mountains were geology — this is the first thing that looks alive.

Scroll down to read ↓

The series so far: part one raised mountains out of noise, part two rained on them until they had drainage and soil. Both articles end with the same kind of object — a landscape you look at. This one starts making a world you could be in, and the difference is mostly grass. Bare terrain reads as a model; ground cover reads as a place. The problem is that "ground cover" means a number with five zeros, and that number forces every decision in this article.

A hundred thousand of anything changes what you're allowed to do. One mesh per blade and the renderer chokes on draw calls. One physics body per blade and the CPU melts. The discipline that makes meadows possible is the oldest one in real-time graphics: decide once, per blade, at planting time; per frame, touch nothing. The CPU's job ends when the seeds are sown. After that, the entire meadow — growth, color, wind, everything — has to live in places that cost nothing to revisit: baked vertex data and a few lines of shader.

Step 1 · The bladeSeven vertices, honestly spent

A believable blade of grass is a tapered ribbon with a curve. The curve matters more than the count: grass is a cantilever, anchored at the root and free at the tip, so it bends like one — barely at the base, increasingly toward the top. The cheapest function with that shape is the parabola, so each vertex at fraction t up the blade leans sideways by an amount proportional to t²: 

offset(t) = bend · t²        // t = 0 root (pinned), t = 1 tip (free)
y(t) ≈ t − bend² · t² · k    // arcs shorten: the tip drops as it swings

The dots are the vertices a real blade gets — three segments, seven vertices, five triangles. The ghost line is the ideal curve they're approximating. Slide the segment count up and the polyline hugs the parabola; slide it down to one and the blade becomes a stiff cardboard triangle. Three is the budget sweet spot: the silhouette stays curved where eyes check (the tip) and nobody audits the base. The second line of the recipe is the detail most cheap grass skips — a bending blade must get shorter in y, or it appears to stretch like rubber. Believability lives in exactly these two terms.

Step 2 · PlantingThe ledger pays out

Where does grass go? Part two left us the answer in writing. The erosion simulation kept a ledger — at every cell, how much material the rain removed or deposited — and that ledger is a soil map. Deposition zones are exactly the flat, wet, silt-rich valley floors where real meadows happen. So the planter walks candidate points (hashed positions, same determinism as everything else in this series) and lets the ground vote: silt raises the odds of a blade taking root, rain-stripped rock lowers them, steep slopes and high altitude refuse outright. No one paints a grass map; the storm already painted it.

Each accepted blade then rolls its character from the same hash: height, width, lean, hue (greener in the damp, straw-colored toward the tree line), and two numbers the wind will use later — a phase, so the meadow doesn't move in lockstep, and a flexibility, because tall young grass whips while short tufts shiver. All of it is baked into vertex attributes, one decision at a time, never to be revisited.

The planted meadow. Watch where it isn't: the gullies' steep walls stay bare, ridgelines stay bare, but the drainage floors — where part two's droplets dropped their loads — grow thick. Push the candidate count up and the fps readout barely moves, because every blade added costs the CPU nothing after birth; the whole meadow is one draw call. That indifference to count is the entire economic argument of this article.

Aside · One draw call, no instances

The classic tool here is GPU instancing — upload one blade, stamp it N times. This meadow uses something even simpler: all the blades are merged into a single static buffer at planting time, pre-transformed, pre-colored. Instancing earns its keep when instances move independently on the CPU or share serious geometry; a seven-vertex blade has nothing to share, and none of them ever moves on the CPU. Merged buffers also let every blade be subtly unique — per-vertex color, per-blade wind data — without an attribute scheme. The lesson generalizes: instancing is a tool, not a reflex.

Step 3 · WindA storm in the vertex shader

Now the part that makes it alive. Simulating wind honestly — pressure fields, turbulence, blade-on-blade collision — is absurd for scenery. What eyes actually verify is much weaker: coherence. Gusts should be patches, not pixels; they should travel; nearby blades should agree and distant ones shouldn't. The cheapest field with those properties is two traveling sine waves at different scales and speeds: 

gust(x, z, t) = 0.7 · sin(k·(x + 0.7z) + 1.7t + φ) + 0.3 · sin(2.9k·(x − 0.7z) + 3.4t + 1.7φ)
sway = (gust · 0.5 + 0.62) · strength · t² · flex   // never negative: lean with the wind, oscillate around it

Evaluated per vertex, every frame, on the GPU — which is the punch line of the whole article. The same t² that shaped the static curve now scales the wind, so blades bend from the root exactly as before; the per-blade phase φ breaks the lockstep; the offset constant keeps sway non-negative, because real grass doesn't swing like a metronome — it leans downwind and flutters around the lean. Two sines are nowhere near a real gust field, but layered at 1:3 scale they produce patches that travel and overlap, and coherence was the only thing being checked.

Deep dive: why the meadow lights like velvet

A real blade's normal points sideways, perpendicular to the ribbon. Light a hundred thousand ribbons honestly and the meadow turns into glitter — every blade a razor flashing as it moves. The standard cheat: lie about the normals. Every vertex here claims its normal points nearly straight up, with a slight sideways tilt for variety, so the lighting model sees not a ribbon but a fuzzy green carpet that happens to have blade silhouettes. The meadow shades like a single soft surface — which, at twenty meters, is what a meadow is. The same trick (normals describing the aggregate, not the primitive) lights fur, hair cards, and particle foliage everywhere in games.

Second subtlety: the baked colors darken toward the root (ambient occlusion for free — deep grass is dark grass), and the displaced vertices keep their original normals even while the wind moves them. Stale normals on moving grass turn out to be invisible; the eye reads the motion from the silhouette, not the shading. Knowing which errors are invisible is most of real-time rendering.

CodaTaller things

The meadow proves out the pipeline that the rest of the series lives on: decide at build time, bake into vertices, animate in the shader, draw once. Hold that sentence, because the next article spends it on something with a skeleton. Trees — grown by an algorithm that's closer to biology than geometry, space colonization, where branches compete for light and the winners thicken. And trees carry a responsibility grass doesn't: next door in Feather & Bone, a wren is learning to fly, and when she comes in to land she will need branches with positions, thicknesses, and headroom — real perches, not scenery. This series grows them for her.
