Eleven articles and two series combine in one demo: an eagle built from tables and a world built from noise. Above, the autopilot flies over the mountains, choosing trees to land on and scream from. Below, the controls become yours.

This article combines the work of both series. Feather & Bone built an eagle from two tables — lofted rings for the body, a roster of feathers for the wings — then added motion: a skeleton weighted by distance, a wing unfolded by inverse kinematics, flight as a balance of forces, a landing flown by guidance laws. Ground Truth, in parallel, grew a world: mountains from noise, valleys carved by simulated rain, grass, and trees raised branch by branch. The two projects now share one demo.

Nothing in this article is new machinery. It is assembly. The bird's flight model meets the terrain's height function (now her ground); her landing controller meets the trees' perch lists (now her targets); her syrinx fills the air. The only genuinely new line of code is the one that reads your keyboard. Everything else is eleven articles of components connected through shared data.

Aside · Why structured representations matter

A downloaded bird and a downloaded landscape would not compose like this. They would be frozen meshes with no shared structure. Because the eagle is a flight model and the terrain is a height function and the trees publish their branches as data, the systems can exchange height and perch data directly.

Step 1 · The worldTerrain and perches

First, the stage. The terrain is the sister series' height function, stood up as a mesh — domain-warped ridged noise, colored by altitude and slope — and sized for an eagle this time: the patch is nearly three times wider and the relief three times taller than the songbird drafts, because at thirteen meters per second a small world goes by in seconds. Onto its gentler, lower ground we scatter big trees, each one grown by space colonization with its own seed, so no two are alike; the steep peaks and snowfields stay bare, the way real treelines work. Every tree also returns its perches — the boughs a bird could actually stand on, found by the same code that drew them.

Orbit the world. The faint gold motes are the perches: every place the eagle is allowed to land, computed from the trees' own geometry, not placed by hand. This is the list read by the landing controller.

Step 2 · You flyManual control

Here is the one new wire. The keyboard sets the same three commands the autopilot did in part four — bank, pitch, flap effort — and the identical force model flies them. You are not moving the bird directly; you set bank and climb commands, and the aerodynamic model determines the result. Steer too slowly and she sinks; dive and she gains the speed back; bank and she carves. She responds according to the same physics used by the autopilot.

Hover the demo and fly: A/D to bank, W/S to climb and dive, Space to pour on the flap. The ground is the terrain's real height under her — fly low and she'll skim it; fly into a hill and the physics pushes back. The same height function that colored the mountains is now the thing she can't fall through.

Deep dive: the controller and the pilot are the same socket

Notice that "you fly" and "AI flies" plug into the exact same hole. The flight model doesn't know or care whether its bank command came from a proportional controller chasing a perch or from your finger on the D key — it's the same three numbers either way. That's why the autopilot from part five and your keyboard are interchangeable: an autonomous agent and a human pilot are, to a physics simulation, both just sources of intent. Swap one for the other mid-flight and the bird never notices. The whole series was quietly building toward that socket.

Step 3 · The sandboxAutopilot

Leave it on autopilot and it repeats the complete behavior loop: wander the valley, pick a tree, fly the approach, flare onto a bough, scream a phrase from her two-voiced syrinx, launch, and go again. Take the controls and the same bird is yours to fly through the same world, past the same trees, with the option to hand her back whenever you like.

The combined demo connects the procedural bird, terrain, and trees to either an autonomous controller or manual input.
