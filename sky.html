The Sky

Eleven articles, two series, one demo. Everything we built — a bird from a function, a world from noise — is loose at last in the same space. Above, the autopilot is flying her over the mountains, choosing trees to land on and sing from. Below, the controls become yours.

This is the article both series were walking toward from their first sentence. Feather & Bone built a wren out of a table of capsules and then spent four articles teaching her to move: a skeleton weighted by distance, a wing unfolded by inverse kinematics, flight as a balance of forces, a landing flown by guidance laws. Ground Truth, in parallel, grew a world: mountains from noise, valleys carved by simulated rain, grass, and trees raised branch by branch. Two projects, written side by side, each ending here — because the end was always the same demo.

Nothing in this article is new machinery. It is assembly. The bird's flight model meets the terrain's height function (now her ground); her landing controller meets the trees' perch lists (now her targets); her syrinx fills the air. The only genuinely new line of code is the one that reads your keyboard. Everything else is eleven articles of parts, finally plugged into each other.

Aside · Why "from a function" was the whole point

A downloaded bird and a downloaded landscape would not compose like this. They'd be frozen meshes with no idea of each other. But because the wren is a flight model and the terrain is a height function and the trees publish their branches as data, the pieces interlock without a seam: she asks the ground how high it is, asks the trees where she can land, and the physics does the rest. Building everything from arithmetic was never about showing off — it was so that, on the last day, the parts would fit.

Step 1 · The worldMountains, with somewhere to land

First, the stage. The terrain is the sister series' height function, stood up as a mesh — domain-warped ridged noise, colored by altitude and slope. Onto its gentler, lower ground we scatter trees, each one grown by space colonization with its own seed, so no two are alike; the steep peaks and snowfields stay bare, the way real treelines work. And every tree hands back its perches — the branches a bird could actually stand on, found by the same code that drew them.

Orbit the world. The faint gold motes are the perches: every place the wren is allowed to land, computed from the trees' own geometry, not placed by hand. This is the menu her landing controller reads. A world that didn't just get drawn, but knows where a bird can sit in it.

Step 2 · You flyYour keyboard, her physics

Here is the one new wire. The keyboard sets the same three commands the autopilot did in part four — bank, pitch, flap effort — and the identical force model flies them. You are not moving a puppet; you're asking for a bank and a climb, and the aerodynamics decides what actually happens. Steer too slow and she sinks; dive and she gains the speed back; bank and she carves. She stays a bird because the physics, not your keystroke, has the final say.

Hover the demo and fly: A/D to bank, W/S to climb and dive, Space to pour on the flap. The ground is the terrain's real height under her — fly low and she'll skim it; fly into a hill and the physics pushes back. The same height function that colored the mountains is now the thing she can't fall through.

Deep dive: the controller and the pilot are the same socket

Notice that "you fly" and "AI flies" plug into the exact same hole. The flight model doesn't know or care whether its bank command came from a proportional controller chasing a perch or from your finger on the D key — it's the same three numbers either way. That's why the autopilot from part five and your keyboard are interchangeable: an autonomous agent and a human pilot are, to a physics simulation, both just sources of intent. Swap one for the other mid-flight and the bird never notices. The whole series was quietly building toward that socket.

Step 3 · The sandboxHand it to the bird, or take it back

And the finale: the whole thing, on a toggle. Leave it on autopilot and she lives her loop — wander the valley, pick a tree, fly the approach, flare onto a branch, sing a phrase from her two-voiced syrinx, launch, and go again. Take the controls and the same bird is yours to fly through the same world, past the same trees, with the option to hand her back whenever you like.

That's the demo this whole site has been a long approach toward: a creature that exists because a table of numbers says so, flying a world that exists because a noise function says so, landing on trees that exist because a growth rule says so — and answering equally to a controller or to you. Every article was a part. This is the assembled machine.

CodaA wren, a world, and you

That phrase has ended every article in both series, a promise made eleven times. Here it is, kept. The wren was modeled from capsules, rigged by proximity, flapped by the law of cosines, flown by lift and drag, and landed by feedback. The world was hashed into noise, summed into mountains, carved by rain, planted with grass, and grown into trees. Neither knew about the other until now, and yet they fit on the first try — because both were built the same way, out of functions, on purpose, for this.

The sister series closes on its own horizon in Ground Truth's finale; this one closes in the air above it. Thank you for reading all the way to the part where the bird flies. Now go fly her.
