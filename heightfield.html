The Heightfield

A new series grows a world from arithmetic. Nobody drew the mountains under this camera — they are a sum of waves read off a grid of hashed gradients, and by the end of the series there will be no edge to them, grass on their flanks, and trees for a certain bird to land in.

This series builds a landscape the way the rest of the site builds everything: from functions, live, on your machine. The plan runs from this article's mountains through erosion (rain as an algorithm), a hundred thousand blades of wind-bent grass, trees grown branch by branch, and finally a world that streams in around you with no border fence — terrain you could fly over for hours and never see repeat.

And something will be flying over it. A sister series, Feather & Bone, starts in parallel with this one: a wren built from a distance field, on her way to being rigged, flapped, and handed to a pilot. The two series converge on one final demo — her sky is this ground. When this series plants trees, it is planting her runways.

But every world starts with one question: where is the ground? The answer this article builds is a heightfield — a function h(x, z) that returns an elevation for any point you ask about, with no stored map behind it. The mountains exist only at the moment of asking, which is exactly the property that will make them infinite later.

Step 1 · Structured randomnessNoise you can lean on

Pure randomness makes terrible terrain. White noise assigns every point an independent height — the result is static, not landscape, because real ground is correlated: the elevation here is a strong hint about the elevation one step away. What terrain needs is randomness with a wavelength.

The classic recipe is Ken Perlin's. Put randomness only at the integer lattice points — not random heights but random slopes (gradients). Between lattice points, the function is forced to pass through zero at every corner with exactly the corner's slope, blended by a curve chosen so that nothing — not the value, not the curvature — betrays where the grid lines are: 

fade(t) = 6t⁵ − 15t⁴ + 10t³       // zero 1st *and* 2nd derivative at the lattice
corner(c, p) = grad(hash(c)) · (p − c)   // each corner's opinion: its slope, extrapolated
noise(p) = blend of the four corners' opinions by fade

The hash is the load-bearing trick: a few rounds of integer multiply-and-xor that turn a lattice point into an apparently random gradient — deterministically. The mountain at coordinates (1402, 88) is random in every statistical sense, and it is also the same mountain every time anyone looks. No table of stored heights, no seed file: the entire landscape is the hash function, evaluated on demand. That determinism is the foundation the final article will stand on when the world becomes infinite — you can't stream terrain in and out of memory unless the terrain is guaranteed to come back identical.

Step 2 · The fractal sumOctaves

One octave of gradient noise is a smooth, rolling swell — pleasant, and dishonest. Real terrain has detail at every scale: ridgelines kilometers long, gullies meters wide, both at once. Mandelbrot's observation — coastlines and mountain profiles look statistically the same whether you view them from orbit or from a hiking trail — gives the recipe: add the noise to itself at higher frequency and lower amplitude, again and again. The result is fractional Brownian motion, and each layer is an octave.

The faint lines are the octaves alone; the cyan line is their sum. Two dials control the character of the world. Lacunarity is the frequency step between octaves (usually 2 — hence "octave"). Persistence is the amplitude step, and it is the roughness dial of the universe: at 0.3 the high frequencies barely whisper and you get the Scottish Highlands, worn smooth; at 0.7 they shout and you get the Dolomites. One number, geology.

Aside · The Oscar for arithmetic

Perlin invented his noise working on Tron (1982), out of frustration with how relentlessly clean computer imagery looked — he wanted a "salt" that could roughen any surface procedurally. The function escaped into every renderer, every game engine, and every texture ever described as "procedural," and in 1997 the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences gave him a Technical Achievement Award for it — an Oscar for a math function, which remains a very short list.

Step 3 · Ridges and warpsTwo dimensions, bent

In two dimensions the same sum makes a map — but plain fBm has a tell: it is too even-tempered. Statistically symmetric noise makes hills and valleys with the same rounded character, and real mountains are not symmetric — crests are sharp, valleys are broad. Two standard distortions fix the temperament:

Ridging folds each octave at zero: take 1 − 2|n|, and the smooth crossings through zero become creases pointing up — instant ridgelines, the Musgrave trick. Domain warping attacks the other tell, the directionlessness: instead of sampling the noise at your coordinates, sample it at coordinates that have themselves been pushed around by another, lower-frequency noise — h = fbm(p + warp·fbm(p)). The grid of features shears into flows and sweeps, the way rock strata bend, and nothing lines up with any axis anymore.

White is high. Slide ridge up and watch valleys broaden while crests tighten into bright threads; slide warp and watch the whole map shear sideways into something with apparent tectonics. Between the two, the noise stops looking like noise — which is the entire job.

Deep dive: keeping the octaves from conspiring

Stacking octaves on the same lattice has a subtle failure: every octave's grid lines coincide at the origin and along axes, and at high octave counts their agreement becomes faintly visible as axis-aligned structure. The fix costs two multiplies: rotate the coordinate frame by a fixed angle between octaves, so no two octaves share a lattice orientation. The same trick — decorrelate by rotation — shows up in dithering, in shadow sampling, and anywhere else repeated patterns need to forget they're related.

Step 4 · Standing it upFrom map to mountain

The third dimension is the cheapest step in the article: take a flat grid of vertices, set every y to h(x, z), done. The interesting work is everything that makes the lifted grid read as terrain. First, shaping: raw fBm is symmetric around zero, so the height function pushes it through a power curve — valleys spread out and flatten, peaks stay proud, because that's what gravity does to real landscapes (a preview of the erosion article, which will do it honestly).

Second, normals. We already paid for the height grid, so each vertex takes its slope from its neighbors by central differences — the same move the ferrofluid used on its density field: 

normal(i, j) = normalize( (H[i−1,j] − H[i+1,j]) / 2Δ,  1,  (H[i,j−1] − H[i,j+1]) / 2Δ )

Third, paint — and here altitude and slope vote on every vertex. Grass holds the low ground. Steep faces shed their soil and show rock, whatever their elevation. Snow takes the high ground but refuses the steeps (it slides off), which is why snowlines in the demo trace the shape of the crests instead of being a flat altitude cut — one comparison per vertex doing the work of a climate model.

All four dials from the flat map, now with consequences and lighting. Toggle the wireframe to see how humble the geometry under the mountains really is — a bent bedsheet, about eighty thousand triangles. The drama is entirely in the height function and the normals. Reroll the seed for a new range; the dials keep their settings because the character of a world and the identity of a world are different inputs, which is exactly how the infinite version will let you explore without ever regenerating the same hill twice.

CodaWhere this series goes

This article's mountains have a secret: they've never seen rain. Real terrain is noise plus history — every gully is a record of water finding a way down. The next article simulates that history: hydraulic erosion, thousands of droplets carving the fBm into drainage networks, depositing sediment into the flats. After that the world gets a skin: instanced grass bending in procedural wind, then trees — grown by space colonization, branch by branch, which matters beyond scenery because branches are destinations: the wren next door will need their positions, thicknesses, and headroom when she learns to land. Then placement and biomes (what grows where, decided by the same hash), and finally the horizon problem: chunks, levels of detail, and a world that generates itself in a ring around wherever you happen to be flying.

Every one of those articles leans on what this one established: a deterministic function answering "where is the ground?" for any coordinate, no map required. The rest of the series is just asking better follow-up questions.
