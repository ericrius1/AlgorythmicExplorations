The Wingbeat

The folded wing now opens. One triangle identity does the unfolding, one warped clock does the beating, and the wren above is doing both slowly enough to inspect. Drag to orbit her.

Part two ended with a bird that could be posed: seventeen joints, each waiting for an angle. That's enough for head-turns and tail-flicks, where one joint does one job. The wing is different. Three segments per side — humerus, forearm, hand — and any pose you actually want is a coordination of all three. Dial the shoulder and you've moved the elbow and wrist somewhere wrong; fix the elbow and the wingtip drifts. Posing a chain joint-by-joint is solving a puzzle backwards.

So this article stops dialing angles and starts asking for places. Inverse kinematics turns the question around: instead of "given these rotations, where does the wingtip end up?" we say "the wingtip should be there — derive the rotations." For a limb of two main segments the answer isn't even iterative; it follows directly from the law of cosines. With it, the entire wingbeat — the unfold, the stroke, the recovery — becomes one moving target evaluated by the solver each frame.

Aside · The arm in the wing

A bird's wing is the same arm you have. Humerus to the elbow, radius-and-ulna to the wrist, and then the hand — fused, stretched, and flying the biggest feathers. When the wing folds, all that arm collapses into the neat teardrop silhouette part one sculpted; the fold lives in the angles, hidden under plumage. Unfolding is just those angles relaxing toward straight — which is exactly what an IK solver does as its target moves away from the shoulder.

Step 1 · Two-bone IKClosed-form wing placement

Fix the shoulder S, pick a target T, and call the two segment lengths a and b. If the wing is to touch the target, the elbow must sit at distance a from S and distance b from T — and now look at what's on the table: a triangle with all three side lengths known (a, b, and d = |T − S|). Triangles with three known sides have no secrets. The law of cosines gives the angle at the shoulder directly: 

cos A = (a² + d² − b²) / 2ad      // angle between the target line and the humerus

Swing the humerus off the shoulder→target line by A, place the elbow, aim the forearm at the target: solved, in closed form, every frame, for the price of one square root. The only thing the triangle does not decide is which way the elbow sticks out — the solution is a whole circle of elbows around the shoulder→target axis. That choice is the pole: a hint vector saying "bend this way". Birds' elbows point back along the body, so our pole does too.

The third segment — the hand — rides on top of the two-bone core: aim it at the true wingtip target, pull the two-bone target back by one hand-length, solve, re-aim from wherever the wrist actually landed, and solve once more. Two passes and the tip is pinned. Drag the orb anywhere: inside her reach the wing folds and unfolds to track it; past her reach the chain goes straight and points, which is the dignified thing to do. The elbow lift slider is the pole made visible — same wingtip, a whole family of wings.

Step 2 · The unfoldFrom silhouette to span

With the solver in place, the wing's biggest transformation needs embarrassingly little code. The folded tip position is in the table (it's the hand capsule's far end); the spread tip is one wing-reach out from the shoulder, level and slightly forward. Slide the target from one to the other along an arc and the solver fills in everything between — shoulder opening, elbow passing through every angle of the fold, blade rolling from flat-against-the-body to flat-against-the-sky.

One slider, and the readout shows that the span more than doubles, from a teardrop to a glider, using the same three segments — nothing is created, only redistributed. Toggle the x-ray and watch the elbow: mid-unfold it kinks backward into a genuine Z before straightening into span. And watch the shoulder seam, too — the mesh smears a little where body weights and wing weights blend. Our weights were computed against the folded pose; production rigs bind with wings spread for exactly this reason. We keep the smear: it is small and not visible at part six's camera distances it's invisible.

Deep dive: a bone is an arrow, a wing is a blade

"Aim the forearm at the target" underdetermines the forearm: it can aim there with its feathers facing up, down, or anywhere around its own axis, and rotation-from-direction-alone leaves that twist to chance. So every segment is posed from a frame, not a direction: build an orthonormal basis from (direction, blade-normal hint) for the rest pose, the same for the desired pose, and take the rotation between the two matrices. The hint is cheap — folded wings face sideways, spread wings face up, blend by how far out the tip is — but it removes the ambiguity exactly, and it gives us a free knob: rotate the hint about the bone axis and the blade twists. Remember that knob; the wingbeat is about to lean on it.

Step 3 · The cycleAn asymmetric clock

A wingbeat is a loop, so the obvious move is a phase p running 0→1 and a tip target riding a tilted ellipse — down-and-forward, then up-and-back. The obvious move is also wrong in a telling way: a real wingbeat is not a sine. Only the downstroke pushes air where air needs pushing; the upstroke is overhead, a recovery. So the bird spends more time on the half that pays — our clock is warped, giving the downstroke its share of the period first and hurrying the upstroke home. And during that hurried half the wrist folds: span pulls in by a third, because hauling a full wing back up through the air would refund most of the downstroke. Less span up than down — watch slow-motion footage of any songbird and it's the first thing you'll see.

The orange trail is the wingtip's own diagram — the tilted loop, fatter on the downstroke side, pinched where the wrist folds. The last slider is the twist knob from the deep dive doing its real job: on the downstroke the blade pitches leading-edge-down so the push has a forward component, on the upstroke it feathers flat to slip through. Right now that's choreography — the air in this article is purely decorative. The next article makes the air push back, and this twist becomes thrust with a number on it.

Step 4 · Full-body motionCoupling the wingbeat to the body

Wings don't beat in isolation; the whole bird is the mechanism. Three cheap couplings, all driven by the same phase. The body rides the reaction — pushed up through the downstroke, sagging in recovery, a two-line heave. The head counter-pitches against the body's bobbing, because a bird's head is a gimbal that holds its gaze level while everything behind it heaves (the part-two saccade system will plug straight into this). And the tail fans: one capsule, scaled sideways along its bone, and the skinned vertices spread into a fan for free — the same trick as every deformation so far, just with scale instead of rotation in the matrix.

Turn the beat rate up and she's a wren in a hurry; down to one beat per second and she's a crow. The shapes of flight are all here now — what's missing is consequence. She flaps and nothing happens, because nothing yet says it should.

CodaFrom motion to force

Next time, physics. The wing is an airfoil and the wingbeat is a force generator: lift that scales with speed and angle of attack, drag taxing everything, the downstroke's twisted blade converting beats into thrust. Give the wren a velocity and let those forces integrate, and flight stops being an animation and becomes an outcome — including banking turns, which fall out of rolling the lift vector sideways rather than being scripted. The flapping you just watched will stop being a looping gif and start being the engine.

And she'll need somewhere to go: Ground Truth has been busy — rain has carved its mountains, grass is growing, and trees are publishing their branches like landing pads with coordinates. Two more articles each. Both series feed into the final flight demo.
