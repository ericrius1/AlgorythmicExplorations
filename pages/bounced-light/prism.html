The Prism

Part six: a glass prism, a glass sphere, one small lamp — and no color anywhere in the scene description. Every hue you see was produced by dispersion as white light passes through the geometry.

Scroll down to read ↓

Every scene in this series has been opaque. Light bounced; it never entered. Rainbows, the bright patterns at the bottom of a swimming pool, the way a lens gathers the sun to a point — all live inside transparent things. The finale adds exactly one physical event to the path tracer, refraction, and then models a quantity simplified since part one: the wavelength of the light being bent.

Step 1 · BendingSnell's law on a tabletop

Light crossing into glass slows down, and the slowing turns it: the ratio of speeds is the refractive index n, and the turn is Snell's law, n₁·sin θ₁ = n₂·sin θ₂. One wrinkle hides the entire finale: n is not a constant of the glass. It depends on the wavelength — blue, the shorter wave, drags more and bends harder. The empirical curve is Cauchy's, from 1836: n(λ) ≈ A + B/λ². White light contains many wavelengths; a prism separates them because each wavelength refracts at a slightly different angle.

Newton's tabletop, in your browser tab. With dispersion at zero the prism bends the white beam — one n, one turn, no color. Raise it and the beam separates into a fan. Now rotate the prism until a face catches the inner beam too shallowly: the fan vanishes and the beam ricochets inside instead — total internal reflection, the same trapping that makes optical fiber possible. Every color in this figure is one number, λ, passed through two equations.

Aside · The crucial experiment

Newton's crucial 1666 experiment was not splitting white light; it used a second prism. A red beam picked out of the first fan and sent through another prism bends but does not split further. Color is not added by the glass; it was in the light all along, and the prism separates it. He published in Opticks in 1704, and the rendering equation's λ subscript is that result, still load-bearing.

Step 2 · Glass in the tracerReflection or refraction

In the path tracer, glass is a fork: part of the light reflects off the surface, part refracts through, in proportions given by the Fresnel equations (graphics uses Schlick's 1994 shortcut). A deterministic renderer would split the path and trace both — and double its work at every interface. Monte Carlo samples one branch: reflect with probability F, refract otherwise; across thousands of samples the average honors the exact proportions, because that is what averages do. The dice that integrated the rendering equation in part five absorb a new physical law without a new mechanism.

And the floor lights up. That bright-edged figure under the sphere is a caustic — the sphere is a lens, and the pattern is the lamp's image, focused and folded. Nothing draws it; camera paths that happen to pass through the glass on their way to the lamp simply deposit more light where the lens concentrates them. Shrink the lamp toward a point and the caustic sharpens to a burning filigree — and gets noisier, because ever fewer random paths thread the needle. Caustics are famously the path tracer's slowest meal, and you are watching exactly why.

Step 3 · One λ per raySpectral sampling

Every renderer so far in this series carried color as RGB — three floats along every ray. But n(λ) makes that a lie: a "white" ray entering glass has no single direction to bend. The spectral fix is pure Monte Carlo doctrine. The pixel's color is an integral over wavelengths? Then sample it: give each path one random λ, bend it by n(λ), and weight its contribution by how the eye responds to that wavelength (the CIE curves, here as little Gaussian fits). One more dimension folded into the same dice. 

λ = 380 + rand()·340            // each sample is one wavelength of one path
trace(ray, n(λ)) · eye_response(λ)   // Snell told the truth; the average makes the rainbow

At zero dispersion the spectral machinery idles invisibly — every λ bends alike, the weights sum to white, and you could never tell. The moment n spreads, the caustics fringe into rainbows: red and violet rays left the same lamp, hit the same prism face, and landed in different places. The renderer never contains the concept "rainbow." It contains Snell, Cauchy, and an unbiased average.

Step 4 · AssemblyThe complete optics renderer

The full instrument. Drag the lamp and watch caustics crawl across the floor; push dispersion past anything real glass could do; then open the aperture. Depth of field is the last rhyme of the series: a pinhole camera is one eye-point, a lens is an area of them — the same step that turned hard shadows soft in part five, applied to the camera itself. Rays start from random points on the lens disk, agree only at the focus distance, and everything elsewhere smears into bokeh. One more integral, the same dice.

Set focus on the sphere and the prism melts into colored fog; rack it back and the caustic on the floor — which lives at a different depth than the glass that cast it — drifts through sharpness on its own schedule. Every blur in this image is the size of some region the dice are averaging over: the lamp's area, the lens's disk, the spectrum's width. By now you can read a photograph as a stack of integrals.

CodaWhat spectral rendering adds

Six parts ago this series asked its question for the first time: not where do things go, which the gravity series spent six parts answering, but what do things see? It has now been answered three ways. Hierarchically — the cascades, which made every pixel see everything by being principled about what not to resolve. Heuristically — the distance field's certificates, spent on shadows and crevices at a fixed frame budget. And statistically — the dice, which answer any question you can phrase as an average, in exchange for patience measured in noise.

The lava lamp's glow, the bonfire's thousand embers, the fog's slanting shafts, the sculpture that exists nowhere, the box that converges out of static, the rainbow assembled from dice rolls — all of it ran live in this browser. The series moved from approximate two-dimensional transport to a spectral path tracer while keeping every stage interactive enough to inspect directly.
