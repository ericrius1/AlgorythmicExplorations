The Trees

These trees are generated by branch tips growing toward open space, then thickened with a pipe-model rule. Different seeds produce different trees.

Scroll down to read ↓

Everything in this series so far has been a function of position: sample the ground and soil fields, then plant a blade. Trees break the pattern. A tree is not a function — it is an iterative structure in which each branch is constrained by the branches created before it. Model that directly (as L-systems do, with grammar rules expanding like sentences) and you get convincing botany but the wrong feel: a grammar grows from the inside out, ignoring the world around it. Real trees grow from the outside in — shaped by the space that's available.

So this article uses the algorithm that takes that idea literally. It's called space colonization, it was published for trees in 2007 after a career simulating leaf veins, and it is a compact procedural model of competition for space.

Step 1 · The competitionBranches follow attraction points

The setup: scatter a few hundred attraction points through the volume where foliage could plausibly live — they represent light, open air, opportunity. Plant a trunk stub. Then loop three rules: 

1. each attractor votes for the nearest branch tip it can see (within influence radius)
2. each tip that got votes grows one step toward the average of its voters
3. attractors that a branch has reached (within kill radius) are deleted

Those three rules are the complete algorithm. Branching is not explicit, but it emerges, the moment one tip's voters disagree hard enough that successive growth steps split into separate clusters. Crown shape is never mentioned — it's just the silhouette of the attractor cloud. Branches never collide, because an attractor near an existing branch is already dead. Every behavior you'd want to program falls out of the competition for space.

Cyan dots are attractors; watch them go dark as branches reach them and the wavefront of growth moves up through the crown. The two dials control most of the result. Influence is how far a tip can see: small means branches only chase nearby points (dense, twiggy, shrub-like), large means everything pulls on everything (sparse, sweeping limbs). Kill radius is how thoroughly space gets used up: small and the crown packs solid; large and it stays airy. Real species differences — oak versus poplar versus windswept pine — come down mostly to these two numbers and the shape of the attractor cloud.

Aside · The algorithm came from leaves

Runions and colleagues built space colonization to model leaf venation first — veins racing toward auxin sources in a growing leaf — and only later turned it on whole trees, where the "auxin" became open space and the veins became wood. The same mechanism keeps being rediscovered at other scales: it draws lightning (charge chasing field maxima), coral, river deltas run backward, and the mycelium networks in every speculative sci-fi UI. One algorithm, most of nature's branchy things.

Step 2 · Branch thicknessThe pipe model

The skeleton comes out of colonization with no widths — every segment a wire. Real trunks are thick for a reason: wood is pipework, lifting water from roots to every leaf, and a branch must carry the combined flow of everything growing beyond it. Leonardo da Vinci noticed the consequence five centuries ago: the cross-sectional area of a trunk roughly equals the summed area of its children. As a recurrence, walking tips-to-root: 

radius(leaf tip) = twig
radius(branch)ᴱ  = Σ radius(child)ᴱ      // E = 2 is Leonardo; hydraulics measures ≈ 2.4

One backward pass over the nodes (children are always newer than parents, so reverse order just works) and the wire-frame becomes a tree — trunk thick, limbs tapering, every fork conserving cross-section the way real forks do. It's the same pattern as part two's carrying capacity: a single physically-motivated formula, applied uniformly, producing all the proportion that would otherwise take an artist's eye.

The studio grows the full 3D version in front of you — watch the trunk shoot and the twigs finish gently (that's the skeleton revealed in growth order, which the mesh already stores). The faceted pentagon branches and dented leaf blobs are deliberate: this site's creatures use low-poly silhouettes, and the trees use the same style. Then press perches.

Step 3 · The perch listPublishing perches

Those cyan markers are the reason trees joined this series early. Next door in Feather & Bone, an eagle is being built from lofted rings, bones, and feathers, and will eventually fly over this terrain and land in these trees. The landing controller needs to know which branches are graspable (radius within the toes' range), level enough (a bird lands on a rail, not a flagpole), and open from above (her approach path needs headroom). So every tree publishes a list of qualifying perches, computed from the skeleton, not the mesh: 

perch = { position, tangent, radius, headroom }   // what a landing controller asks for

The skeleton retains information absent from the triangles: parent relationships, thickness, and clearance above each branch. As with the bear's qi gong and the eagle's bones, keeping the structured representation after the mesh is built, because every later system would rather query structure than geometry.

Deep dive: the cost of growing a forest

Space colonization's inner loop is "every attractor finds its nearest node" — naively attractors × nodes per round, a few hundred times each, which lands around twenty million distance checks per tree. JavaScript completes that in well under a tenth of a second, and the demos here grow trees live without spatial indexing. (The 2007 paper used a 3D grid for the lookups; worth it for thousands of attractors, not for hundreds. The neighbor-search article in the particle series is exactly this optimization, if a forest of ten-thousand-attractor oaks is ever on the menu.)

The grove in the hero grows only four trees and instances them across the hillside with hashed rotations and scales. Two trees with the same skeleton but different yaw, lean, and size read as different trees — silhouette uniqueness matters, mesh uniqueness doesn't. The placement (which slope, what altitude, how far apart) is the next article's whole subject.

The world now has parts: mountains with drainage, meadows in the silt, trees with perches. But assembling them so far has been manual — each demo hand-places its grove. A landscape's real appearance depends heavily on the pattern: trees near the water line and thinning to krummholz at altitude, grass yielding to scree, the bare band above the treeline. That pattern has rules — and conveniently, they're functions of things we already compute (altitude, slope, moisture). Next article: biomes — the hash decides what grows where.
