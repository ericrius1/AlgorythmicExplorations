The Rain

The mountains under this camera are being rained on right now. Each drop rolls downhill, erodes material up to its carrying capacity, and deposits it where it slows — and by the time the storm passes, the noise from part one will have gullies, fans, and flat-floored valleys it never had before. Noise plus history.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part one produced mountains without erosion. Fractal noise gets the silhouette of terrain right — detail at every scale — but real landscapes are not just statistics. They are records. Every valley on Earth is the memory of water that passed through it, and noise has no memory at all. You can see the difference instantly even if you can't name it: fBm valleys wander aimlessly; real valleys connect, joining each other downstream in tree-shaped networks, because water that finds a channel makes that channel better at being found.

This article adds a simulated history. The tool is hydraulic erosion — not the full fluid dynamics of a watershed, but a simplified approximation: one raindrop at a time, simulated as a particle rolling over the heightfield, allowed to do only two things — pick up dirt and put it down. Run a few hundred thousand of them and geology comes out.

Step 1 · One dropletDroplet simulation

A droplet is five numbers: a position on the map, a direction, a speed, an amount of water, and a load of sediment. Its life is a loop: 

dir    = dir · inertia − gradient · (1 − inertia)   // downhill, with momentum
move one cell along dir
speed  = √(speed² + drop · gravity)                 // falling makes it faster
water *= (1 − evaporation)                          // shrinking makes it weaker

The inertia blend prevents abrupt direction changes. A droplet that follows the gradient exactly turns on a dime at every cell and gets trapped by every pebble-sized dimple in the noise. A droplet with momentum cuts smooth curves and can coast across small flats — which is how real water behaves, and it is the difference between drainage that meanders like a river and drainage that jitters like static. The height and gradient under the droplet come from bilinear interpolation, so the droplet feels a continuous landscape even though we only stored a grid.

Rain on the map and watch the paths. Nobody routed them — each droplet only ever sees the slope under its own feet — but they merge into the same channels, because converging is what downhill means. The demo shows how drainage networks emerge from local downhill motion rather than being drawn explicitly. Increase the inertia slider and the streams swing wide like rivers in flood; set it to zero and they hug every wrinkle.

Step 2 · SedimentThe carrying capacity

Tracing paths is hydrology; changing the ground is erosion. The rule that turns one into the other is carrying capacity — how much sediment a droplet can hold: 

capacity = slope · speed · water · C
if sediment > capacity:  deposit the surplus    // slow, flat, or drying up → drop it
else:                    erode toward capacity  // fast and hungry → dig

Everything geological follows from the product. On a steep face the droplet is fast, capacity is high, and it strips material. When it reaches the valley floor, slope collapses, capacity collapses with it, and the droplet sheds its load — which is why mountain streams run over bare rock while valley floors fill with soft flat alluvium. That one product is enough to reproduce both effects.

Two constraints keep the erosion stable. A droplet never erodes more than the height it is about to descend — otherwise it digs a spike pit below its own path and the terrain fills with needle holes. And it never digs at a single point: the bite is spread over a soft brush a few cells wide, because a real stream undercuts its banks and pulls the walls down after it. Skip the brush and you get channels exactly one cell wide — drainage drawn with a needle instead of carved by water.

Press rain and let the storm run — each second of it is thousands of complete droplet lifetimes. Flip before/after to see what the water did to the raw fBm: ridgelines survive nearly untouched (droplets are born slow and starving up there), slopes grow rills and gullies that join downstream, and the valley floors — watch them with sediment paint on — flatten under pale fans of deposited silt. That last one matters beyond scenery: part one faked flat valleys with a power curve; the rain produces them for the underlying reason.

Aside · Earlier erosion models

Eroding fractal terrain is almost as old as fractal terrain. Musgrave, Kolb and Mace's 1989 paper — the same one that gave part one its ridges — already ran hydraulic and thermal erosion over noise, on hardware with less memory than this paragraph. Thermal erosion is the lesser-known sibling: no water at all, just "scree slides downhill until slopes are stable," which rounds everything toward a desert look. Hydraulic carves, thermal relaxes; landscapes you believe tend to need a little of both.

Deep dive: why the result is stochastic

Each droplet is randomized only at birth — where it lands. From then on it is fully deterministic, and yet the aggregate looks richly stochastic. That's because the heightfield itself is the random number generator: tiny differences in landing position route droplets into entirely different watersheds. It also means the simulation is reproducible — same seed, same storm, same canyon. The droplets use the same hash as the mountains, preserving deterministic generation.

The expensive part is not the math per step (a bilinear sample and a multiply) but the brush — a 5×5-ish stamp on every eroding step. The budget arithmetic: 100,000 droplets × ~60 steps × ~20 brush cells ≈ 10⁸ memory touches, which is why the hero storm takes a few seconds to pass rather than a few frames. GPU implementations run the droplets in parallel and pay instead in collisions — two droplets writing one cell — whose errors mostly average out because erosion is already a statistical process.

Step 3 · OutputsResulting terrain

The next articles build on several outputs. Drainage networks: valleys now join downstream instead of wandering, one of the strongest visual cues of eroded terrain. Flat floors: deposition fills the low ground, making the only terrain flat enough to walk on, build on, or — looking ahead — grow a meadow on. Sharpened relief: erosion steepens the middle slopes while sparing the crests, stretching the histogram of slopes toward both ends. And one more, subtle: the sediment map itself. Recording where material was removed and deposited produces a useful soil map. Parts three and five use it to place vegetation.

CodaLimits of precomputed erosion

This approach introduces a constraint that matters for streaming. Part one's mountains were a pure function — ask h(x, z) at any coordinate, get the same answer forever, no storage. The rain broke that. Erosion is a process: each droplet changes the ground the next droplet sees, so the carved terrain exists only as a grid of modified heights — bounded, stored, finite. Unlike the source height function, eroded terrain cannot be evaluated at an arbitrary coordinate without prior simulation. Part six addresses this directly: streamed chunks cannot each carry a hundred-thousand-droplet history that agrees with their neighbors'. The practical solution is to use erosion nearby and the procedural height function at a distance.
