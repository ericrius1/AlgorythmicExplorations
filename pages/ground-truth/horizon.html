The Horizon

The terrain streams continuously. There is no stored far edge under the fog: chunks are built a few milliseconds at a time, just beyond what you can see, and dismantled just behind you. The world is generated around the camera as it moves.

Scroll down to read ↓

Every demo in this series so far has had four walls. A patch of mountains 16 units wide, a meadow 56 units wide — generous stages, but stages. Meanwhile, from the very first article, every design decision has supported this final requirement: part one insisted the ground be a pure function with no stored map; part five insisted every tree's existence depend only on integers it can compute locally. Those constraints were never about the demos that carried them. They support this article's question: what does it take to never run out of world?

Memory says you can't keep an infinite world; arithmetic says you never need to. If everything visible is a function of coordinates, then the world doesn't have to be stored — it has to be generated just in time in the moving window around the camera. The streaming system has three parts: chunks (what to build), levels of detail (how well to build it), and a budget (when to build it). Together they allow continuous movement through the terrain.

Step 1 · The ringChunk coordinates

Carve the plane into square chunks and key each one by the integer pair (⌊x/S⌋, ⌊z/S⌋). The rule of existence: a chunk exists if and only if it lies within a few chunks of the camera. Each frame, the manager compares that rule against reality — chunks newly inside the ring get queued for construction, nearest first; chunks that fall outside get dismantled, geometry and all. (One chunk of hysteresis on the way out, so a camera dithering on a boundary doesn't make the same chunk flicker in and out of existence.)

The view from above exposes the streaming process. The white flashes mark construction; watch how they wavefront ahead of the camera's motion, because the queue is sorted by distance and the budget only allows a couple of builds per frame. At the trailing edge, chunks vanish without losing unique state because every chunk can be regenerated. That is what all the determinism was for: revisit any coordinate next week and the hash rebuilds the same hill, the same boulder, the same tree leaning the same way.

Step 2 · Resolution by distanceDistance-based LOD

A chunk three rings out covers the same screen area as a postage stamp; building it at full resolution is generosity nobody sees. So resolution follows distance — full grid density for the ring the camera occupies, half for the next band, a quarter beyond that. The savings are not subtle: in this demo the far rings hold most of the chunks but a small fraction of the vertices, and the vertex total stays roughly constant no matter how far you fly.

The price of mixed resolutions is the seam. Where a 48-segment chunk meets a 24-segment neighbor, their edges sample the same height function at different spacings — and between samples they disagree, leaving pixel-thin cracks of sky. The exact fix (stitching edge vertices to match the coarser side) is a bookkeeping tax on every border of every LOD pair. The pragmatic fix, used by engines you've played, is the skirt: every chunk wears a short curtain of geometry hanging straight down from its border. Cracks don't show sky anymore; they show skirt, shaded like the terrain above it, and at any distance where LODs differ the eye reads continuous terrain. As with the grass normals two articles ago, the approximation is placed where it is difficult to notice.

Deep dive: matching normals across chunk seams

Cracks are the visible seam problem; lighting is the invisible one. Normals come from central differences on the height grid, and a chunk's border vertex has no neighbor on one side — use a one-sided difference there and every chunk boundary shows up as a faint crease of shading, even with zero geometric cracks. The fix costs almost nothing because the height function is pure: sample one extra row of heights beyond each edge — heights the chunk doesn't own and will never draw — and every border normal becomes two-sided, identical to the one its neighbor computes. Purity pays twice: the margin row is guaranteed to match the neighbor's interior, no communication required.

Step 3 · The budgetThe build budget

At sixty frames a second the renderer has 16.7 ms per frame, and building a chunk requires thousands of height samples, normals, a scatter query, sometimes ten thousand grass blades. Build everything at once and the world hitches every time you crest a ridge. The manager's discipline: each frame it builds from the front of the distance-sorted queue until a millisecond budget runs out, then stops mid-list and yields. Anything not built remains in the queue for the next frame. Flight continues while construction stays within its allotted time. 

wanted.sort((a, b) => a.ring - b.ring);              // distance-sorted queue
const t0 = performance.now();
for (const w of wanted) {
  if (!needsWork(w)) continue;
  if (performance.now() - t0 > budgetMs) continue;  // over budget — yield, try next frame
  buildOneStep(w);                                   // terrain, then scatter, then grass
}

The same thrift decides what far chunks deserve. Trees exist out to ring three (a forest's silhouette carries); grass exists only in the ring you could land in, because at two hundred meters a hundred thousand blades are, measurably, nothing. Population follows attention exactly the way resolution does.

The finished machine. Drag to steer (sideways for heading, up and down for altitude) and pick a direction you like — any direction; they're all infinite. Press freeze streaming and keep flying to do the useful test: catch up with the generated edge and inspect it. Unfreeze and watch the build queue close the gap in front of you. LOD tint paints each new chunk by its resolution class so you can see the pyramid of attention follow the camera; the readout's "last build" number is the budget at work.

Aside · Why erosion is omitted

One earlier technique is omitted. Part two's erosion is a history — each droplet changes the ground the next one sees — and a hundred-thousand-droplet history cannot be replayed per chunk in a 6 ms budget, nor chopped into chunks that agree at their borders (a river is the ultimate neighbor-dependence). So the infinite world approximates mature terrain with shaping curves, and keeps simulated rain for bounded scenes. This is a recurring tradeoff in procedural worlds: functions scale, while simulated history adds detail. Large games run erosion offline and ship the result as data; the no-download browser version chooses scale.

CodaA streamed procedural world

The completed system contains mountains from hashed gradients, valleys shaped by simulated erosion, meadows placed from sediment, trees with published perch data, biome placement, and continuous streaming. Every part is reproducible from a seed and a coordinate.

The terrain is also used by the final Feather & Bone demo. That series adds wingbeats, banking turns, and a landing flare onto one of the generated branches.
