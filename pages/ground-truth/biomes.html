The Biomes

Forests hold the wet ground, meadows take the middle, scree and snow divide the heights. Two noise fields and a slope test determine the distribution under this camera.

Scroll down to read ↓

The series has parts now — mountains, drainage, grass, trees — but so far every demo has assembled them by hand: this grove here, that meadow there. In a real landscape, the pattern is information: where trees stop indicates the temperature, where green turns tan tells you the rainfall, and a glance at a hillside reveals its climate. Uniform placement, by contrast, makes procedural terrain visibly artificial.

Ecologists compressed the real rules into one famous picture. Whittaker's biome diagram plots temperature against precipitation and carves the plane into regions: tundra here, grassland there, forest where both are generous. This article builds the pocket version — altitude stands in for temperature, a noise field stands in for rainfall — and then spends its real effort on a quieter problem: placing a million plants in a way that an infinite world will be able to reproduce, chunk by chunk, without any two chunks ever comparing notes. That second problem decides the design of everything.

Step 1 · ClimateThe moisture field

The terrain already gives us altitude and slope at any point — both pure functions. For the missing moisture axis, we use the same method that made the mountains: fractal noise, but much broader — climate varies over kilometers, not meters. Three octaves at a fiftieth of the terrain's frequency produce wet regions and rain shadows that sprawl across many hills, which is what makes the pattern read as weather rather than camouflage: 

moisture(x, z) = fbm(x · k, z · k) + offset      // k broad; offset is the climate dial
biome(h, slope, m): slope > 0.55 → cliff
                    h > treeline + ε → alpine
                    h > treeline     → m wet ? meadow : scree
                    else             → m wet ? forest : m mild ? meadow : scrub

The classification painted over the terrain. Flip to the moisture view to see the underlying field — note how much broader its features are than the hills'. Then play the two dials: wet ↔ dry shifts the whole world's climate (slide toward dry and watch forests retreat to the dampest hollows, like a time-lapse of a drying century); tree line moves the altitude where trees surrender. Real tree lines do exactly this — they're a temperature contour made visible in vegetation.

Aside · The missing river

A more physically grounded moisture model would use drainage. Water flows downhill and collects, so valley floors are wet because of the shape of the terrain, and part two computed precisely that. But erosion's output is a stored grid, and this article needs moisture to be a pure function for the infinite world's sake. So the noise is a stand-in, and the compromise is visible if you look: this climate doesn't quite follow the valleys. The final article will spend real effort on exactly this tension — what must be a function, and what's allowed to be history.

Step 2 · PlacementDeterministic placement

Knowing forest-or-meadow at a point is not the same as knowing where each individual tree stands. Tree spacing has texture — natural but not uniform, scattered but never clumped into absurdity — and graphics' favorite answer is Poisson-disk sampling: points placed randomly but never closer than a minimum distance. It looks superb. It is also a process: each new sample must check the samples placed before it, which means a chunk can't place its trees without knowing its neighbors' trees — and that dependency is poison for a streaming world (the same poison as erosion, in a smaller bottle).

The chunk-proof alternative is the jittered grid. Divide the world into cells in global coordinates — cell indices from floor(x / cellSize), not from any chunk origin. Each cell hosts at most one candidate, jittered inside the cell by the cell's own hash. Any region query now reproduces the identical trees, because a tree's existence and position depend only on its cell's integers: 

candidate(ci, cj) = ((ci + jitter(hash)) · cell, (cj + jitter(hash)) · cell)
keep if: hash(ci, cj) < P(biome at candidate)     // the climate votes, the hash decides

Spacing comes free (two candidates can't share a cell), and the slight regularity that makes jittered grids inferior to Poisson for sampling theory is invisible once trees land on hilly ground with hashed scales and rotations. The acceptance probability is where ecology re-enters: near-certain in forest, a stray pioneer in meadows, zero above the tree line — with a fade band below it where acceptance drops and the trees themselves shrink, because real tree lines end in stunted krummholz, not a fence.

Every tree, rock, and grass blade here was accepted or rejected by the rules — slide the climate and watch whole forests migrate downhill into the hollows. The rocks follow their own grid (scree fields above the tree line, boulders thinning to pebbles by a squared hash, because nature stocks many more small rocks than large). The readout's tally is the article's thesis: nobody placed any of them, and yet none of them is anywhere twice.

Deep dive: four trees playing a forest

The patch above contains hundreds of trees but only four tree geometries — grown once by part four's space colonization, then instanced with hashed yaw and scale. The eye identifies a tree by silhouette, and a rotated, rescaled skeleton on different ground reads as a different individual; meshes are expensive, silhouettes are free. The same economy stretches further in big games — LOD imposters, billboard crowds — but the principle is already fully present here: uniqueness is a property of placement, not of geometry.

One subtlety worth stealing: each instanced variant is one draw call per variant, not per tree, so the whole forest costs about a dozen draw calls regardless of count. The grass takes the part-three route instead (one merged buffer) because blades are too small to be worth a matrix each. Two scales of the same problem, two different answers — the boundary between them is roughly "does one instance have more vertices than its matrix has floats."

CodaPreparing for streaming

Stand back and audit what the series now owns. Ground: h(x, z), a pure function. Climate: moisture(x, z), a pure function. Population: scatter(region), a pure function of cell integers. Everything the eye sees is now computable from coordinates alone — no stored maps, no history, no assembly steps — except for rain, which still uses a grid, and the streaming system. The next article builds that system: chunks, rings of level-of-detail, a build budget measured in milliseconds, and a camera that can fly in one direction for as long as you care to hold the key. The world is already infinite on paper. Part six makes it infinite in person — and somewhere above it, an eagle is going to need somewhere to land.
