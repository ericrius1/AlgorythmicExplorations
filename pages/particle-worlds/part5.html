Gravity in Frequency Space

Part five solves gravity in frequency space, using a mesh instead of a tree, and grows the cosmic web from small primordial density variations. Drag through it to perturb the field.

Scroll down to read ↓

Every part of this series so far has simulated something you could point a telescope at: a galaxy, a dome of stars, a glass of water, a ring of dust becoming moons. The simulation above models matter on cosmological scales. Each glowing fleck is not a star but a parcel of matter the size of a galaxy cluster, half a million of them filling a box that stands in for the whole universe — starting from the universe's actual starting condition: almost perfectly smooth, with ripples of one part in a hundred thousand. Left alone, gravity amplifies those ripples into knots, the knots link into filaments, the filaments fence off voids. Astronomers call the result the cosmic web. It is the largest known pattern in the distribution of matter. Large galaxy surveys reveal the same broad structure.

None of the machinery this series has built can run that simulation. Part one's tree is efficient when it can summarize clumps; the early universe is uniform, and the structure is in very small differences. Part three's grid assumes forces with a cutoff; gravity has none. This article uses a third structure: a mesh and a Fourier transform that solve gravity in frequency space.

Step 1 · A third kind of problemPeriodic boundaries

Start with what makes cosmology different. A galaxy is a thing in empty space; you can put a box around it and the box ends. The universe is not in a box — wherever you stand, it continues, statistically the same, in every direction. Simulators commonly handle this by making the box periodic. Leave through the right wall, re-enter through the left. A particle near the edge pulls on its neighbours through the wall. Part four freed the grid from its box; cosmology glues the box back together into a torus.

For part one's tree this is quietly disastrous. Barnes–Hut walks outward until contributions dwindle — but on a torus there is no outward, and the sum over infinite periodic images converges so slowly that using Ewald summation, borrowed from crystallography, roughly doubles the cost and triples the code. The deeper problem: in the early universe, density contrasts are 0.001%. A tree spends its cleverness deciding which lumps matter; here there are no lumps yet.

The key change is to stop thinking of gravity as pairs and start thinking of it as a field. Mass density is a function ρ over the box. The gravitational potential φ is another function, and physics relates them with one equation — Poisson's: 

∇²φ = 4πG ρ        // the potential's curvature equals the density

Forces are then the downhill direction: F = -∇φ. There are no particle pairs. Each particle samples the local gradient of a field that already includes every mass and periodic image. Given the density everywhere, we must solve that equation for the potential everywhere. Sample the box on a 512×512 mesh and Poisson's equation becomes 262,144 simultaneous equations, every unknown coupled to its neighbours, sixty times a second. That sounds like a research problem. In frequency space, the core solve is three lines of code.

Step 2 · Fourier decompositionRepresenting a field with waves

The key fact: any function over the box — however jagged — can be written exactly as a sum of sine waves. That is Fourier's theorem, and the demo below makes it tactile. A signal of 256 samples, rebuilt from its waves, lowest frequency first. The bump is cheap — a handful of waves nail it. The square wave needs many more components: slide the budget up and watch the overshoot at the corners persist. Even noise can be reconstructed, because 256 samples can only hold 128 waves of information — the two representations are the same size. Nothing is lost in either direction.

Aside · The committee refused to publish it

In 1807 Joseph Fourier, fresh from administering half of Egypt for Napoleon, handed the French Academy a memoir on heat flow containing this claim — and the committee, including Lagrange, the greatest analyst alive, refused to publish it. Lagrange objected that sums of smooth waves could never make a sharp corner. He was nearly right: the corner costs infinitely many waves, and they ring where they meet it — the overshoot you just saw is the Gibbs phenomenon. Fourier's result survived the objection and became foundational to modern analysis.

The point that matters for us: a function and its spectrum are the same object written in two alphabets. The samples say what the value is at each place; the spectrum says how much of each wave it contains. Either determines the other. Changing alphabets is the Fourier transform, and the reason to do it is that some sentences are dramatically easier to say in one alphabet than the other. Ours is one of them.

Step 3 · Frequency spaceSolving Poisson's equation

Take a single wave of density: ρ = sin(kx). Differentiate twice and you get −k² sin(kx) — the same wave, scaled. Waves pass through the Laplacian untouched in shape. So for one wave, the potential is just that wave divided by −k², and there is nothing more to solve. Any density is a sum of such waves; the equation is linear, so each wave solves independently — and the system of 262,144 coupled equations falls apart into 262,144 separate one-line problems.

Watch the solve happen under your scroll wheel — decompose, divide, recombine:

In code: 

spectrum = fft2d(density);          // change alphabet
for (each wave k) {
  spectrum[k] /= -dot(k, k);        // ...solve Poisson's equation (!)
}
potential = inverse_fft2d(spectrum); // change back

The middle line completes the physical solve. In position space, Poisson's equation couples every cell to every other cell; in frequency space it becomes elementwise division. Dividing by k² suppresses short wavelengths relative to long ones. Periodic boundaries are a natural fit because the basis waves close across the torus exactly, so the transform handles the boundary condition directly.

Deep dive: the k = 0 wave, or why only contrast gravitates

One wave gets special treatment: k = 0, the box's average density, would divide by zero. We zero it instead, which quietly subtracts the mean — in a periodic universe only contrast gravitates, because the uniform background pulls equally in every direction and cancels. Cosmologists argued about the legitimacy of this for decades (it's sometimes called the "Jeans swindle"); setting the zero mode to zero implements the required subtraction directly.

Below, the idea running live. Drag to paint mass; the box solves Poisson's equation for the entire field every frame and draws the potential as a topographic map. Fifty thousand weightless tracer grains fall through the field so you can feel the slopes. Paint two blobs and watch their wells merge into one basin before the blobs themselves touch. Paint near an edge and the well wraps — the torus, visible. Shift-drag erases.

This is the third change of representation in the series. The tree didn't make gravity cheaper; it changed the question to one a tree answers fast. The sort didn't find neighbours faster; it rearranged memory so neighbours found themselves. And the transform doesn't grind through a differential equation; it carries the problem to an alphabet where the equation was never hard. Mathematicians call the pattern diagonalization; engineers call it picking the right representation. This change of representation is a common move in computational science.

Step 4 · Fast Fourier transformO(n log n)

One performance problem remains: fft2d itself. Computing one wave's coefficient is a weighted sum over all n samples; n coefficients therefore cost n², recreating the scaling problem from earlier parts. A 512×512 mesh transformed naively is ~69 billion multiplications per transform, four transforms per frame. The scheme dies right here without a fast transform.

The escape: a transform of size n splits into two transforms of size n/2 — one on the even-indexed samples, one on the odd — stitched together with one rotation per output. Applied recursively, that is n log n. The wiring diagram below is the butterfly network for 16 points: log₂(16) = 4 layers, each layer pairing every value with a partner half the array apart, then a quarter, then an eighth. The shape matches part three's prefix-sum ladder, except that each stage applies complex rotations. The same doubling cascade also maps well to a GPU. The bars underneath race the direct n² transform against the FFT on your machine, right now.

Aside · Early and modern FFTs

The fast transform was found by the right man absurdly early. In 1805 Carl Friedrich Gauss — interpolating the orbit of the asteroid Pallas, which is to say, doing gravity — needed exactly these sums, noticed the even/odd split, scribbled it in a notebook in Latin, and never published. The idea went unpublished for 160 years. In 1965, Cooley and Tukey independently developed it so seismometer arrays could distinguish Soviet underground nuclear tests from earthquakes — a treaty-verification problem with a budget. Within a decade the FFT was running everywhere signals were. Every phone call, every image, every MRI scan passes through Gauss's notebook; by operation count it is plausibly the most-executed nontrivial algorithm in existence.

Deep dive: the FFT as a GPU kernel

One workgroup takes one row of the mesh into shared memory, folds it log₂(512) = 9 times with a barrier between folds — the identical rhythm to part three's scan — and writes it back. Rows, then columns: a 2D transform is just two rounds of 1D ones. Four such transforms plus one division per cell make the box's exact periodic gravity cost about as much as blurring a 512-pixel image — under a millisecond.

Step 5 · Particle-mesh methodCoupling particles to the field

The solver speaks fields; the matter is particles. The bridge is the last new idea, and after parts three and four it will feel familiar. The scheme is called particle-mesh, and each step walks one lap: 

deposit:  particles paint their mass onto the mesh     // gather → field
solve:    FFT → divide by -k² → inverse FFT             // solve for the potential
gradient: difference neighbouring cells → force mesh    // downhill, per cell
gather:   particles read the force where they stand     // field → particles
kick & drift, wrap through the walls, repeat

Only deposit and gather need design. The crude deposit drops each particle's whole mass into the cell under it — and jitters: as a particle glides across a cell border its mass teleports, the field twitches, and the twitch kicks the particle that caused it. The standard fix, cloud-in-cell, splits the mass over the four cells the particle overlaps, weighted by overlap area, so the deposit slides as smoothly as the particle does. Gathering interpolates the force back with the same four weights — using one scheme down and a different one up injects tiny self-forces, a particle tugging its own bootstraps. And that is all of it — no tree to walk, no buckets to sort, just paint, transform, divide, transform, read. Below, the full machine: part four's cold blob of dust, reborn in a periodic box. The slider changes nothing about the particles and everything about the mesh. At 64×64 the force is a blur and the collapse is mush; at 512×512 the same blob shatters into filaments and knots. The method has one important limit: below the size of a cell, gravity does not exist. The mesh is the softening. Watch the edges of the box while it collapses — the periodic images collapse in perfect sympathy, because they are it.

For a galaxy, that price is fatal — part one needed forces down at star spacing, which is why it grew a tree. For cosmology the price is perfect: the structures of interest are enormous, smooth, and everywhere, exactly what a uniform mesh resolves evenly. Tree and mesh are not rivals; they are two ends of one trade — the tree spends bookkeeping to focus resolution where matter clumps, the mesh spends uniformity to make the whole box cheap. Hold that thought for the coda.

Step 6 · Initial conditionsEvolving the density field

The solver is built. Two ingredients remain for the cosmology demo.

The seeds. The young universe was almost featureless — density ripples of one part in 10⁵, random in phase but with a measured statistical fingerprint: how much ripple at each scale, the power spectrum. We know it because the ripples are photographed — they are the mottling of the cosmic microwave background, mapped by COBE, WMAP and Planck. Making initial conditions is therefore short: fill a grid with white noise, transform it, scale each wave to the chosen spectrum. Then, rather than placing particles randomly (which would add noise of our own), start them on a perfect lattice and displace each one along the field's gradient, with tiny velocities pointing the same way.

Aside · Zel'dovich, 1970

The displacement recipe is Zel'dovich's approximation: for the gentle beginning of structure formation, every ripple grows in lockstep, so a straight-line nudge is exact. The seeds are made of FFTs too, and run in milliseconds at re-seed time.

The stretching. Space itself expands, and gravity must win against it. Simulators fold the expansion into the coordinates — comoving coordinates ride the stretch, so the box stays size 1 while real distances grow with the scale factor a(t). The expansion survives only as a drag term in the equations of motion: kicks and drifts weighted by powers of a. Nothing else changes — the same kernels, the same four FFTs; the clock just ticks in scale factor, from a = 0.02 (a universe at 2% of its present size) toward a = 1, which is the day you are reading this.

Run it. The readout shows the scale factor climbing; the toggle shows why expansion matters. With expansion off, gravity runs unopposed and the box crunches into a few fat blobs. With it on, gravity wins slowly, locally, and only where the seeds favoured it: knots first, then filaments between knots, then the great voids as matter drains onto the web. The ripple strength slider is the cosmologists' σ₈: turn it down and structure arrives later and wispier. The tilt reweights large scales against small: negative grows a few grand filaments, positive a foam of small halos. These are the same dials Planck measured for the real sky — and there are only three of them.

One important caveat: this box is 2D, so the growth exponents differ from our universe's, and the "web" is really walls and wells seen edge-on. The algorithms are the real ones — the dimensions are a display choice. Add the third coordinate and this exact pipeline — FFTs, cloud-in-cell, comoving kicks — is how the canonical simulations of the 1980s established that cold dark matter plus these seeds makes the galaxy distribution we observe.

CodaComparing the three spatial methods

Across five parts, the n² wall fell three times, using three different computational structures:

1. The tree summarized space. Far things blur; resolution should follow interest. Part one's pyramid answers "everything, from here" in log n, and pays in memory spent on hierarchy.

2. The sort organized memory. Near things touch; neighbours should be adjacent in RAM, not just in space. Part three's counting sort answers "who is beside me" in O(1), and pays with a scan.

3. The transform changed alphabets. Some equations are only hard in the language they were asked in. The FFT carries Poisson's equation to the alphabet where it is division, and pays n log n.

And the real codes — the ones that ran the Millennium Simulation's ten billion particles, the ones fitting dark energy right now — choose none of the above. They choose two. TreePM splits gravity at a seam a few mesh cells wide: the mesh (this article) carries the far field, periodic and fast; the tree (part one) carries the near field, sharp and adaptive where matter clumps. Part four married the tree to the sort; the professionals marry the tree to the mesh. Every structure in this series is load-bearing in production cosmology, in combinations chosen to match the physics at different scales.

Further extensions include a third coordinate, adaptive meshes nested in deep wells, and initial conditions drawn from Planck's actual parameter chains rather than a slider. The same FFT used to reconstruct a square wave also generates the initial density field, while the same gravity from part one evolves it into a cosmic web.
