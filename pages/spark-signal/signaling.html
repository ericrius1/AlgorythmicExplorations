How Neurons Signal

This series approaches neuroscience through biophysics rather than starting with brain regions and Latin vocabulary. Most of what we call "thinking" is downstream of a much older mechanism: a cell membrane that holds a voltage, briefly flips it, and passes the flip to its neighbours. Memory, emotion, and language all ride on top of that — but the spike is the atom. So we start with the spike and build outward.

A neuron is not a wire. It is a sealed bag of salty water — the cytoplasm — wrapped in an oily lipid bilayer pierced by protein pores called ion channels. Most of the time the inside sits about 70 millivolts negative relative to the fluid outside. That steady voltage, the resting potential, is not idle — it is stored energy, like a compressed spring. Sensation, thought, and movement all begin when something releases that spring far enough to trigger a brief, stereotyped pulse called an action potential, which then hands the message to the next cell across a synapse.

Four live simulations below walk through that chain. Each one isolates a layer of the mechanism so you can see what changes when you turn a conductance knob, click an axon, or stimulate a neuron in a small circuit. The numbers in the prose match the models running in your browser.

Prologue · The partsAnatomy of a neuron

Before the physics, the geography. A typical neuron has three functional zones, and each zone does a different job in the signaling pipeline: 

dendrites   → receive input from other neurons (graded potentials)
soma        → integrate input; decide whether to fire
axon        → broadcast an all-or-none spike to downstream targets
terminal    → convert the electrical spike into a chemical message

The dendritic tree contains thousands of thin branches covered in synapses. Their inputs are small voltage changes, not spikes. They sum in the soma. If the sum crosses a line called threshold, the soma launches an action potential down the axon, a cable that can stretch from your spine to your toe. At the axon terminal, the electrical event becomes chemistry: neurotransmitter molecules dumped into a gap, received by the next neuron, and the whole cycle repeats.

Aside · 100 billion is a misleading number

Textbooks often say the human brain has ~86 billion neurons. The more important fact for signaling is the connection count: each neuron receives input from hundreds to tens of thousands of others. The brain is not a collection of cells — it is a collection of graphs. Part four of this post is already a tiny one.

Step 1 · The charged membraneResting potential

Where does −70 mV come from? Two forces tug on every ion: a concentration gradient (ions diffuse from where they're crowded to where they're scarce) and an electrical gradient (opposite charges attract, like charges repel). At equilibrium those forces cancel. For potassium alone, with the inside rich in K⁺, the balance point is about −77 mV — the Nernst potential for K⁺.

The resting membrane is not at −77 mV, because sodium leaks in too. Na⁺ is scarce inside and abundant outside; even the small number of open leak channels lets it creep in, pulling the voltage upward (less negative). The sodium–potassium pump actively trades 3 Na⁺ out for 2 K⁺ in, spending ATP to maintain the gradients that make the resting potential possible. The compromise voltage — what you actually measure at rest — is about −70 mV. 

resting potential ≈ −70 mV     (inside relative to outside)
K⁺ equilibrium    ≈ −77 mV     (if only potassium channels were open)
Na⁺ equilibrium   ≈  +58 mV     (if only sodium channels were open)
threshold         ≈ −55 mV     (voltage that triggers a spike)
peak of spike     ≈  +40 mV     (brief reversal, then recovery)

The demo below is a scrolling voltage trace of a patch of membrane. The cell diagram on the right brightens when the interior depolarizes. Adjust the Na⁺ and K⁺ conductance sliders and watch the resting line drift: more K⁺ conductance pulls toward −77 mV; more Na⁺ leak pulls upward. Hit inject current to mimic synaptic input arriving at the soma. A small pulse produces a small bump — a graded response. Push past threshold and the full action potential unfolds: voltage-gated Na⁺ channels snap open (rapid depolarization), then voltage-gated K⁺ channels open (repolarization), then the cell overshoots slightly negative (hyperpolarization) before settling back.

To inspect the model, lower K⁺ conductance and inject current: the cell is harder to repolarize — spikes get wider and stranger. Raise Na⁺ conductance at rest: the resting line creeps toward threshold and the cell becomes excitable, firing with smaller inputs. The shape of the spike — how fast it rises, how fast it falls — is not decorative. It is the fingerprint of which channels opened, in what order, for how long.

Deep dive: the membrane as a capacitor

The lipid bilayer is an insulator roughly 5 nm thick. Cytoplasm and extracellular fluid are conductors. That geometry makes the membrane a capacitor: charge piled on one side produces voltage across the barrier without ions necessarily crossing. Ion channels are the resistors — each conductance is a path for charge to flow and equalize. The time constant of a neuron (how fast voltage changes when current arrives) is τ = R × C: membrane capacitance times channel resistance.

This is why dendrites can integrate slowly: their capacitance is large and their input resistance is high. A synaptic input delivers a brief current pulse; the voltage rises, then leaks back down. If another input arrives before the leak finishes, the voltages add. That temporal summation — plus spatial summation from many branches — is how a neuron counts votes before deciding to fire.

Aside · Not electricity in a wire

The signal is a voltage wave caused by ion movement across a capacitor, not electrons sluicing through a copper conductor. That is why neurons are slow — unmyelinated axons conduct at roughly 1 m/s; myelinated ones reach ~120 m/s — yet extraordinarily versatile: the same physical principle encodes the touch of a key, the colour of the sky, and the decision to reach for coffee. Their key property is reliable, conditional signal propagation rather than raw speed.

Step 2 · The spikeAction potential propagation

An action potential is all-or-none. Weak input that fails to reach threshold produces nothing; input that barely reaches threshold produces a full-sized spike. There is no "small" action potential. This is why the spike is a digital bit riding on an analog world — the dendrites do analog summation; the axon commits to a binary event.

Once one patch of axon membrane fires, local current spreads to neighbouring patches. Each region has its own complement of voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels. When the depolarization front arrives, Na⁺ channels in that region open too, and that region fires. The result is a traveling pulse — self-regenerating, like a burning fuse made of voltage.

The pulse travels in one direction because the region behind it is briefly refractory: Na⁺ channels are inactivated and cannot reopen for a millisecond or two. Forward is fresh membrane; backward is spent. This absolute refractory period also sets a maximum firing rate — a typical neuron cannot spike much faster than ~500 Hz, and most fire far slower. 

unmyelinated axon   ≈   1 m/s conduction
myelinated axon     ≈ 120 m/s conduction   (saltatory jumping at nodes of Ranvier)
refractory period   ≈   1–2 ms             (limits max firing rate)

Myelin sheaths — fatty insulation wrapped around many axons — are not modeled in the demo, but they matter enormously. Myelin increases membrane resistance and decreases capacitance, so the pulse jumps between exposed gaps called nodes of Ranvier rather than fighting through insulated cable. Multiple sclerosis is, in part, myelin destruction: signals slow, distort, or fail.

The nervous system encodes some information in timing (when the spike arrives), not just whether it arrives. Sound localization in the brainstem compares arrival times from the two ears at microsecond resolution.

Deep dive: why the spike shape is conserved

Every patch of membrane on the axon runs the same channel program. That is why the action potential looks the same at the cell body and at the fingertip — the waveform is a property of the membrane dynamics, not of the stimulus that triggered it. The stimulus only decides if and when; the channels decide what.

This conservation is what lets axons be long. A sensory neuron from your toe has one axon that can be over a meter long, yet the spike that arrives in your spinal cord is the same shape as the one that left your skin. The alternative — graded signals decaying with distance, as in dendrites — would not survive the trip.

Step 3 · The gapSynaptic transmission

Neurons rarely touch. The synaptic cleft — the gap between cells — is about 20 nanometres wide. An action potential arriving at the presynaptic terminal opens voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels. Calcium rushes in (it is far more concentrated outside than inside). Calcium does not directly change the postsynaptic voltage; instead it triggers the fusion of synaptic vesicles with the membrane, dumping neurotransmitter into the cleft.

Neurotransmitter diffuses across the gap in microseconds, binds postsynaptic receptors, and opens ion channels. If the channels pass Na⁺ or depolarizing current, the postsynaptic cell gets a small upward bump — an EPSP (excitatory postsynaptic potential). If they pass Cl⁻ or K⁺ in a hyperpolarizing configuration, you get an IPSP (inhibitory). These are graded again: more transmitter, more channels open, bigger bump. One vesicle releases only a fraction of the transmitter needed to fire the next cell. 

synaptic cleft width     ≈  20 nm
vesicle contents         ≈ 5,000 molecules of transmitter (order of magnitude)
EPSP size (one vesicle)  ≈  0.1–1 mV   (too small to reach threshold alone)
typical threshold gap    ≈ 15 mV from rest to spike

A single synaptic event therefore produces a small voltage change. The postsynaptic neuron integrates thousands of them. Spatial summation: many EPSPs from different synapses add. Temporal summation: EPSPs arriving in quick succession add before the membrane leaks back. Only when the combined depolarization crosses threshold does the postsynaptic cell fire its own action potential.

Excitation and inhibition are not opposites in a moral sense — they are opposite signs of current. Inhibition is not "off"; it is active shoving the membrane away from threshold. Many circuits use inhibition to sculpt timing: feedforward inhibition can narrow the window in which a cell is allowed to fire; lateral inhibition sharpens contrast (this is how the retina begins edge detection).

Aside · Glutamate and GABA

The brain's main excitatory neurotransmitter is glutamate; the main inhibitory one is GABA. Dozens of others (dopamine, serotonin, acetylcholine, …) modulate circuits on slower timescales — changing gain, mood, attention — but fast point-to-point signaling is mostly glutamate and GABA. Every psychedelic, every antidepressant, every cup of coffee eventually touches this chemistry; the details are for later posts.

Deep dive: electrical synapses — when cells touch directly

Not all synapses are chemical. Gap junctions — electrical synapses — connect the cytoplasm of two cells directly through pores called connexons. Current flows instantly, with no vesicle delay. They are common in cardiac muscle (so your heart beats as one unit) and in some brain circuits that need sub-millisecond synchrony. This post focuses on chemical synapses because they are slower, amplifiable, and chemically reconfigurable — the basis for learning.

Step 4 · The circuitNetworks

A single neuron in isolation is a curiosity. A network of neurons is a computer — or at least, the substrate on which whatever the brain is doing gets done. Each spike in one cell contributes weighted input to every cell it connects to. The weight can be positive (excitatory) or negative (inhibitory). The receiving cell integrates all inputs and decides whether to fire.

The graph structure matters as much as the weights. Feedforward layers transform input to output. Recurrent loops hold state — a trace of the recent past — in the ongoing activity of the circuit. Inhibitory interneurons (small, fast, GABAergic) are the clock and the sculptor: without them, excitatory networks tend toward epilepsy-like runaway firing. 

typical cortical neuron   ≈ 10,000 synaptic inputs
cortical firing rate      ≈   0.1–10 Hz average (bursts can be higher)
energy cost of a spike    ≈ 10⁸ ATP molecules (mostly pump work to restore gradients)

No cortical neuron is ever truly silent — it is always being pushed and pulled by thousands of unrelated inputs.

Aside · Hodgkin and Huxley, 1952

The membrane demo uses simplified Hodgkin–Huxley kinetics — the equations Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley fitted to the giant squid axon, for which they shared the Nobel Prize in 1963. They voltage-clamped the membrane (holding it at set voltages with a feedback circuit) and measured the ionic currents that flowed — decomposing the action potential into sodium entry and potassium exit. Their model is still the textbook account of how voltage-gated channels produce the spike shape.

Deep dive: the spike as a design constraint

Why all-or-none? Why not send graded voltages down the axon, as dendrites do? Because graded signals decay exponentially with distance. An action potential solves this by spending energy to regenerate the signal at every patch of membrane so it arrives intact a metre away. Nervous systems use that mechanism wherever long-range reliable transmission mattered.

Why chemical synapses? They add latency (~0.5 ms per synapse) but gain amplification and flexibility. A small presynaptic spike can release enough transmitter to depolarize a large postsynaptic cell; the sign and strength of the connection can be tuned by receptor type and channel count; and the synapse can change with experience — the physical basis of learning, which this series will take up in part two.

EpilogueThe signaling pipeline

Dissection complete. A neural signal is not one thing — it is a sequence of transformations, each with its own physics: 

dendrites     → graded potentials     (analog input, summation)
soma          → threshold decision    (analog → digital conversion)
axon          → action potential      (digital broadcast, all-or-none)
synapse       → chemical transmission (digital → analog again, with gain)
next neuron   → repeat

Every sensation you have ever had rode that pipeline. Light hit your retina; photoreceptors changed their glutamate release; bipolar and ganglion cells summed the result; ganglion cells fired spikes down the optic nerve; the lateral geniculate relayed them to cortex; and somewhere in the chain the physical world became seeing. Strip away the myelin, the neurotransmitters, the Latin names, and the skeleton is always what you simulated today: voltage, threshold, propagation, chemistry.

What this post left out is as important as what it included. Real synapses change strength with use (plasticity). Glial cells feed and modulate neurons. Dendrites are not passive wires — they can compute locally. Neuromodulators reconfigure entire circuits on second-to-hour timescales. And the graph is not a generic network — it has a wiring diagram shaped by genes and experience. Part two of this series takes up the first of those: how repeated firing at a synapse makes the connection stronger, and what that has to do with memory.
