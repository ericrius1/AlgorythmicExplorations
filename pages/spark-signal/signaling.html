How Neurons Signal

A neuron is not a wire. It is a sealed bag of salty water — the cytoplasm — wrapped in an oily membrane that is selectively permeable to ions. Most of the time it sits at a steady electrical voltage, the resting potential, roughly −70 millivolts inside relative to outside. Thought, sensation, and movement all begin when that voltage shifts enough to trigger a brief, stereotyped pulse called an action potential, which then hands the message to the next cell across a synapse.

This post walks through that chain with four live simulations. Each one isolates a layer of the mechanism so you can see what changes when you turn a conductance knob or poke a cell.

Step 1 · The charged membraneResting potential

The inside of a neuron is rich in K⁺ and poor in Na⁺; the extracellular fluid is the opposite. Ion channels in the membrane leak mostly potassium, so K⁺ diffuses out until its negative charge pulls it back — an equilibrium near −77 mV for K⁺ alone. Sodium channels are mostly closed at rest, but a small leak still pulls the resting voltage to about −70 mV. 

resting potential ≈ −70 mV     (inside relative to outside)
threshold       ≈ −55 mV     (voltage that triggers a spike)
peak of spike   ≈  +40 mV     (brief reversal, then recovery)

Aside · Not electricity in a wire

The signal is a voltage wave caused by ion movement across a capacitor (the membrane), not electrons sluicing through a conductor. That is why neurons are slow (up to ~120 m/s in myelinated axons) yet extraordinarily versatile: the same physical principle encodes touch, vision, and the decision to reach for coffee.

Step 2 · The spikeAction potential propagation

Once one patch of membrane fires, local current spreads to neighboring patches. Each region has its own voltage-gated Na⁺ and K⁺ channels; when the depolarization front arrives, that region fires too. The result is a traveling pulse — unidirectional because the region behind is briefly refractory (channels recovering).

Myelin sheaths (not modeled here) insulate the axon and let the pulse jump between gaps called nodes of Ranvier, greatly increasing speed. In the demo, each colored segment is a patch of axon membrane. Click anywhere along the axon to stimulate, or use the button. Brighter segments are depolarized; orange ticks above show local voltage.

Step 3 · The gapSynaptic transmission

Neurons rarely touch. At a chemical synapse the action potential reaches the presynaptic terminal, voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open, and calcium triggers vesicles of neurotransmitter to fuse with the membrane. Molecules diffuse across the ~20 nm synaptic cleft and bind postsynaptic receptors, opening ion channels and producing a small graded potential — an EPSP (excitatory) or IPSP (inhibitory).

One vesicle release does not fire the next neuron. Summation does: many small EPSPs arriving close in time add. The demo shows vesicle depletion and refill, transmitter cloud in the cleft, and the postsynaptic voltage trace below. Press presynaptic spike repeatedly to see summation build an action potential in the receiving cell.

Step 4 · The circuitNetworks

Brains are not single neurons — they are graphs. A spike in one cell weighted by synaptic strength contributes to the input of another. Some connections are excitatory (glutamate → depolarization), others inhibitory (GABA → hyperpolarization). Inhibition shapes timing: it can prevent runaway excitation or create precise windows for firing.

Aside · Hodgkin and Huxley, 1952

The membrane demo uses simplified Hodgkin–Huxley kinetics — the equations Alan Hodgkin and Andrew Huxley fitted to the giant squid axon, for which they shared the Nobel Prize in 1963. Their model is still the textbook account of how voltage-gated channels produce the spike shape.

What this leaves out

Real synapses also have short-term plasticity, neuromodulators, glial regulation, dendritic computation, and thousands of channel types. But the skeleton is always the same: graded potentials integrate input, threshold converts to an all-or-none spike, propagation carries it down the axon, and chemical transmission passes it to the next cell. Every sensation you have ever had rode that pipeline.
