The Tandem Tree

Why the most interesting solar cell today is not a replacement for silicon, but a very thin, very picky crystal grown on top of it.

The first version of this page was a lab instrument: particles, layers, sliders, and not much argument. That was the wrong center of gravity. A perovskite/silicon tandem cell is interesting because it is not one invention. It is a convergence: semiconductor band theory, fifty years of silicon interface engineering, solution-processed thin films, molecular passivation, and an industrial wager that the silicon module does not need to be thrown away to get better.

The short version is simple. Silicon is excellent but spectrally wasteful. A single bandgap cannot use the whole sun efficiently. A tandem cell stacks two absorbers with different bandgaps: the top cell takes high-energy photons, the bottom cell takes the red and infrared photons that pass through. Perovskites became the obvious top-cell candidate because their bandgap can be tuned by chemistry and their films can be only hundreds of nanometers thick.

The old problemOne bandgap sees one slice of the sun

A photovoltaic absorber is a gate. Photons below its bandgap do not have enough energy to free an electron and hole. Photons above the bandgap can be absorbed, but the excess energy rapidly becomes heat. This is the core tradeoff behind the Shockley-Queisser limit: choose a low bandgap and you catch more photons but waste more heat; choose a high bandgap and you waste less heat but miss the redder photons.

Silicon's bandgap is about 1.12 eV, which is a strong compromise for a single-junction cell. It is not a perfect answer. Blue and green photons arrive with more energy than silicon needs, and that excess is thermalized. Tandems attack that waste by making the first absorber a better match for high-energy light, then letting silicon do what it already does well underneath.

The useful mental model

A tandem is not "twice the solar panel." It is a spectral sorting machine. The top cell should absorb enough blue-green light to make the same current as the silicon bottom cell makes from transmitted red-infrared light. In a series-connected tandem, the weaker current limits the whole stack.

Why perovskiteThe top cell can be tuned by composition

"Perovskite" names a crystal structure rather than one exact material. The lead-halide perovskites used in high-performance tandems are compositionally flexible: formamidinium, cesium, and rubidium can share the A-site; iodide and bromide share the halide site. Raise the bromide fraction and the bandgap rises. Lower it and the top cell absorbs more of the visible spectrum.

That chemical freedom is why formulas such as FA0.78Cs0.17Rb0.05Pb(I0.65Br0.35)3 show up in papers. The formula is not ornament. It is the control surface. Bromide tunes the bandgap toward the 1.7-1.75 eV region that pairs well with silicon, but too much bromide can increase phase segregation risk. The best recipe is therefore not the absorber with the most impressive isolated absorption. It is the absorber that makes the two subcells agree.

This is also why the supporting layers matter. Transparent conductive oxides collect charge without blocking light. Self-assembled monolayers act as hole-selective interfaces. C60 and SnO2 extract electrons. Passivation molecules reduce defects where carriers would otherwise recombine. The stack succeeds when every interface is slightly less hostile to the carrier that needs to cross it.

The silicon inheritanceTandems stand on industrial wafers

Perovskites did not make silicon obsolete. They made silicon more valuable. The bottom cell in a tandem can be a high-quality HJT or TOPCon-style silicon device with excellent passivated contacts. That matters because the tandem inherits the voltage, current collection, module packaging, and manufacturing discipline of a huge existing industry.

The practical question is therefore less romantic than "what is the future material?" It is: can a thin perovskite process be added on top of silicon wafers without wrecking yield, lifetime, cost, or module reliability? Laboratory cells in the mid-30% range show why the attempt is worth making. Fielded modules have to survive heat, moisture, ultraviolet light, electrical bias, and imperfect encapsulation for years.

Deep dive: why passivation keeps appearing

Photovoltaics is a surface problem disguised as a bulk-material problem. Absorbers create electron-hole pairs in the volume, but useful power is lost when those carriers hit dangling bonds, vacancies, rough contacts, or chemically unstable interfaces before they are collected. Silicon learned this slowly through oxides, texturing, PERC, HJT, and TOPCon. Perovskites are learning it faster because nearly every layer in the stack is an interface.

Passivation does not make the material magical. It removes traps from the carrier's path. That is why molecules such as melamine or pyridine/imidazolium-style additives matter: they are small chemical patches at places where the electrical model would otherwise leak.

Where the cutting edge isFrom record cells to products

The record-efficiency race is real, but it is not the whole story. Certified perovskite/silicon tandem cells have moved beyond 30% and into the mid-30% range. That is far above ordinary commercial silicon modules, and it is close enough to theoretical promise that investors and manufacturers have to pay attention.

The hard part is that a record cell is small, watched carefully, and optimized for a measurement. A product is large, repeated thousands of times, laminated, shipped, heated, cooled, shaded, biased, and expected to keep working. The cutting edge is therefore half physics and half manufacturing: wider bandgap stability, textured silicon compatibility, low-temperature processing, lead containment, scalable deposition, encapsulation, and bankable lifetime data.

That is why the technology tree above ends at "factories begin," not "problem solved." The exciting claim is not that perovskites already replaced silicon. The exciting claim is subtler: the silicon solar panel may have a second act, and it is made from a film thin enough to look like chemistry but precise enough to behave like a semiconductor.
