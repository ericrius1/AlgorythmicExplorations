The Crown

The field over this pool is uniform, so it produces no net force. The spikes form because every particle is magnetized and interacts with its neighbors. The cursor adds a separate movable magnet.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part one used a moving magnet to grow one or two spikes at a time. It could not produce the Rosensweig crown, in which a whole surface forms evenly spaced spikes, because its fluid particles were not magnetized. In real ferrofluid, the grains align with an applied field and generate fields of their own. The interactions among those magnetized grains produce the crown.

This article adds those interactions, following Shao, Huang & Michels' "A Current Loop Model for the Fast Simulation of Ferrofluids" (2022). The paper contributes two things this page needs: a way to compute every particle's magnetic moment, and a pair force that points into the fluid at the surface, where the classical Kelvin force points outward. The difference is demonstrated below by switching between the two force models.

Aside · Development of the method

This method follows a sequence of papers from one group at KAUST. In 2019, Huang, Hädrich & Michels simulated ferrofluid spikes from first principles for the first time in graphics, using SPH particles with the classical Kelvin force. In 2020, Huang & Michels used a surface-only boundary-element method. The 2022 paper returns to particles and replaces the Kelvin force's outward surface force, allowing a faster simulation architecture.

Step 1 · Becoming a magnetThe moment problem

A ferrofluid's response to a field is summarized by one number, the susceptibility χ: place the material in a magnetic field H and it magnetizes to M = χH, the grains aligning in proportion to what they feel. Per particle, that means carrying a little arrow — a magnetic moment m — alongside position and velocity. The complication is that the field a particle feels is the applied field plus the field of every other particle's moment, and those moments are also unknown. Every particle's result depends on all the others.

That circularity is a linear system. In the paper's formulation, each particle's moment is proportional to the local flux density, 

m_i = V · χ/(1+χ) · B(r_i)/μ₀     // one unknown vector per particle
B(r_i) = external + Σ_j field(m_j)  // ...defined by all the others

and the field of a single particle is not the textbook point dipole — which blows up like 1/r³ at the origin — but a kernel-smoothed dipole: the field of a moment smeared over the particle's SPH blur. As elsewhere in SPH, the point source is replaced with a smooth kernel and integrated into a closed-form expression. Far away it is exactly the point dipole; up close it stays finite, and two overlapping particles feel a gentle, well-defined field instead of a singularity. No softening hack required — the smoothing is the physics of the blur.

That is the moment field, drawn one arrow per particle (every other one, for legibility) over the fluid body. Bring the cursor near and watch the arrows swing toward it and brighten — the cursor is itself just one more smoothed dipole, a big one. Notice the arrows do not simply point at the magnet: each one answers the sum of the external field and its neighbors' fields, which is why a column of aligned particles brightens its own interior. This creates a positive feedback loop, capped by the χ/(1+χ) factor, that produces the crown.

Deep dive: the smoothed dipole, and why an average appears

The derivation (from the 2019 paper, reused by this one) solves the magnetostatic Poisson equation ∇²φ = ∇·M for a single particle whose magnetization is the kernel W. The solution has a tidy form: the field at radius r depends on the kernel value W(r) and on W̄(r), the average of W over the ball of radius r. Outside the kernel's support the ball average becomes 3/(4πr³) — the whole blur looks like a point, and the formula collapses to the exact dipole. Inside, W̄ and W approach each other, and their difference (which drives the field's angular part) fades smoothly to zero. The demo implements W̄ for the cubic spline as a closed-form piecewise polynomial. Below r = h, the singular-looking terms in the force gradient cancel exactly.

Step 2 · Iterative solutionAn amortized moment solver

The paper solves the moment system with conjugate gradients each step. The 2019 work used least-squares conjugate gradient because the matrix was not known to be symmetric. The 2022 paper establishes that it is symmetric, allowing plain CG on the original system. Least-squares CG effectively solves the normal equations and squares the system's condition number, so plain CG converges in far fewer iterations.

This demo reduces the cost further. Instead of iterating to convergence, each frame applies one under-relaxed sweep of the fixed-point update — compute the field from current moments, blend each moment partway toward its implied value — and keeps last frame's moments as the starting guess. The result remains slightly behind the fully converged solution, but the crown takes dozens of frames to grow and the field changes only as fast as the fluid flows. A sixteen-millisecond delay in a process lasting many frames is not visually apparent. This spreads the solve across frames and reuses the previous frame's work.

Step 3 · The sign of the forceKelvin vs. current loop

After computing the moments, the simulation must compute their forces. Classical ferrohydrodynamics offers several force models that disagree in the bulk but, by a 1977 result of Byrne, produce the same pressure jump across the surface once their boundary terms are accounted for. In continuous theory, they produce the same equilibrium shapes. The 2019 paper chose the Kelvin force, μ₀(M·∇)H. The 2022 paper chooses the current loop model, which equals the Kelvin force plus one bulk term: 

F_CL = F_Kelvin + μ₀(∇M)·M    // same pressure jump, opposite surface sign

This equivalence does not make the models interchangeable in a discrete simulation. At the fluid surface the Kelvin force points outward: magnetization drops to zero across the interface, the field strength increases, and surface particles lift away. In the 2019 simulation, negative pressures in the weakly compressible solver pulled those particles back, but required a time step of 3·10⁻⁵ seconds. For aligned moments, the current loop's extra term attracts overlapping neighbors and changes the net surface force to point inward. This allows modern incompressible solvers such as DFSPH and IISPH to use time steps sixteen times larger. The force distribution changes numerical stability even though the continuous equilibrium is unchanged.

The button swaps the force model while keeping the moments, solver, and parameters fixed. On current loop, spikes form and hold; the near-field attraction between aligned neighbors keeps the tips coherent. Switch to Kelvin and watch the surface: particles at the tips lift away and the pressure solver repeatedly pulls them back. This 2D pool is less unstable than the paper's 3D comparison, but it shows the same outward surface force.

Aside · Equivalent continuous force models

J. V. Byrne's 1977 paper compared the classical magnetic stress formulations — Kelvin's, the current loop picture, and others — and showed that once each is paired with its own surface traction, they all predict the same observable: the pressure rise at a boundary. The models differ in how they distribute force between the bulk and surface. A particle discretization does not directly represent the compensating surface traction, only the bulk force density, so the choice affects the simulation's stability.

Deep dive: what the demo computes per pair

The paper evaluates the force between two overlapping particles by numerically integrating the smoothed force density over the kernel support, then bakes the results into piecewise quartic polynomials — three curves, tabulated to four intervals of normalized distance, looked up at runtime out to 4h and replaced by the closed-form dipole gradient beyond. This demo takes the collocation shortcut: it evaluates the smoothed-dipole gradient (the Kelvin term) and the bulk term at the particle centers instead of integrating. Far pairs use exact dipole–dipole forces, and the near field preserves the key distinction: under Kelvin, two aligned overlapping particles repel; add the bulk term and they attract. The forces are also equal and opposite by construction, which answers a momentum-conservation worry the 2019 paper had raised about current loop models.

Step 4 · AssemblyA self-organized crown

Set the external field to the same strength and direction everywhere. A uniform field has no gradient, so it exerts zero net force on anything; part one's entire mechanism is switched off. Spikes still form. The uniform field only magnetizes the particles; the forces that raise the spikes come from particle interactions. Vertically stacked aligned neighbors attract, so columns gather and sharpen. Adjacent columns — parallel dipoles side by side — repel, producing the regular spacing absent from part one. Gravity and surface tension set the scale through the capillary length; χ controls how strongly the magnetic feedback deforms the surface.

CodaDifferences from the full method

The full method is three-dimensional, runs on implicit incompressible solvers this 2D version does not need, integrates the pair force instead of sampling it, and simulates 32,000–93,000 particles with adaptive time steps — scenes like a magnet dragging a fluid bulge through an obstacle course, ferrofluid climbing a magnetized hemisphere, and droplets spiraling down a helix groove, each frame in seconds rather than the minutes the 2019 stabilizer demanded. The authors point at fast multipole methods for the long-range field sums (this demo truncates at 4h) and at FLIP-style hybrid solvers as a possible replacement. Part three takes the crown into three dimensions and compares three surface-extraction methods, including dual contouring for sharper spike tips.
