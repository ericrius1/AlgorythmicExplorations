Part one ended with a confession dressed as a feature: "with one moving magnet, this demo grows them one or two at a time." The magnet pulled, the fluid obeyed, and the famous Rosensweig crown — a whole surface erupting into evenly spaced spikes at once — never appeared, because the simulation was missing the property that defines the material. Each particle felt the magnet, but no particle was one. Real ferrofluid magnetizes: the grains align with the field, the aligned grains produce a field of their own, and the spikes are a negotiation among trillions of tiny magnets, not a tug-of-war against one big one.

This article adds that negotiation, following Shao, Huang & Michels' "A Current Loop Model for the Fast Simulation of Ferrofluids" (2022). The paper contributes two things this page needs: a way to compute every particle's magnetic moment (a linear system, solved here by a trick of patience), and a pair force whose crucial virtue is its sign — it points into the fluid at the surface, where the textbook alternative points out. That sign is the difference between a crown and a cloud of levitating spray, and the duel between the two models is staged live further down.

Aside · Three papers, one liquid

This is the third act of a research arc from one group at KAUST. In 2019, Huang, Hädrich & Michels simulated ferrofluid spikes from first principles for the first time in graphics — SPH particles with the classical Kelvin force. In 2020, Huang & Michels threw the particles away entirely and computed only the surface, by boundary elements. The 2022 paper returns to particles and fixes the 2019 model's one expensive flaw: the Kelvin force's outward push, which had quietly dictated the whole simulation's architecture — and its speed limit.

Step 1 · Becoming a magnetThe moment problem

A ferrofluid's response to a field is summarized by one number, the susceptibility χ: place the material in a magnetic field H and it magnetizes to M = χH, the grains aligning in proportion to what they feel. Per particle, that means carrying a little arrow — a magnetic moment m — alongside position and velocity. Computing it would be one multiplication, except for the catch that makes this section exist: the field a particle feels is the applied field plus the field of every other particle's moment, and those moments are what we're trying to compute. Everyone's answer depends on everyone else's answer.

That circularity is a linear system. In the paper's formulation, each particle's moment is proportional to the local flux density, 

m_i = V · χ/(1+χ) · B(r_i)/μ₀     // one unknown vector per particle
B(r_i) = external + Σ_j field(m_j)  // ...defined by all the others

and the field of a single particle is not the textbook point dipole — which blows up like 1/r³ at the origin — but a kernel-smoothed dipole: the field of a moment smeared over the particle's SPH blur. This is the same move SPH makes everywhere (replace the point with a blur, integrate, keep the closed form), applied to magnetostatics. Far away it is exactly the point dipole; up close it stays finite, and two overlapping particles feel a gentle, well-defined field instead of a singularity. No softening hack required — the smoothing is the physics of the blur.

That is the moment field, drawn one arrow per particle (every other one, for legibility) over the fluid body. Bring the cursor near and watch the arrows swing toward it and brighten — the cursor is itself just one more smoothed dipole, a big one. Notice the arrows do not simply point at the magnet: each one answers the sum of the external field and its neighbors' fields, which is why a column of aligned particles brightens its own interior — alignment begets field begets alignment. That feedback loop, capped by the χ/(1+χ) factor, is the engine the crown runs on.

Deep dive: the smoothed dipole, and why an average appears

The derivation (from the 2019 paper, reused by this one) solves the magnetostatic Poisson equation ∇²φ = ∇·M for a single particle whose magnetization is the kernel W. The solution has a tidy form: the field at radius r depends on the kernel value W(r) and on W̄(r), the average of W over the ball of radius r. Outside the kernel's support the ball average becomes 3/(4πr³) — the whole blur looks like a point, and the formula collapses to the exact dipole. Inside, W̄ and W approach each other, and their difference (which drives the field's angular part) fades smoothly to zero. The demo implements W̄ for the cubic spline as a closed-form piecewise polynomial; the singular-looking terms in the force gradient cancel exactly below r = h, which is the kind of small algebraic mercy you learn to be grateful for.

Step 2 · Solving without finishingAn amortized answer

The paper solves the moment system with conjugate gradients each step — and scores a quiet half-contribution on the way. The 2019 work had used least-squares conjugate gradient, the cautious choice when you don't know your matrix is symmetric. But it is symmetric, and the caution is expensive: least-squares CG effectively solves the normal equations, which squares the system's condition number. Plain CG on the original system converges in a fraction of the iterations. One line in a residual plot, years of compute saved.

This demo is lazier still, and the laziness is load-bearing. Instead of iterating to convergence, each frame applies one under-relaxed sweep of the fixed-point update — compute the field from current moments, blend each moment partway toward its implied value — and keeps last frame's moments as the starting guess. The solver never finishes; it is perpetually one step behind a target that moves slowly. But the crown takes dozens of frames to grow, the field changes only as fast as the fluid flows, and an answer that lags sixteen milliseconds behind a sixty-frame process is indistinguishable from the truth. The gravity series rebuilt its trees every frame because rebuilding parallelizes; this page extends the family motto: re-derive what you can, amortize what you can't.

Step 3 · The sign of the forceKelvin vs. current loop

Moments in hand, the force remains — and here lives the paper's title. Classical ferrohydrodynamics offers several force models that disagree in the bulk but, by a 1977 result of Byrne, produce the same pressure jump across the surface once their boundary terms are accounted for. Same equilibrium shapes, same physics, free choice. The 2019 paper chose the Kelvin force, μ₀(M·∇)H. The 2022 paper chooses the current loop model, which equals the Kelvin force plus one bulk term: 

F_CL = F_Kelvin + μ₀(∇M)·M    // same pressure jump, opposite surface sign

Why bother, if the equilibrium is identical? Because a discrete simulation is not an equilibrium. At the fluid surface the Kelvin force points outward — magnetization drops to zero across the interface, the field strength jumps up, and surface particles get pulled off the top. The 2019 simulation only survived this because its weakly compressible solver developed negative pressures that sucked the escapees back — a stabilizer with a vicious bill: the time step had to stay at 3·10⁻⁵ seconds. The current loop's extra term is, for aligned moments, an attraction between overlapping neighbors; it flips the net surface force inward, surface particles press onto the fluid instead of lifting off, and the model drops cleanly into modern incompressible solvers (DFSPH, IISPH) at time steps sixteen times larger. Same physics, opposite sign, an order of magnitude of speed.

The duel, live. The button swaps the force model and nothing else — same moments, same solver, same everything. On current loop, spikes form and hold; the near-field attraction between aligned neighbors keeps the tips coherent. Switch to Kelvin and watch the surface: particles at the tips lift away into a glittering haze that the pressure solver keeps having to recapture. (This 2D pool is gentler than the paper's 3D comparison, where the Kelvin run dissolves into noise — but the direction of failure is the same, and it is the sign of one term.)

Aside · An argument settled 45 years in advance

The theory underwriting the swap is older than computer graphics. J. V. Byrne's 1977 paper compared the classical magnetic stress formulations — Kelvin's, the current loop picture, and others — and showed that once each is paired with its own surface traction, they all predict the same observable: the pressure rise at a boundary. The models differ only in how they distribute force between bulk and surface, which physics doesn't care about. Forty-five years later, a GPU does: the discretization never sees the compensating surface traction, only the bulk force density — so the "equivalent" choice of where the force lives becomes the difference between a stable simulation and confetti.

Deep dive: what the demo computes per pair

The paper evaluates the force between two overlapping particles by numerically integrating the smoothed force density over the kernel support, then bakes the results into piecewise quartic polynomials — three curves, tabulated to four intervals of normalized distance, looked up at runtime out to 4h and replaced by the closed-form dipole gradient beyond. This demo takes the collocation shortcut: it evaluates the smoothed-dipole gradient (the Kelvin term) and the bulk term at the particle centers instead of integrating. The structure survives — far pairs feel exact dipole–dipole forces, and the near field keeps the property everything hinges on: under Kelvin, two aligned overlapping particles repel; add the bulk term and they attract. The forces are also equal and opposite by construction, which answers a momentum-conservation worry the 2019 paper had raised about current loop models.

Step 4 · AssemblyA crown from nowhere

Now the part that should feel slightly illegal. Set the external field perfectly uniform — same strength, same direction, everywhere. A uniform field has no gradient, so it exerts zero net force on anything; part one's entire mechanism is switched off. The pool erupts anyway. The uniform field's only job is to magnetize; every newton after that is particle-on-particle. Aligned neighbors stacked vertically attract, so columns gather and sharpen. Adjacent columns — parallel dipoles side by side — repel, so the spikes space themselves, the even pitch that part one could only describe in a parenthesis. Gravity and surface tension set the scale (the capillary length, as before); the magnetic feedback decides how hard the surface fights to break flat, with χ as the knob.

Everything from both articles, in one dish: SPH with surface tension, the per-frame moment solve, current-loop pair forces, and a surface re-meshed from scratch every frame by part one's marching squares — the renderer needed no changes at all, which was the point of building it field-first. Sweep χ low and the fluid forgets it is magnetic; sweep it high and the columns over-commit and merge. Raise tension to round the crown into lobes, drop it to shatter the spikes into spray. And the cursor still works — hold it near the surface and its dipole gradient drags fluid sideways through a standing crown, which is the moving-magnet experiment from the paper's figure 6, performed by you.

CodaWhat the paper does that this tab doesn't

The full method is three-dimensional, runs on implicit incompressible solvers this 2D toy doesn't need, integrates the pair force properly instead of sampling it, and simulates 32,000–93,000 particles with adaptive time steps — scenes like a magnet dragging a fluid bulge through an obstacle course, ferrofluid climbing a magnetized hemisphere, and droplets spiraling down a helix groove, each frame in seconds rather than the minutes the 2019 stabilizer demanded. The authors point at fast multipole methods for the long-range field sums (this demo simply truncates at 4h and accepts the small lie) and at FLIP-style hybrid solvers as the next backbone. Meanwhile this series still owes its surface a dimension: marching squares wants to become marching cubes, segments want to become triangles, and the spike tips that define this liquid will want dual contouring — same field, sharper knife. That debt is now paid: part three takes the crown into three dimensions and extracts it three different ways.
