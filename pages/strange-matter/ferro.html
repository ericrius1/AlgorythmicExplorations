The Ferrofluid

This ink-black magnetic liquid responds to your cursor. Hold it near the pool and the fluid climbs; the visible surface is reconstructed from the particles every frame.

Scroll down to read ↓

Ferrofluid is an oil containing magnetite grains about ten nanometers wide. Thermal motion keeps the grains suspended, allowing the liquid to respond to a magnet without separating. Hold a magnet underneath a dish of ferrofluid and the surface forms a regular crown of spikes — the Rosensweig instability, ferrofluid's most recognizable behavior.

This article builds one in a browser tab. The simulation reuses the particles, grid sort, and double-density relaxation from the water and lava lamp articles. It adds a magnetic force and a new rendering method: instead of thresholding a blurry field into pixels, it extracts the surface as line-segment geometry with marching squares on the GPU.

Aside · Ferrofluid's NASA origin

Ferrofluid was invented at NASA. In 1963, Steve Papell was working on a problem of weightlessness — rocket propellant floats away from the pump inlet in zero g — and proposed grinding magnetic particles into the fuel so a magnetic field could move it toward the pump inlet. The propellant idea lost out to other solutions, but ferrofluid later found uses in loudspeaker coolants, rotary seals, and display objects. Ronald Rosensweig, who developed the theory (and the word ferrohydrodynamics), explained the spikes the fluid grows in a field — and they now carry his name.

Step 1 · Three forcesWhy spikes form

The spike crown results from three competing forces:

Gravity favors a flat surface. Surface tension — the Akinci-style cohesion force the lava lamp added to keep its wax in one piece — penalizes additional interface area, so it also favors a flat surface and rounds sharp features. The magnet pulls in the opposite direction. A magnetizable fluid is pulled toward where the field is strongest — the force is the gradient of the field's energy density, F ∝ ∇|B|² — and for a dipole that gradient has two useful components: 

|B|² ∝ (3cos²θ + 1) / r⁶     // strongest on the dipole's axis
F = ∇|B|²                     // toward the magnet — and sideways, toward its axis

The radial part pulls fluid toward the magnet. The angular part is the source of the spike: off-axis fluid is pushed toward the axis, so instead of a broad mound the fluid gathers into a column aligned with the field. Surface tension limits the sharp tip, gravity limits the height, and the balance produces a spike of definite size. (In a real dish the balance repeats across the whole surface and the spikes space themselves out — their spacing is set by the same gravity-versus-tension balance, the capillary length. With one moving magnet, this demo grows them one or two at a time.)

The magnet adds six lines to the force kernel. Like the dome, tank walls, and the lamp's buoyancy, it is implemented as another force term; the rest of the SPH implementation is unchanged.

Step 2 · Geometry from a fieldMarching squares

The lava lamp rendered its wax by splatting particles into a blurry density field and shading every pixel past a threshold. That worked because wax is soft and the lamp is dim. A ferrofluid spike is the opposite case: glossy, ink-dark, with a silhouette that defines its appearance. For that, we need the surface itself — as geometry, not as a smoothstep.

The surface is the contour where the splat sum crosses the threshold. Marching squares extracts it. For every 2×2 cell of field texels, classify each corner as above or below the threshold. The sixteen possible patterns determine which cell edges the contour crosses, with each crossing interpolated from the corner values. Each cell contributes zero, one, or two line segments; together they form the complete surface.

That is the extracted mesh, alone — every cyan stroke is one segment from one cell, and the chain re-derives itself every frame as the fluid moves. On the GPU the extraction is one compute pass: each cell that finds a crossing appends its segments to a buffer with an atomic counter — the same wait-free append as the gravity series' scatters — and a one-thread pass converts the final count into the arguments of an indirect draw. The GPU allocates and draws the segments without a CPU readback.

Aside · The history of marching cubes

The 3D version — marching cubes, 256 corner cases instead of 16 — was published by Lorensen and Cline in 1987 and became the default way to pull surfaces out of CT scans, MRI volumes, and every implicit field since; by some counts it's among the most-cited papers in the field. It was also patented, which helped motivate the license-free marching tetrahedra alternative. The patent expired in 2005.

Deep dive: resolving the two saddle cases

Fourteen of the sixteen corner patterns are unambiguous. The two "saddle" cases — diagonal corners inside, the other diagonal outside — admit two readings: one blob pinching off, or two blobs barely touching. The cell's four corners genuinely cannot tell the difference; the demo breaks the tie by sampling the field once more at the cell's center. Watch a blob split in the mesh view and you are watching the selected topology change frame by frame. For a pinching ferrofluid neck, that tie-break determines the moment of separation.

Step 3 · AssemblyThe dish

Everything together: the SPH pool, the cursor-magnet, and the per-frame mesh — filled and shaded this time. The shading uses an approximation: the field's gradient stands in for a surface normal (dense interior = tall), which produces the glossy highlight that makes the liquid read as ferrofluid rather than tar. Drag the tension to zero and the spikes dissolve into spray; drag it high and the fluid forms rounded clumps and resists the magnet. Moderate tension produces the most realistic balance. The view button exposes each stage — final, mesh, the raw field with its threshold contour, and the particles underneath it all.

Worth noticing while you play: the mesh view's segment count (in the readout) holds roughly steady while the fluid is calm, then spikes when the surface grows spikes, because more detailed surfaces require more segments. Nothing retains the mesh between frames. Like the gravity series' trees, rebuilt from scratch every step because rebuilding parallelizes and updating doesn't, the surface is cheaper to re-derive than to maintain.

CodaPossible extensions

This article is the 2D proof of a pipeline: particles → field → geometry, every frame, all on the GPU. Several extensions follow directly. A second simulated phase could replace the lava lamp's prescribed buoyancy. Moving the meshing to three dimensions changes marching squares into marching cubes, segments become triangles, and the prefix-sum machinery from the gravity series can allocate them. Sharp features also benefit from a sharper extractor: marching cubes rounds off exactly the spike tips that define this fluid, which is the problem dual contouring exists to solve.

Part two extends the physics by allowing the particles to magnetize each other. Under a perfectly uniform field, with no magnet gradient, the resulting pair forces grow and space a full crown of spikes.
