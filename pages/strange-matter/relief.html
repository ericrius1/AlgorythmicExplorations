Sharp Relief

The crown, finally in the round. Twenty-six thousand particles magnetize each other in a dish; a quarter-million-voxel field is rebuilt from them every frame; and the surface you are looking at is re-extracted from that field every frame too — by dual contouring, the extractor that refuses to round off a spike. This series has been writing itself a promissory note for two articles straight. Part one built the pipeline — particles to field to geometry, every frame — and admitted in its coda that the geometry was line segments and the world was a flatland. Part two built the physics — mutual magnetization, the current-loop force — and grew a crown of spikes that space themselves, still in cross-section. Both articles ended pointing at the same door: marching squares wants to become marching cubes, segments want to become triangles, and the spike tips that define this liquid will want dual contouring. This article walks through the door, and it turns out the door is three doors.

Everything from part two survives the move intact — the same kernels, the same warm-started moment solver, the same pair force with the same inward-pointing sign. What's genuinely new is on the rendering side of the pipeline, where "extract the surface" stops being one obvious algorithm and becomes a choice with consequences. We will make the choice three times, each time differently, on the same liquid, and the third choice is the reason for the title.

Aside · What the dish owes the paper

The physics here is still Shao, Huang & Michels' current loop model (2022) — this is the dimension their paper actually lives in. Their comparison scene is a 6 × 6 × 1.5 cm box of 52,000 particles; their crown forms under a uniform vertical field of 1.3·10⁵ A/m, exactly the experiment the hero above is running in cartoon units. What they compute with offline care — exact pair-force integrals baked into polynomial tables, implicit incompressible pressure solves — this page approximates at sixty frames per second, and the coda keeps an honest ledger of the differences.

Step 1 · The dimension taxEverything times itself

Adding a dimension to a particle simulation is mostly bookkeeping, but the bookkeeping compounds. The sorting grid grows from 256² cells to 64³ — a quarter the cells, amusingly, but each particle's neighbor search now visits a 3 × 3 × 3 block of them, 27 cells instead of 9. The counting sort itself barely notices: histogram, prefix scan, scatter, the same three passes the series has used since the water article, except the scan now folds 1,024 block sums instead of 256. The particle, though, has gotten fat: position, velocity, magnetic moment, and the cached magnetic acceleration are each three components now, sixty-four bytes a particle, and all of it rides through the scatter together.

The real tax collector is the magnetic neighborhood. Part two truncated pair magnetics at four kernel radii, an 81-cell sweep. The same reach in three dimensions is 9³ = 729 cells, which is a budget, not a neighborhood. This article pulls the cutoff in to three radii — 343 cells, roughly nine hundred neighbors — and keeps it honest with a check: the near field that gathers fluid into columns lives inside two radii, and the first lobe of far-field repulsion that makes adjacent columns keep their distance peaks just beyond it. What the truncation discards is the long 1/r⁴ tail, which in a dish this size mostly cancels anyway. The paper, computing offline, simply sums every pair against every pair and points at fast multipole methods for anyone who minds; a browser tab minds.

The amortization trick from part two carries over unchanged and matters more here: the moment solve and the pair forces run once per frame, on the first of four substeps, and the resulting acceleration is cached on the particle and reused by the other three. Within a sixtieth of a second the particles barely move; the cache is honest, and it converts the most expensive passes in the simulation from a per-substep cost into a per-frame one.

Step 2 · Native dimensionThe apology, withdrawn

One pleasant surprise in the port: the magnetic mathematics required no porting at all. The smoothed dipole — the kernel W, its ball-average W̄, the gradient with the canceling singularities — was derived in three dimensions from the start, by Huang et al. in 2019, for exactly this use. Part two borrowed it into flatland with a small apology about slab approximations and tuning constants absorbing the difference. Here the apology is withdrawn. The moment a particle carries is a genuine 3-vector; the field it feels from a neighbor is the genuine solution of the magnetostatic Poisson equation for a blurred dipole; the crown that forms is the instability operating in its native geometry, free to choose spike positions on a surface rather than along a line. Watch the hero long enough through one slow breath of its field and you can see the choice being made: bumps appear, negotiate their spacing sideways — a freedom the 2D crown never had — and settle into the staggered, nearly hexagonal packing that laboratory dishes produce.

The uniform field still does none of the pulling, and it's worth repeating why, because in 3D the effect is even more striking: a uniform field has no gradient and exerts zero net force. Its only job is to magnetize. Every newton that raises a spike is particle-on-particle — head-to-tail attraction gathering columns, side-by-side repulsion spacing them — and gravity and surface tension close the books. The cursor magnet, when you bring it in, is the gradient: one more smoothed dipole, big and movable, and the fluid chases it across the dish.

Step 3 · A field with no pixelsSplatting a volume

Part one splatted particles into a texture by drawing little blurry quads and letting the blending hardware add them up. A volume has no blending hardware to lean on, so the splat becomes a compute pass: each particle walks the 5³ block of voxels inside its radius and adds its kernel weight to each. The field is 128³ — two million voxels, eight megabytes — and one frame's worth of fluid deposits about three million additions into it, concurrently, from thousands of threads.

Concurrent additions want atomics, and here WGSL has an opinion: there are no floating-point atomics. The workaround is older than GPUs — fixed-point arithmetic. Scale the kernel weight by 256, round to an integer, atomicAdd it into a 32-bit unsigned voxel, and divide the 256 back out at read time. The quantization error is a fraction of a percent of the surface threshold, far below anything the extractors can see, and integer atomic addition is associative — the one mathematical property floating-point addition famously lacks — so the field is not just race-free but deterministic, byte for byte, regardless of which thread arrives first. A small irony: the cheap trick is also the more correct one.

Step 4 · Triangles from a fieldThe table and the dodge

Now the choice. The field is a number at every voxel; the surface is where those numbers cross the threshold; the renderer wants triangles. The canonical answer since 1987 has been marching cubes: visit every 2×2×2 cell of voxels, classify its eight corners as above or below, and look the answer up. Two hundred fifty-six corner patterns, each mapping to a recipe of up to five triangles — a lookup table with four thousand and ninety-six entries, traditionally copy-pasted from one implementation to the next like a sacred text, because deriving it by hand is an afternoon of error-prone solid geometry and getting one entry wrong puts a hole in every mesh you ever extract.

Part one's history aside told the other half of this story: the algorithm was patented, the community invented marching tetrahedra largely to dodge the license, and the patent died in 2005. This article resurrects the dodge for a different reason. Split each cell into six tetrahedra around its main diagonal and the 256-case table evaporates: a tetrahedron has four corners, sixteen sign patterns, and every one of them is trivial — empty, full, one corner clipped (a triangle), or split down the middle (two triangles making a quad). Sixteen cases you can write by hand and verify by eye. The price is honesty in triangle count: the tets cut the cell finer than the surface needs, so the same fluid costs roughly twice the triangles of true marching cubes, with a faint diagonal grain in the mesh where the tetrahedra all lean the same way. The patent dodge returns as a table dodge.

The whole 3D pipeline, live: particles to volume to triangles, twice a frame's worth of work in one canvas. The view button drops to the raw particles (brightness is magnetization — the moment solve made visible, no arrows required in 3D, the glow says it). The extract button is this article's argument in physical form — cycle it and the same field grows three different skins. Stay on marching tets for now and look closely at a spike while the crown is up: the tip is there, but it is a little… polite. Rounded. Hold that thought; it is the next two sections.

Deep dive: six tetrahedra, one diagonal, and winding nobody checks

The six tetrahedra share the cell's main diagonal (corner 0 to corner 6), fanning around it like orange segments; every face between two tets is interior to the cell, and the faces on the cell's boundary tile each square face with two triangles whose shared edge agrees with the neighboring cell's decomposition — which is what makes the extracted surface watertight across cells. Crossing points interpolate along edges exactly as marching squares did, and normals come not from the triangle (flat, faceted, ugly) but from the field gradient interpolated to the crossing point — the same trick part one's glossy fill used, promoted from pixels to vertices. One more sloth-powered simplification: the triangles are emitted with no particular winding order, because the renderer doesn't cull and the fragment shader flips any normal that faces away from the eye. An hour of orientation bookkeeping, deleted by one if.

Step 5 · The dual ideaOne vertex per cell

Both marching algorithms share an assumption so basic it's easy to miss: vertices live on cell edges, and each cell is processed in splendid isolation. The dual family flips the assumption. Put one vertex inside each cell the surface passes through — positioned wherever best represents the surface in that cell — and then connect vertices of neighboring cells into faces. The mesh's connectivity stops being a 256-case riddle and becomes almost embarrassingly simple: every lattice edge that crosses the threshold is shared by exactly four cells, and those four cells' vertices form one quad. That's the entire algorithm. No table for the vertices, no table for the faces, watertight by construction.

The simplest member of this family is the surface net (Gibson, 1998 — invented for smoothing the staircase artifacts out of segmented medical scans, a noble first job for an algorithm). It places each cell's vertex at the mass point: the average of the cell's edge crossings. Averages are smooth, conservative, slightly shy — they pull every vertex a little toward the middle of its cell, which is to say surface nets round things. For a blobby liquid that's nearly free. For the one liquid whose entire visual identity is a field of needle tips, "slightly rounded everywhere" is exactly the artifact we came here to kill.

Step 6 · The sharp edgeDual contouring

Dual contouring (Ju, Losasso, Schaefer & Warren, 2002) keeps the surface net's connectivity and fixes its vertex placement with one more piece of information the field was offering all along: the gradient. At each edge crossing we know not just where the surface passes but which way it faces — position plus normal, a tangent plane, what the literature calls Hermite data. The right vertex position is the point that agrees with all of the cell's tangent planes at once; the paper finds it by minimizing a quadratic error function over the planes. Where the surface is flat, the planes agree everywhere and the answer is the mass point. Where the surface creases or spikes, the planes intersect, and their intersection is the feature itself — the vertex snaps to the tip, and the tip survives extraction.

The full QEF minimization is a small singular-value decomposition per cell, which is more linear algebra than a shader wants to host for two hundred thousand cells a frame. This demo uses the standard real-time substitute, due to Schmitz: start at the mass point and walk. Each iteration measures the vertex's signed distance to every tangent plane and steps along the sum of the corrections; ten iterations converge to within a fraction of a voxel of the true minimizer, and the step scale is exposed as the sharpness slider in the demo below. At zero the walk never starts and you have surface nets exactly. At one the vertex chases the planes' intersection into the corner of the cell. The slider is, quite literally, an interpolation between two published algorithms.

Aside · Why sharpness is a slider and not a switch

The QEF has a failure mode the original paper spends an appendix on: with noisy normals — and normals differenced from a field that 25,000 particles re-deposit every frame are noisy — the minimizer can sit outside its cell, producing self-intersections that flicker frame to frame. Clamping the vertex to its cell (this demo does) tames the worst of it, and backing the sharpness off from 1.0 trades a little tip acuity for temporal calm. Production implementations make the same trade with more ceremony, weighting the mass point into the QEF as a regularizer. Watch a single spike tip while sweeping the slider and you can see the whole trade-off space in about two seconds.

Step 7 · AssemblyThe dish

Everything, together, interrogable: the magnetized pool, the breathing of χ and field strength and tension from part two (now with a whole extra axis to be unstable in), the surface level (the threshold — slide it down and the skin inflates around the particles, slide it up and it shrink-wraps), the extractor menu, and the sharpness slider doing its two-algorithm interpolation. The triangle counter in the readout is worth a glance while you switch extractors: marching tets spends about three times the triangles of the dual meshes for the same fluid, and all of them breathe with the crown — more surface, more triangles, allocated by atomic counter and drawn by indirect draw, with the CPU never once hearing a number it would have to wait for.

Things worth trying, in ascending order of violence: hover the magnet just above a calm pool and lift a single tent of fluid; set χ high and tension low and watch the crown over-commit into a forest; drop the surface level until the skin barely contains the particles and the extractors disagree most visibly about what the tips look like; and switch to the particle view during a full crown to see the moment field glowing brightest up the spike columns — the 3D version of part two's arrows, the feedback loop caught mid-loop.

CodaThe ledger

What the paper still does that this dish doesn't: solve pressure implicitly (DFSPH/IISPH — the very capability the current-loop force's inward sign was invented to unlock), integrate the pair force over the kernel overlap instead of sampling it at centers, sum magnetics over every pair instead of three radii, and run to 93,000 particles with adaptive time steps — for seconds per frame, exported at 200 frames a second, not sixty frames per second in a browser tab. What the tab does that the paper doesn't ask for: the whole pipeline — sort, magnetize, force, splat, extract, shade — resident on one GPU with the CPU reduced to writing forty-eight bytes of uniforms a frame.

And the series' own ledger, three articles in: particles → field → geometry, now in any number of dimensions this site is likely to need; a magnetization solver that never finishes and never needs to; two force models and the sign that separates them; and four surface extractors — marching squares, marching tetrahedra, surface nets, dual contouring — each one a different answer to the same question of where, exactly, the liquid ends. The open road from here: a second simulated phase (the lava lamp's buoyancy, earned honestly this time), the paper's suggested FLIP hybrid as a faster backbone, and somewhere past that, the helix scene — droplets spiraling down a carved groove, which is the kind of sentence that gets a series a part four.
