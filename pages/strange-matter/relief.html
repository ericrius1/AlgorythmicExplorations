Sharp Relief

Twenty-six thousand particles magnetize each other in a dish. A volumetric field and a dual-contoured surface are reconstructed from them every frame, preserving the sharp spike tips. Drag to orbit. Hover to park a magnet over the pool.

Part one built a two-dimensional pipeline from particles to a field to line-segment geometry. Part two added mutual magnetization and current-loop forces, producing a self-spacing crown in cross-section. This article moves both the simulation and surface reconstruction into three dimensions, then compares marching tetrahedra, surface nets, and dual contouring.

The physics from part two carries over intact — the same kernels, the same warm-started moment solver, the same pair force with the same inward-pointing sign. What's genuinely new is on the rendering side of the pipeline. Three extraction algorithms applied to the same field produce different triangle counts, surface smoothness, and spike tips.

Aside · Reference simulation

The physics here is still Shao, Huang & Michels' current loop model (2022), now applied in the three dimensions used by the paper. Their comparison scene is a 6 × 6 × 1.5 cm box of 52,000 particles; their crown forms under a uniform vertical field of 1.3·10⁵ A/m, the same experiment the hero above runs in normalized units. What they compute with offline methods — exact pair-force integrals baked into polynomial tables, implicit incompressible pressure solves — this page approximates at sixty frames per second. The coda lists the main differences.

Step 1 · Scaling costsMoving to three dimensions

Adding a dimension changes the storage and neighborhood costs. The sorting grid changes from 256² cells to 64³, but each particle's neighbor search now visits a 3 × 3 × 3 block of them, 27 cells instead of 9. The counting sort still uses the same histogram, prefix-scan, and scatter passes as the water article, except the scan now folds 1,024 block sums instead of 256. Each particle also grows to sixty-four bytes: position, velocity, magnetic moment, and the cached magnetic acceleration are each three components now, and all of those fields are scattered together. 

let center = cellOf(p.position);
for (let dz = -cutoff; dz <= cutoff; dz++) {
  for (let dy = -cutoff; dy <= cutoff; dy++) {
    for (let dx = -cutoff; dx <= cutoff; dx++) {
      let c = center + vec3i(dx, dy, dz);
      for (let k = starts[c]; k < starts[c + 1]; k++) {
        accumulatePairForce(p, sortedParticles[k]);
      }
    }
  }
}
// cutoff = 1 → 27 hydro cells; cutoff = 3 → 343 magnetic cells

The largest cost is the magnetic neighborhood. Part two truncated pair magnetics at four kernel radii, an 81-cell sweep. The same reach in three dimensions is 9³ = 729 cells, which is a large search for every particle. This article reduces the cutoff to three radii — 343 cells, roughly nine hundred neighbors. The near field that gathers fluid into columns lives inside two radii, and the first lobe of far-field repulsion that makes adjacent columns keep their distance peaks just beyond it. What the truncation discards is the long 1/r⁴ tail, which in a dish this size mostly cancels. The paper's offline simulation sums every pair and suggests fast multipole methods as a faster alternative.

The amortized calculation from part two carries over unchanged and matters more here: the moment solve and the pair forces run once per frame, on the first of four substeps, and the resulting acceleration is cached on the particle and reused by the other three. Within a sixtieth of a second the particles barely move, so reusing the result converts the most expensive passes from a per-substep cost into a per-frame one with little visible error.

Step 2 · Native dimensionThree-dimensional magnetics

The magnetic equations require no changes in three dimensions. The smoothed dipole — the kernel W, its ball-average W̄, the gradient with the canceling singularities — was derived in three dimensions from the start, by Huang et al. in 2019, for exactly this use. Part two adapted it to two dimensions with a slab approximation and tuned constants. Here each particle carries a 3-vector moment, and the field from each neighbor uses the original three-dimensional solution of the magnetostatic Poisson equation for a blurred dipole. Spikes can form anywhere on the surface instead of only along a line, so they settle into the staggered, nearly hexagonal packing seen in laboratory dishes.

As in part two, the uniform field has no gradient and exerts zero net force; it only magnetizes the particles. Pairwise head-to-tail attraction gathers particles into columns, while side-by-side repulsion spaces the columns. Gravity and surface tension oppose those forces. The cursor adds a large, movable smoothed dipole whose gradient pulls the fluid across the dish.

Step 3 · A field with no pixelsSplatting a volume

Part one splatted particles into a texture by drawing little blurry quads and letting the blending hardware add them up. Three-dimensional textures have no equivalent blending operation, so the splat becomes a compute pass: each particle walks the 5³ block of voxels inside its radius and adds its kernel weight to each. The field is 128³ — two million voxels, eight megabytes — and one frame's worth of fluid deposits about three million additions into it, concurrently, from thousands of threads.

Concurrent additions require atomics, but WGSL has no floating-point atomics. The workaround is fixed-point arithmetic: scale the kernel weight by 256, round to an integer, atomicAdd it into a 32-bit unsigned voxel, and divide the 256 back out at read time. The quantization error is a fraction of a percent of the surface threshold, below the resolution relevant to the extractors. Integer atomic addition is associative — the one mathematical property floating-point addition famously lacks — so the field is not just race-free but deterministic, byte for byte, regardless of which thread arrives first. 

// WGSL: fixed-point density deposit into a 3D voxel buffer
let weight = kernel(length(voxelCenter - particle.position));
let q = u32(round(weight * 256.0));
atomicAdd(&field[voxelIndex], q);

// later, when extracting the surface
let density = f32(atomicLoad(&field[voxelIndex])) / 256.0;

Step 4 · Triangles from a fieldMarching tetrahedra

The field is a number at every voxel; the surface is where those numbers cross the threshold; the renderer wants triangles. The canonical answer since 1987 has been marching cubes: visit every 2×2×2 cell of voxels, classify its eight corners as above or below, and look the answer up. Two hundred fifty-six corner patterns, each mapping to a recipe of up to five triangles — a lookup table with four thousand and ninety-six entries. Deriving the table by hand is error-prone, and one incorrect entry can leave holes in every extracted mesh.

Marching cubes was patented, which helped motivate marching tetrahedra as a license-free alternative. The patent expired in 2005. Marching tetrahedra is useful here because it avoids the large lookup table. Split each cell into six tetrahedra around its main diagonal and the 256-case table is replaced by sixteen cases: a tetrahedron has four corners, and each sign pattern is empty, full, one corner clipped (a triangle), or split down the middle (two triangles making a quad). These sixteen cases can be written and verified directly. The tradeoff is triangle count: the tetrahedra divide each cell more finely than the surface needs, so the same fluid costs roughly twice the triangles of true marching cubes, with a faint diagonal pattern in the mesh from the shared tetrahedron orientation. 

for (let tet of cellTets) {
  let mask = classify4Corners(tet, surfaceLevel);
  let crossings = interpolateEdges(tet, field, surfaceLevel);
  emitTetCase(mask, crossings); // 0, 1, or 2 triangles
}

Deep dive: six tetrahedra, one diagonal, and triangle winding

The six tetrahedra share the cell's main diagonal (corner 0 to corner 6), fanning around it like orange segments; every face between two tets is interior to the cell, and the faces on the cell's boundary tile each square face with two triangles whose shared edge agrees with the neighboring cell's decomposition — which is what makes the extracted surface watertight across cells. Crossing points interpolate along edges exactly as marching squares did. Normals come from the field gradient interpolated to the crossing point rather than from the triangle, avoiding flat, faceted shading. This is the same method part one's glossy fill used, now applied at vertices. The triangles are emitted with no particular winding order. The renderer does not cull back faces, and the fragment shader flips any normal that faces away from the eye.

Step 5 · The dual ideaOne vertex per cell

Both marching algorithms share an assumption so basic it's easy to miss: vertices live on cell edges, and each cell is processed independently. The dual family flips the assumption. Put one vertex inside each cell the surface passes through — positioned wherever best represents the surface in that cell — and then connect vertices of neighboring cells into faces. The mesh's connectivity becomes simple: every lattice edge that crosses the threshold is shared by exactly four cells, and those four cells' vertices form one quad. No vertex or face lookup table is needed, and the result is watertight by construction.

The simplest member of this family is the surface net (Gibson, 1998), developed to smooth staircase artifacts in segmented medical scans. It places each cell's vertex at the mass point: the average of the cell's edge crossings. Averaging pulls every vertex toward the middle of its cell, so surface nets round sharp features. That works well for smooth liquid surfaces but softens the ferrofluid's spike tips.

Step 6 · The sharp edgeDual contouring

Dual contouring (Ju, Losasso, Schaefer & Warren, 2002) keeps the surface net's connectivity and fixes its vertex placement with one more piece of information available from the field: the gradient. At each edge crossing we know not just where the surface passes but which way it faces — position plus normal, a tangent plane, what the literature calls Hermite data. The Dual contouring positions the vertex to fit all of the cell's tangent planes at once; the paper finds it by minimizing a quadratic error function over the planes. Where the surface is flat, the planes agree everywhere and the answer is the mass point. Where the surface creases or spikes, the planes intersect, and their intersection is the feature itself — the vertex snaps to the tip, and the tip survives extraction.

The full QEF minimization is a small singular-value decomposition per cell, which is expensive to run in a shader for two hundred thousand cells a frame. This demo uses the standard real-time substitute, due to Schmitz: start at the mass point and walk. Each iteration measures the vertex's signed distance to every tangent plane and steps along the sum of the corrections; ten iterations converge to within a fraction of a voxel of the true minimizer, and the step scale is exposed as the sharpness slider in the demo below. At zero the walk never starts and you have surface nets exactly. At one the vertex moves toward the planes' intersection, potentially reaching a corner of the cell. The slider interpolates between surface nets and this iterative dual-contouring approximation. 

var x = massPoint;
for (var iter = 0u; iter < 10u; iter++) {
  var correction = vec3f(0.0);
  for (var i = 0u; i < planeCount; i++) {
    let plane = planes[i]; // position + gradient at an edge crossing
    correction += plane.normal * dot(plane.normal, plane.point - x);
  }
  x += sharpness * correction / f32(planeCount);
}
x = clamp(x, cellMin, cellMax);
Aside · Why sharpness is a slider and not a switch

The original paper discusses an important QEF failure mode: noisy normals can place the minimizer outside its cell, producing self-intersections that flicker between frames. This demo's normals are differenced from a field that 25,000 particles re-deposit every frame, so some noise is unavoidable. Clamping the vertex to its cell prevents the largest errors, and backing the sharpness off from 1.0 trades some tip sharpness for temporal stability. Production implementations make the same trade by weighting the mass point into the QEF as a regularizer. Watch a single spike tip while sweeping the slider to see the tradeoff directly.

Step 7 · AssemblyThe dish

The final demo combines the magnetized pool with controls for χ, field strength, tension, surface level, extraction method, and sharpness. Lowering the surface-level threshold expands the surface around the particles; raising it contracts the surface. The triangle counter in the readout is worth a glance while you switch extractors: marching tets spends about three times the triangles of the dual meshes for the same fluid. As the crown increases the surface area, every extractor emits more triangles, allocated by atomic counter and drawn indirectly without a CPU readback.

To compare the controls and extractors, hover the magnet just above a calm pool and lift a single column of fluid; set χ high and tension low to produce a dense crown; drop the surface level until the skin barely contains the particles and the extractors disagree most visibly about what the tips look like; and switch to the particle view during a full crown to see the moment field glowing brightest up the spike columns, the 3D equivalent of part two's arrows.

CodaLimitations and extensions

The paper's full method solves pressure implicitly with DFSPH or IISPH, integrates the pair force over the kernel overlap instead of sampling it at particle centers, sums magnetic forces over every pair instead of truncating at three radii, and runs up to 93,000 particles with adaptive time steps. It takes seconds to compute each frame and is exported as a 200-fps animation rather than running interactively. This browser version keeps the complete sort, magnetization, force, splat, extraction, and shading pipeline on one GPU; the CPU writes only forty-eight bytes of uniforms per frame.

Across three articles, the series has developed a particles-to-field-to- geometry pipeline; an amortized magnetization solver; two magnetic force models; and four surface extractors: marching squares, marching tetrahedra, surface nets, and dual contouring. Possible extensions include a second simulated phase, a faster FLIP-based fluid solver, and the paper's helix scene with droplets spiraling down a carved groove.
