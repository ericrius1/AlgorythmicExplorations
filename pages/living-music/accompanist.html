The Accompanist

The finale ended with a band that generates plausible music but cannot listen. This article turns the pipeline around. Play — the letter rows of your computer keyboard become a piano, or plug in a MIDI controller — and the browser estimates the current key, infers the implied chord, and generates a bass line and comping that adapt as the evidence changes.

Note that nothing here detects pitch from a microphone. Notes arrive as events — key down, key up, exact and timestamped — so the hard signal-processing problem of pitch detection does not apply. What's left is a musical inference problem: given a trickle of notes, in real time, decide what they mean. That decision splits into three questions a human accompanist answers without noticing: what key are we in, what chord is happening, and how much should I play?

Step 1 · Key estimationMatching pitch-class profiles

A key is not a fact in the signal; it is an impression that builds up and fades. The key estimator models it that way: a twelve-slot histogram, one slot per pitch class, where every note you play adds a vote and every vote decays exponentially — a memory with a half-life. Play C–E–G–C and the histogram bulges at C, E, and G; stop playing for ten seconds and the bulge melts back toward silence, the way your own sense of key loosens during a long rest. 

evidence[pc] × 2^(−Δt / T)      // memory fades — T ≈ 5 s for key, 1.2 s for chords
evidence[pc] += 1 on note-on    // every note is a vote
key = argmax over 24 candidates of corr(evidence, profile)

The histogram is compared against a remarkable pair of measurements. In 1982 Carol Krumhansl and Edward Kessler played listeners a key-establishing context, then a probe tone, and asked how well it fit; repeating this for all twelve probes yields a profile of the key — tonic rated highest, then the fifth, then the third, the out-of-key notes at the bottom. One profile for major, one for minor, each rotatable to any tonic: twenty-four candidate keys. Correlate the decaying histogram against all twenty-four and the best match is your key, with the correlation itself as a confidence score. It is the same move as part one's pitch inference one level up: don't measure the answer, score every hypothesis against the evidence and report the winner.

One musicianly refinement: hysteresis. A single chromatic note — a blues third, a borrowed chord — briefly makes some other key score better, and a naive tracker would lurch its whole worldview sideways for one bar. So a challenger key must beat the incumbent clearly, two consecutive bars before the active key changes. This hysteresis prevents brief chromatic notes from causing unstable key changes.

Step 2 · Chord inferenceChoosing the chord

Twice a bar, the system scores the key's seven diatonic triads (part five's stack-of-thirds) best explains the recent notes? Each triad is scored on the fast-decaying histogram — notes currently under your fingers count extra — plus three small nudges that turn statistics into taste: 

score(chord) = Σ evidence[chord tones]          // explain the player's notes
             + inertia                          // changing has to be worth it
             + bonus if root falls a fifth      // V→I, the strongest move in tonal music
             + prior favoring I, IV, V          // and shunning the diminished hand grenade

The winner still has to be voiced. A C major triad is a set of pitch classes, not a set of piano keys, and a real accompanist's hand stays where it was: from F major to C major you don't leap a fifth, you slide F→E and keep the C. The system tries all three inversions near middle C and keeps the one whose fingers travel the least total distance — the minimum-motion voice-leading rule from part five, now running live. The bass is even simpler and even more traditional: roots and fifths, on the strong beats, an octave and a half down.

Aside · A short history of machine bandmates

This problem has a forty-year lineage. Roger Dannenberg's 1984 score follower let a computer accompany a live trumpeter through a written piece, solving the alignment problem this page dodges by gridding time. Robert Rowe's Cypher and George Lewis's Voyager (built through the 1980s–90s) went the other way — free improvisation, with the software acting as an independent partner rather than a follower; Lewis, a trombonist, insisted his program be allowed to disagree with him. François Pachet's Continuator (2002) learned your style from your own phrases and answered in kind. The seesaw rule below — play busy, accompany sparse — is the main principle these systems share with every good human sideman.

Step 3 · RestraintChoosing when to play

Accompaniment also requires restraint. The system keeps a running count of your notes per second and uses density as a control signal: leave space and it fills the space — sustained pads with a gentle arpeggio on the eighths; play conversationally and it switches to off-beat comping stabs, jazz rhythm-section style; flurry up and down the keyboard and it thins to bare pads and root notes, because somebody in this band has to hold the floor down. You can override the seesaw and pin any one style, but auto adapts the arrangement by reducing its activity when the player becomes busier.

Step 4 · Real-time responseThe complete accompanist

Click the demo to arm the keys, then play your computer keyboard like a piano: A S D F G H J K are the white keys from middle C (the caps are printed on the on-screen piano), W E · T Y U the black keys between them, and Z/X shift octaves. A MIDI controller is wired up automatically the moment you plug it in — velocity and all — and the on-screen piano works on a phone. Play two or three notes and the band joins: your notes are the white trace, the generated response is amber, the gridlines are the key it currently believes in, and the chord changes are labeled as they happen. Try sitting on a sustained chord, then a fast run, and watch the readout switch styles under you.

Two limitations remain. The system keeps the time and you follow it — real accompaniment-following (rubato, your tempo, Dannenberg's problem) needs score alignment this page doesn't attempt; the groove toggle exists because playing to a pulse is the price of admission. And the harmony is greedy — it picks the best chord right now, with no memory of form, so it will never set up a deceptive cadence or save the IV for the chorus. Both are the series' oldest lesson wearing a new hat: the algorithm doesn't understand music; it encodes the assumptions supplied to it.

CodaFrom generation to response

Six parts built a path from air pressure to songcraft, and the finale generated music out of that theory. The encore completes the circuit: notes in, theory applied, notes out, fast enough to feel like a partner. Every piece was already on the shelf — part three's grid names the notes, part four's scales become Krumhansl's profiles with measured numbers attached, part five's triads and voice-leading search became the hands, and the jukebox's step clock keeps the time. The series ends as a band that needs one more player: you.
