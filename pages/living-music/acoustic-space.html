Rooms That Play Back

Most browser spatial audio treats space as a knob: pan left, pan right, maybe add a fixed reverb. Real rooms are not knobs. A wall removes high frequencies, leaks low frequencies, reflects part of the wave, and changes the path by which sound reaches you. A doorway matters. A water-filled volume matters. The same emitter should sound intimate, muffled, distant, or blooming depending on where you stand.

This article explains a probe-based acoustic system built for a browser scene. It is inspired by Steam Audio's idea of precomputed spatial information, but the graph is intentionally sparse: probes are placed only where routing changes, connections are partial, and each link stores just enough directionality to make corner paths convincing. The simulation does not synthesize the sound. It produces a small set of control signals: low/mid/high energy for the direct path, low/mid/high energy for the routed path, and a media label. The instrument listens to those signals.

I like this framing because it separates three jobs that are often tangled together. Geometry answers reachability questions. Acoustic materials answer color questions. The instrument answers taste questions. If the wall is between you and a source, the geometry should say so; it should not decide whether the result is alarming, meditative, metallic, or warm. That choice belongs to the voice that consumes the simulation.

The system therefore behaves less like a single effect and more like a tiny audio operating system. The room produces a set of slowly smoothed buses: direct low, direct mid, direct high, routed low, routed mid, routed high, plus a few descriptors such as path length and medium mixture. Synth voices, granular beds, convolution reverbs, and UI meters can all subscribe to the same buses without knowing how the room was queried. That is what makes it possible to keep the physics legible while changing the musical surface completely.

The sound design is intentionally gentle. Long sine drones, filtered breath noise, slow detune drift, and sparse bell partials leave enough room for the acoustics to speak without turning every occlusion event into a sharp filter edge. The goal is not to make the solver less expressive. The goal is to make the room audible as motion, warmth, and distance.

The signalWhat the simulation outputs

The audio engine wants a mix curve, not a physics paper. Every frame, each emitter asks the room a few questions: 

direct = primary rays from emitter to listener
path   = best sparse-probe route around occluders
media  = distance through participating volumes
bands  = { low, mid, high } energy after material and media loss

The important design choice is the three bands. A blocked source should not simply become quieter. Walls leak bass and kill brightness; water removes high frequencies faster than low ones; absorptive panels reduce reflected energy without necessarily sealing the room. Three bands are crude compared to a full spectrum, but they are expressive enough to make the room audible.

A single frame is deliberately small. The emitter does not request a new impulse response, allocate a filter graph, or rebuild a reverb. It asks for the current acoustic state between two moving points. The answer is a handful of floats that can be ramped over tens or hundreds of milliseconds. That ramping matters as much as the numbers themselves. Without it, a source crossing the edge of a wall would chatter between bright and muted, and the ear would hear the algorithm instead of the room. 

state = solveAcoustics(emitter, listener)
directBus.setTarget(state.directBands,  80ms)
routeBus.setTarget(state.routedBands, 180ms)
airBus.setTarget(state.mediaMix,      250ms)
instrument.renderFromBuses()

The direct bus is allowed to move quickly because it describes immediate audibility. The routed bus moves more slowly because reflected and corner-bent energy is perceptually less precise. This is not a cheat so much as a psychoacoustic contract: nearby line-of-sight sound should feel responsive, while the room's bloom can lag and breathe.

The small state vector also keeps debugging honest. When a path sounds wrong, the HUD can show whether the direct ray is blocked, which material was crossed, how much distance traveled through a medium, and which probes were chosen. You can inspect the acoustic cause before touching the synthesizer.

The direct ray in the interactive figure is the cheapest and most important test. If it is clear, the listener hears the source with distance rolloff and only mild air/media loss. If it crosses a surface, the material's transmittance curve becomes a color filter. A concrete wall and a glass slot are not just different meshes; they are different low/mid/high transfer functions.

In practice I cast more than one primary ray. A single segment is too binary for a moving first-person listener: one pixel of doorway can flip a source from hidden to visible. A tiny bundle aimed at the head, shoulders, and source radius gives a fractional visibility estimate. It is still cheap, but it produces the soft transitions that make the instrument feel continuous. The same bundle also gives a better answer for partially submerged or partially occluded sources.

The acoustic output is intentionally independent of camera rendering. The renderer can use any art direction it wants, but the acoustic world needs clean collision surfaces, medium volumes, and materials with stable names. In a larger scene I would treat the acoustic mesh as a simplified sibling of the visual mesh, not as a verbatim copy. The ear does not need every bevel, screw, and trim strip. It needs the surfaces that change audibility and timbre.

MaterialsAbsorb, transmit, scatter

Each surface carries an acoustic material. Rendering already needs a material; audio adds a second representation: 

material = {
  absorption:    { low, mid, high },   // energy removed from reflections
  transmittance: { low, mid, high },   // energy allowed through the surface
  scatter:       0..1                  // how directional the bounce remains
}

This is why sound outside a wall can still leak through. That leak is not a bug in collision. It is the wall's transmittance, mostly in the low band. The useful perceptual trick is that the leaked source changes color: less presence, less sparkle, more body. Your ear reads that color change as "behind a barrier" faster than it reads volume alone.

Absorption and transmittance are kept separate because they describe different stories. Absorption is energy that fails to return to the room after a bounce. Transmittance is energy that survives through a barrier. A thick curtain can absorb reflected highs while transmitting a surprising amount of low body. A pane of glass can transmit a hard, narrow band while reflecting a bright snap. A game-like "occlusion" slider would flatten all of those cases into the same muffled sound.

Scatter is the third knob because real surfaces do not all reflect like mirrors. A polished tile wall preserves directionality; a bookshelf or rippled water boundary smears it. In the sparse-probe graph, scatter widens the baked directional lobe so more outgoing paths remain plausible, but with reduced focus. Musically, high scatter tends to make a space feel wider and less localizable. Low scatter makes routes feel sharper, as if the sound is slipping through a specific doorway or reflecting from a hard corner.

Implementation note

The implementation keeps these values deliberately small: three bands and a few floats per surface. That makes the acoustic result easy to feed into Web Audio gain nodes and filters. If the renderer later grows a physically based material library, the acoustic material can still remain independent; visual roughness and acoustic scatter are related metaphors, not the same number.

One useful authoring workflow is to start with a small material palette: hard wall, soft wall, glass, curtain, water surface, and open portal. Those labels are enough to hear whether the system is behaving. After that, the values can be tuned by scene scale and musical taste. The numbers do not have to pretend to be laboratory measurements to be useful; they need to be consistent, directional, and perceptually distinct.

PathingWhy probes need direction

Routing sound around a corner looks like graph search, but a plain graph is not enough. If the sound arrives at a probe from the left and leaves forward, that may represent a plausible reflection. If it arrives from behind and leaves forward, the same edge pair may be impossible or very lossy. A probe is therefore more than a point. It stores directional compatibility: "energy coming from this region can leave toward that region with this strength."

A navigation graph answers the question "can an agent move from A to B?" An acoustic graph has to answer a stricter question: "can energy arriving from this direction plausibly leave in that direction after interacting with the nearby surfaces?" That directional dependency is why a naive corner route can sound wrong even when it is geometrically short. Sound is not a token teleporting between probe centers. It has a history.

The system bakes that compatibility with spherical Gaussian lobes. At runtime, Dijkstra only searches a sparse graph, but each turn on the path gets weighted by the baked lobe. The result is a route that behaves like reflected sound instead of like a video-game AI walking around a box.

During baking, each probe samples nearby surfaces and portals. For each incoming direction, it records a compact outgoing distribution: a center direction, a sharpness value, and per-band energy. You can think of it as a tiny directional impulse response without the full audio buffer. Runtime pathing only needs to ask whether the current turn lines up with that lobe. If it does, the path keeps energy. If it cuts across an impossible reflection angle, the path becomes expensive or dies.

This is the reason partial connections work. The graph does not need to connect every probe to every other probe, because the lobe already says which directions are meaningful. In an open hall, a few long links are enough. Around a doorway, a small cluster of probes captures the turn. Near a thick obstruction, links can exist on both sides without pretending there is a direct acoustic shortcut through the center.

Sparse probes are the practical win. A dense regular probe field is easy to reason about and expensive to traverse. A sparse field follows the shape of the space: probes cluster near corners, occluders, portals, and volume boundaries; empty open space needs only a few anchors. With partial links, the number of edges stays small, so route cost grows sub-linearly with the number of probes in the scene.

The placement rule is "put information where the answer changes." A huge empty warehouse does not need a checkerboard of acoustic samples. A single doorway in that warehouse deserves attention because crossing it changes visibility, reflection, and transmittance. The same logic applies to stairs, balconies, waterline boundaries, and soft partitions. This is a better fit for authored spaces than a uniform grid because it spends detail where a listener can actually hear the difference.

At runtime, the emitter and listener are temporarily attached to the graph by finding nearby visible probes. Then a normal shortest-path search runs, except edge cost includes distance, material loss, medium loss, and directional lobe compatibility. The winning path is not necessarily the shortest geometric route. It is the route with the most believable surviving energy.

The lobe diagram is the missing piece in many "sound around corners" sketches. If the outgoing ray points into the bright part of the lobe, the route keeps energy. If it points into the dark part, the route is technically connected but acoustically weak. That distinction lets a sparse graph feel continuous without letting every connected edge become an equally valid portal.

MediaAir and water are both part of the room

Participating media are handled as volumes. A ray or routed segment measures how much distance it spends inside each volume and applies a band attenuation curve. This is Beer-Lambert extinction in the simple form that matters for audio: 

energy.band *= exp(-volume.attenuation.band * distanceInside)

Air has mild high-frequency loss over distance. Water can be much more aggressive, especially in the high band, and the system does not require the volume to be a box. Any shape that can answer "how far did the segment travel inside me?" can participate.

Volumes are evaluated per segment, so they work for both direct rays and routed paths. A source can be in air, pass through a water pocket, leave through a doorway, and then reach a listener in air again. Each segment contributes its own extinction. The final band energy is the product of all those small losses, which makes mixed spaces feel gradual instead of switching between presets.

The same idea can describe fog, heavy cloth, glass, or sci-fi fields if the project wants them. They do not need to be physically literal. A volume is any region that colors sound as it crosses it. For a musical browser piece, that makes media a composition tool: entering water can soften bells, a warm mist can roll off presence, and a thin bright portal can let only a shimmer through.

The hard part is avoiding discontinuities at volume boundaries. The solver treats volume distance as a continuous measurement and smooths the resulting bus. When a listener hovers at the surface of the water, the sound should not click between two worlds. It should feel like dipping your ears below a line.

The instrumentMaking the room beautiful

The instrument is built to make the simulation beautiful rather than merely obvious. Slow sine and triangle drones, tiny detune drift, filtered breath noise, long reverb, and sparse bell tones give the acoustic system a soft body to shape. The room still controls the mix, but harsh partials are not waiting to punish every occlusion event.

The gentler voice is built from layers with different responsibilities. The low layer is body: slow sines near stable musical intervals, with tiny detune drift so the sound never locks into a sterile loop. The mid layer is presence: quiet tones and filtered breath that make the room feel occupied. The high layer is air: sparse triangle partials and bell tones, kept low enough that occlusion removes sparkle without creating a brittle edge. 

direct.low  -> warm body
direct.mid  -> presence
direct.high -> small amount of air, never a bright saw edge
path.*      -> slower attack, longer delay, wetter reverb

This is the central lesson: simulation output should be treated as a musical control signal. Correct physics can still sound bad if it drives an ugly instrument. A gentle instrument lets the listener hear the room as motion, color, and distance rather than as a harsh filter sweep.

This is also why the path bus is treated differently from the direct bus. The routed signal gets a softer attack, a little more reverb, and a wetter delay. It is not pretending to be a measured late reflection impulse response. It is taking the acoustic fact "energy arrived by a longer indirect route" and turning that fact into a musical gesture: less point, more halo.

If the same solver were driving footsteps, alarms, or voice chat, the synth would change again. Footsteps might use the low band for thumps and the high band for shoe texture. Voice chat would need speech intelligibility protection and more conservative filtering. A healing sound bath can be more willing to blur, sustain, and bloom. The solver stays general because the instrument stays replaceable.

Frame budgetWhy this fits in a browser

The frame loop has two modes. With pathing disabled, each simulated emitter casts primary audibility rays and evaluates material/media loss: constant time with respect to probe count. With pathing enabled, it connects the emitter and listener to nearby visible probes, searches the sparse graph, and evaluates the chosen route. The graph is intentionally smaller than a uniform probe mesh, and the path search touches only the useful neighborhood. 

without pathing: direct rays + band filters                  ≈ constant
with pathing:    visible probe anchors + sparse route search ≈ small graph cost

The important phrase is "with respect to probe count." Direct audibility still scales with the number of active simulated emitters, because each emitter needs its own answer. What the no-pathing mode avoids is paying for the global probe field. That makes it a good default for many sources: ambient beds, small props, or distant emitters can use primary occlusion only, while important foreground sources opt into routed energy.

This interactive cost diagram is not a profiler. It is a mental model for why the sparse graph stays manageable. Turning pathing off leaves only the direct rays and band output. Turning pathing on adds anchor selection and graph search, but the graph is partial: each probe sees a few useful neighbors instead of every possible neighbor. Raising the link budget improves route options and costs more, which is exactly the tradeoff an author should be able to tune.

In the browser implementation, the simulation cost is deliberately small compared with rendering. With pathing active, the route search lands around the low millisecond range on the example scene. Without pathing, direct audibility and band shaping stay near a tenth of a millisecond. Those numbers are scene-dependent, but the architecture matters more than the exact timing: local ray tests remain local, sparse pathing remains sparse, and Web Audio does the sample-rate work after receiving smoothed targets.

The output is tiny: six gain targets, one panner position, and a few labels for the HUD. Web Audio then handles interpolation, smoothing, HRTF panning, delays, and reverb. The renderer, the simulation, and the synth stay separate, which is what keeps the project debuggable.

Separation also keeps failure modes understandable. If a path is too bright, inspect material transmittance. If it bends through an impossible corner, inspect lobe compatibility. If it arrives too late or too wet, inspect the instrument's mapping from route length to reverb and delay. Each layer owns a different class of mistake.

DebuggingWhat to show while tuning

Acoustic systems need visual debug tools because the ear cannot tell you which part of the pipeline lied. I want overlays for primary rays, crossed surfaces, volume intersections, chosen probes, rejected probes, lobe energy at each turn, and final low/mid/high bands. The goal is not to make the screen busy during normal play. The goal is to let the author pause on a bad moment and see the complete causal chain.

A good tuning session usually starts with the direct path. Move the listener around a wall and watch the visibility fraction. It should change continuously near doorways and edges. Then enable material bars and make sure blocked paths lose brightness before they lose body. Only after that should pathing be tuned, because pathing can hide direct-ray problems by adding plausible indirect energy.

The final pass is musical rather than physical. Walk the listener in a loop and listen for fatigue. If a repeated route creates a whistle, lower the high band or widen the lobe. If every room feels like the same cave, reduce the wet path mapping and let direct energy speak. If a barrier feels fake, increase the color difference between transmitted low and transmitted high. The debug display finds the cause; the instrument decides whether the result is beautiful.

CodaThe room becomes part of the music

A spatial audio system is easy to describe as realism: make the sound behave like it would in the room. But the more useful framing is instrument design. The room is a performer. Materials shape timbre, media shape distance, probes shape routes, and the listener conducts by walking. Once the synthesis is gentle enough, the acoustic simulation stops feeling like an effect and starts feeling like a place that plays back.
