The Dome Show

Part two gave every dome pixel a source coordinate. A real room adds a physical question: which projector puts that pixel on the dome, and how does its image line up with the projector beside it?

The demo below is only about the visual system. The blue and orange dots are the projector camera positions: each physical projector is modeled as a camera looking at part of the dome. Their images overlap in the middle, where one fades down while the other fades up. The sparse grid is the calibration mesh that bends each projector image until rings and lines meet on the dome.

Step 1 · Place the viewsProjector cameras

In dome work, a "projector camera" is not the audience camera. It is a calibrated model of one physical projector: where the projector sits, what direction it points, and what cone of the dome it can cover. In a two-projector rig, each camera owns a large region plus a shared overlap region. 

projector position  -> where the hardware is mounted
projector view cone -> what part of the dome it reaches
coverage mask       -> which pixels belong to that projector

Drawing the camera positions first keeps the rest of the pipeline grounded. The warp mesh and blend mask are corrections applied to a specific physical view, not abstract effects floating over the dome.

Step 2 · Warp before blendCalibration meshes

A projector draws a rectangle. A dome is curved. The warp mesh is the editable function between those two facts. In a simple installation, each mesh vertex stores a source coordinate in the fisheye master and a destination coordinate on the projector output. Moving the vertex bends the sampling so the grid lands on the physical dome. 

projector output pixel
  -> triangle in calibration mesh
  -> interpolated source uv
  -> sample fisheye or cubemap master

Calibration starts with boring patterns: rings, meridians, labels, and crosshairs. Adjust geometry first. A soft edge blend cannot hide a warped line that misses its neighbor.

Step 3 · Blend without a scarOverlap, gamma, and black level

Adjacent projectors need overlap because no real edge is perfect. In the overlap, projector A fades down while projector B fades up. The sum should look like one projector, not a bright stripe or a dim valley. That is why the demo exposes edge gamma: the fade curve matters as much as the overlap width. 

final = colorA * weightA + colorB * weightB

// good overlap target
weightA + weightB ~= 1
colors match
black levels match

Black level is the detail beginners underestimate. Projectors cannot project darker than their own black, and two projectors in an overlap can make the "black" overlap brighter than the rest of the dome. Serious systems compensate with masks and projector settings, not only with the content file.

Step 4 · Match the picturesColor, focus, and test patterns

Geometry is only the first visible failure mode. Two projectors can agree on line position and still disagree on brightness, black level, focus, or white point. That is why calibration uses boring images before beautiful ones: rings, meridians, numbered zones, grayscale ramps, and solid fields. 

rings and meridians -> geometry and seam alignment
grayscale ramps     -> brightness, gamma, black level
solid color fields  -> white point and projector matching
small labels        -> focus and orientation

If a test grid does not line up, the content cannot fix it. If a flat gray field shows a vertical stripe in the overlap, the blend or black level is wrong. Visual calibration is mostly the discipline of making the plain patterns boring before judging the artwork.

Practical check

Calibrate in the same order the eye notices errors: line position first, then overlap brightness, then color. A soft blend cannot hide a bad mesh, and color correction cannot hide a bright overlap stripe.

EpilogueThe full loop

A reliable dome show is the same loop repeated every frame: 

render or load one seamless dome image
for each projector camera:
  sample the image through its calibrated warp mesh
  apply edge blend, black-level, and color correction
display, measure, adjust, repeat

The artistic ceiling is high, but the technical shape is manageable. Circles become directions. Directions become fisheye pixels. Fisheye pixels pass through calibrated projector meshes. The room only looks seamless when each projector camera, warp mesh, and overlap mask agrees about the same dome geometry.
