The Dome Show

Part two gave every dome pixel a source coordinate. A show adds the parts that make that map survive a room full of equipment: warp meshes, edge blends, color matching, timing, and inputs. The math is still there, but now it is inside a system.

The demo below compresses a two-projector dome rig into one diagram. The blue side and orange side overlap in the middle. The mesh lines stand for calibration warps. The timeline is the show clock. The sensor slider feeds the render state after smoothing, which is what a real depth camera, MIDI controller, phone, or microcontroller should do.

Step 1 · Warp before blendCalibration meshes

A projector draws a rectangle. A dome is curved. The warp mesh is the editable function between those two facts. In a simple installation, each mesh vertex stores a source coordinate in the fisheye master and a destination coordinate on the projector output. Moving the vertex bends the sampling so the grid lands on the physical dome. 

projector output pixel
  -> triangle in calibration mesh
  -> interpolated source uv
  -> sample fisheye or cubemap master

Calibration starts with boring patterns: rings, meridians, labels, and crosshairs. Adjust geometry first. A soft edge blend cannot hide a warped line that misses its neighbor.

Step 2 · Blend without a scarOverlap, gamma, and black level

Adjacent projectors need overlap because no real edge is perfect. In the overlap, projector A fades down while projector B fades up. The sum should look like one projector, not a bright stripe or a dim valley. That is why the demo exposes edge gamma: the fade curve matters as much as the overlap width. 

final = colorA * weightA + colorB * weightB

// good overlap target
weightA + weightB ~= 1
colors match
black levels match

Black level is the detail beginners underestimate. Projectors cannot project darker than their own black, and two projectors in an overlap can make the "black" overlap brighter than the rest of the dome. Serious systems compensate with masks and projector settings, not only with the content file.

Step 3 · Music is a clockBeats, cues, and render state

For a music-driven dome show, audio should not be an afterthought glued onto the visuals. Treat it as a clock and a control signal. The clock gives reliable phase: bars, beats, and cue points. Analysis gives slower envelopes: bass energy, onset strength, spectral centroid, or loudness. 

clock phase  -> repeatable motion, beat hits, cue changes
audio energy -> intensity, bloom, particle emission, color pressure
show cues    -> scene changes that happen on musical boundaries

The practical rule: clocks trigger structure; analysis modulates texture. If every scene change depends on raw microphone amplitude, the show will feel twitchy. If every visual detail is locked to a rigid timeline, the show will ignore the room. Use both.

Step 4 · Sensors are noisy instrumentsSmoothing, calibration, and latency

Sensors are noisy measurement devices. A depth camera loses hands, a phone jitters, a pressure mat drifts, and a microcontroller sends values at its own cadence. Put a smoothing layer between raw input and render state, then design the visuals to tolerate missing data. 

rawSensor     = readInput()
calibrated    = map(rawSensor, minSeen, maxSeen, 0, 1)
smoothed      += (calibrated - smoothed) * response
visualControl = clamp(smoothed, 0, 1)

Latency is part of the instrument. A slow, dreamy dome can tolerate 100 ms of smoothing. A drum-triggered flash cannot. Fast controls need prediction or less filtering; slow controls need enough damping that the audience sees intention instead of measurement noise.

Aside · OSC, MIDI, WebSocket, serial

The transport matters less than the contract. Name every control, define its range, decide whether it is momentary or continuous, and document its expected rate. A clean sensor.handHeight: 0..1 signal is more valuable than a clever protocol with unclear semantics.

EpilogueThe full loop

A reliable dome show is the same loop repeated every frame: 

read music clock and sensors
update smoothed show state
render one seamless master
sample it through each projector warp
apply edge blend and color correction
display, measure, adjust, repeat

The artistic ceiling is high, but the technical shape is manageable. Circles become directions. Directions become fisheye pixels. Fisheye pixels pass through calibrated projector meshes. Music and sensors do not bypass the math; they drive the state that the math displays.
