The Fisheye Map

Part one named points on a circle. A dome adds one more idea: each point on the image represents a direction in space. The center of the fisheye is the zenith above the viewer. The edge is the horizon. The angle around the image is the compass direction.

This is why dome renders usually start as a circular fisheye master. The master is not trying to look natural on a flat monitor. It is a lookup table for directions.

Step 1 · Direction firstFrom hemisphere to image

A point on the dome can be described by two angles: azimuth φ, the direction around the room, and zenith angle θ, the tilt away from straight up. For a 180 degree dome master, θ = 0 is the center and θ = 90° is the circular edge. 

// direction from dome angles, z is up
dir.x = sin(theta) * cos(phi)
dir.y = sin(theta) * sin(phi)
dir.z = cos(theta)

That vector is the real object. The fisheye image is just one way to pack it into pixels. Drag either blue point to move the direction, then change the projection button: the same dome direction lands at a different radius in the image. The azimuth stays the same; only the radial law changes.

Step 2 · Fisheye projection familiesDifferent lenses, different radii

A fisheye projection chooses a radius ρ for a given zenith angle. Equidistant fisheye is the cleanest mental model: equal angle steps make equal pixel steps. Other lenses preserve other properties, such as area or straightness near the center. 

equidistant   rho = theta / thetaMax
equisolid     rho = sin(theta/2) / sin(thetaMax/2)
orthographic  rho = sin(theta) / sin(thetaMax)
stereographic rho = tan(theta/2) / tan(thetaMax/2)

Content pipelines often use an equidistant 180 degree master because it is simple and predictable. Camera lenses, projector lenses, and mapping tools may use a different law internally. Do not guess. Name the projection, write the formula down, and test it with a grid before trusting the show file.

Step 3 · The projector asks backwardInverse mapping onto the dome

In a real room, the projector is not at the dome center, the lens has distortion, and the dome may not be a perfect hemisphere. The robust renderer still asks the same backward question per output pixel: 

projectorPixel -> projectorRay
projectorRay   -> intersection point on dome
dome point     -> normalized direction from dome center
direction      -> fisheye uv
fisheye uv     -> source color

Calibration is the process of making those arrows match the physical room. The math gives you the ideal answer. Control points, mesh warps, lens correction, and edge blend masks turn the ideal answer into a practical one.

Aside · Forward maps create holes

If you push source pixels forward onto the dome, some output pixels receive no source and others receive several. That is how cracks and sparkle appear. Inverse mapping avoids that class of error: every displayed pixel asks where it should sample from, exactly once.

Step 4 · Seams, poles, and gridsHow to debug a bad map

The fastest dome debugging texture is not beautiful. It is a grid with labeled azimuths, latitude rings, a clear center mark, and a visible seam. If the seam opens, the azimuth wrap is wrong. If rings bunch up, the fisheye projection law is wrong. If labels drift between projectors, the calibration mesh is wrong.

Deep dive: cubemaps are another master format

A fisheye image is not the only way to store directions. A cubemap stores the same sphere as six square faces. Real-time engines often render cubemaps first because cameras with 90 degree fields of view are easy, then convert the cubemap to a fisheye master or directly sample it in the projector warp shader. The invariant is still the direction vector.
