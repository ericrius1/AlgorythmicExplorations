The Bonfire

Part two: every ember above is a real light source, and there are thousands of them. The trees cast their shadows, the ground blushes with bounced fire, the sky is whatever the topmost cascade says it is. Stir the smoke with your cursor — or click ✋ in the last demo and feed the fire by hand.

Part one used one lamp in one room to introduce the distance field, cascade hierarchy, and merge. The bonfire above uses the same machinery without adding a rendering pass. It tests a case that is expensive for many other lighting techniques: what if everything is a light?

Step 1 · Fixed rendering costA thousand lights, one price

Recall the shape of the algorithm. Radiance cascades never ask "where are the lights?" — they ask every probe "what do you see?", marching rays against the distance field and reading emission wherever they land. The scene's light sources are just bright pixels. Nothing in the cost depends on how many there are: the work is fixed by the canvas resolution and the cascade count, full stop.

Forward rendering pays per light, per pixel — film and game lighting departments have spent forty years budgeting light counts. The gravity series spent six parts reducing an n² between bodies. Light transport has the same n² — every pixel wants light from every source — and radiance cascades dissolve it by changing the question: stop enumerating sources and sample the field they create. It is similar to the particle-mesh approach from part five.

Drag the slider from sixteen lights to eight thousand and watch the only number that matters: the frame rate, which does not care. Each spark is a genuine emitter and a genuine occluder — sparks shadow each other, visible when a dense cloud drifts behind a pillar. The wind is part six's curl noise, four lines of it, because divergence-free drift is still the cheapest way to make particles look alive.

Step 2 · The time loopBounce from the previous frame

Everything so far is direct light: source to surface, one hop. Real rooms are mostly lit by the second hop — light that hit a wall and left again, tinted. The textbook answer solves a matrix coupling every surface to every other. This demo instead reuses the previous frame. 

emission(x) = base_emission(x) + albedo(x) · fluence_last frame(x)
// every surface re-emits a tinted slice of the light it received a frame ago

That is the entire implementation — one texture read in the scene shader, sampling the cascade-0 output the previous frame already paid for. The first frame computes direct light. The second frame's surfaces glow with it, so its cascades deliver one-bounce light. The third delivers two-bounce. The infinite series converges geometrically (each bounce is dimmer by the albedo) and it converges in time, about a frame per bounce — which for light in a room means: instantly, as far as any human can tell.

Aside · Radiosity and the Cornell box

The textbook approach is radiosity, published in 1984 — the year Cornell built the box that every graphics paper since has photographed. It solves the full surface-to-surface coupling as a matrix. The time-loop trick above computes the same geometric series, one term per frame, for the cost of a texture read.

The crimson wall on the left and the teal wall on the right are not lights. Toggle the bounce off and the room turns literal: one warm disk, hard shadows, black walls. Toggle it on and carry the lamp toward a wall — the whole room leans crimson, the way a hallway does when sun hits a painted door. Crank the strength past 1 and you can even make the room unstable, a resonating light-cavity no real paint could sustain. Above 1.0, the slider deliberately violates energy conservation for art direction.

Step 3 · The biggest lightThe sky is a cascade

One light has been missing from this series, and it is the big one. Daylight is not a source in the scene — it is what the world looks like when rays escape the scene entirely. The cascade hierarchy has a precise slot for exactly that idea. When a top-cascade ray finishes its interval having hit nothing, part one merged it with darkness. But "what does a ray see when it leaves the world?" has a better answer: the environment. Replace the black with a function of direction — dark blue overhead, ember-orange at the horizon, a bright smear where the sun sits — and every probe, every pixel, every shadow now agrees about the sky, for free. 

if (top cascade ray escapes):
    radiance = sky(direction)   // the horizon is just the merge nobody claimed

This is not a hack bolted onto the algorithm; it is the algorithm's own semantics. The top cascade's intervals are the longest rays — light from very far away — and an environment map is precisely "light from infinitely far away, indexed by direction." Skylight, with soft sky-shadows under the trees and a warm band at the horizon, costs one function evaluated at the top level only.

Slide from night to day and watch the power balance invert. At night the bonfire owns every surface; the ground is fire-colored and the tree shadows point away from the flame. By noon the sun owns the scene, shadows snap parallel, and the fire's contribution shrinks to a warm smudge — exactly why campfires feel different at night, rendered by the same five cascades either way.

Step 4 · AssemblyFeed the fire

Everything at once, plus the part six controller: click ✋, let the camera find your hand, and use it to control the fire. Your palm stirs the wind that carries the embers; your index fingertip emits them directly. Every new ember is a light, illuminating the trees, the ground, the smoke of other embers — and costing the renderer nothing it wasn't already paying.

The debug views tell the same story as part one, with better scenery. Scene is a few silhouettes and a swarm of bright dots — the entire input. Distance field shows the jump flood rippling as embers fly through it, a thousand moving holes in space. Light only shows the transported-light result directly: turn it on at night and everything you see arrived at its pixel from somewhere else.

CodaWhat the question costs

This part adds a thousand lights, repeated bounce, and an environment map with three small edits, none of which touch the cascade march, merge, or distance field. The expensive part of the renderer does not depend on how many light sources the scene contains.

What it cannot do yet is also visible above, if you look for it. The smoke doesn't scatter — rays pass through empty space untouched, so there are no god rays slanting through the trees, no glow trapped in haze. Light in this world is binary: it travels free or it stops dead. The next part is about the in-between: participating media, with fog that absorbs and scatters light.
