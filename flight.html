Flight

Three articles built a bird that could flap. This one adds aerodynamic forces. Their integration produces the repeated flight path above without a scripted trajectory.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part three gave the eagle a visual wingbeat, but the air in that article was decorative — the wing pushed against nothing, so the flapping was a looping animation that left her hanging exactly where she started. This article makes the bird a point mass acted on by air — four and a third kilograms of point mass, on more than half a square meter of wing — and her motion follows from four forces, summed and integrated, frame after frame.

The model uses four forces. Weight pulls straight down, always. Lift results from moving through air at an angle — perpendicular to the flight path, and the thing that holds her up. Drag opposes motion. And thrust comes from the twisted downstroke from part three. Adding them, dividing by mass, and integrating produces the flight path without scripting it.

Aside · Why not just keyframe it?

Because a keyframed bird can only do what it was animated to do. A simulated one does what the situation demands — it speeds up in a dive because gravity says so, sinks when it slows because lift fell off with the square of speed, and recovers when it flaps because thrust restored the speed. None of those behaviors are authored; they emerge from the force model. One force balance produces a range of flight behavior, and you can later hand the controls to a player or an AI and the bird stays a bird, because the physics, not a clip, is in charge.

Step 1 · The balanceFour forces

Start static, so the forces hold still long enough to see. A wing of area A moving at speed v through air of density ρ has a dynamic pressure ½ρv², and two coefficients determine the resulting forces: the lift coefficient CL and drag coefficient CD. Lift grows with the wing's angle of attack — the tilt between the wing and the oncoming air — until, abruptly, it doesn't: 

L = ½ ρ v² A · C_L(α)      C_L = C_L0 + slope·α   // until the wing stalls
D = ½ ρ v² A · C_D         C_D = C_D0 + k·C_L²     // induced drag: the price of lift

Tip her out of balance and watch the white net arrow appear: in trim it vanishes, because lift cancels weight and thrust cancels drag. Raise the angle of attack and the green lift arrow grows — until you cross the stall, where CL collapses instead of climbing and the wing simply lets go of the air. That cliff is real aerodynamics, not a safety rail: a wing tilted too steeply stops working all at once, which is why a landing bird flares to the edge of it and not past. The speed slider demonstrates the v² term: double the speed and every aerodynamic force quadruples. The numbers are an eagle's now — her weight arrow is forty-two newtons, and the speed that balances it sits near thirteen meters per second, which is why everything below moves with more momentum than the songbird drafts of this series ever did.

Step 2 · The glideGliding dynamics

Cut the engine. With no flapping, a bird is a glider, and a glider is optimized for one task: converting altitude into distance at a fixed exchange rate. That rate — the glide ratio — is just lift divided by drag, and it's why the angle of attack matters so much. Too shallow and lift is weak; too steep and induced drag (that k·CL² term, the unavoidable cost of bending air downward) eats the gain. Somewhere between sits the angle that flies farthest per meter dropped.

Release her and the orange trail draws the descent; the readout keeps the running glide ratio. Hunt the angle of attack slider for the flattest trail — that's her best-glide angle, found by the same physics a paper airplane obeys. Push past it toward stall and the trail steepens; pull below it and the bird dives. No part of this path was drawn. It's lift and drag and gravity, resolved sixty times a second.

Deep dive: integrating without blowing up

The motion is F = ma, but the force depends on the velocity (via v² and the angle of attack), so it's a differential equation, not a formula. We integrate it the cheap-and-stable way: semi-implicit Euler — update the velocity from the current force, then update the position from the new velocity. That one-line reordering (new velocity, not old) is what keeps a fast, springy system from spiralling to infinity, and it's the same integrator the n-body and fluid series leaned on. When a frame runs long or the bird runs fast, we split it into a couple of substeps so the force never extrapolates too far on stale data. Cheap insurance; flight is stiff near stall.

Step 3 · The turnBanking turns

Here is the most satisfying thing in the article, and it costs one line. To turn, a bird does not yaw like a car. It banks — rolls so its wings tilt — and the lift vector, which was holding it straight up, now leans. The vertical slice of that leaned lift still fights gravity; the new horizontal slice points sideways, into the turn, and pulls the flight path into a curve. Roll angle in, circle out. We never compute a turn rate or a heading; we just roll the lift vector and let the integrator produce the circular path.

Drag the bank slider and the trail coils tighter as the lean steepens — exactly the geometry, because a bigger bank gives a bigger sideways slice. Bank the other way and she circles the other way. This is why banking turns look convincing in good flight games and so wrong in bad ones: the right ones turn because they bank, and here you can see there's nothing else going on. Steepen the bank toward vertical and she has to flap harder to hold height because the vertical lift component shrinks.

Step 4 · Free flightThe flight controller

Put it together and let her go. The wings flap (part three), the flap makes thrust (step one), the forces integrate (step two), and a small autopilot closes the loop: it reads the gap between her altitude and a target, and from that error alone commands her angle of attack and flap effort — climb when low, ease off when high. A lazy wander rolls her into slow banking turns. None of it is a path; it's a controller flying a physics model, which is precisely what part six will hand to you.

The camera chases from behind and above, so the world streams past and the flight reads as flight. Raise the cruise altitude and watch the autopilot pitch up and pour on flap to climb, then settle; turn up wander and she roams. She is, for the first time, going somewhere — though "somewhere" is still a bare plane with a scatter of posts. The remaining articles add landing and terrain.
