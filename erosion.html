The Rain

The mountains under this camera are being rained on right now. Each drop rolls downhill, eats what it can carry, and drops it where it slows — and by the time the storm passes, the noise from part one will have gullies, fans, and flat-floored valleys it never had before. Noise plus history.

Part one ended with a confession: its mountains had never seen rain. Fractal noise gets the silhouette of terrain right — detail at every scale — but real landscapes are not just statistics. They are records. Every valley on Earth is the memory of water that passed through it, and noise has no memory at all. You can see the difference instantly even if you can't name it: fBm valleys wander aimlessly; real valleys connect, joining each other downstream in tree-shaped networks, because water that finds a channel makes that channel better at being found.

So this article adds the missing ingredient: history, simulated. The tool is hydraulic erosion — not the full fluid dynamics of a watershed (that's a supercomputer's afternoon) but a beautifully blunt approximation: one raindrop at a time, simulated as a particle rolling over the heightfield, allowed to do only two things — pick up dirt and put it down. Run a few hundred thousand of them and geology comes out.

Step 1 · One dropletThe life of a raindrop

A droplet is five numbers: a position on the map, a direction, a speed, an amount of water, and a load of sediment. Its life is a loop: 

dir    = dir · inertia − gradient · (1 − inertia)   // downhill, with momentum
move one cell along dir
speed  = √(speed² + drop · gravity)                 // falling makes it faster
water *= (1 − evaporation)                          // shrinking makes it weaker

The inertia blend is doing quiet but important work. A droplet that follows the gradient exactly turns on a dime at every cell and gets trapped by every pebble-sized dimple in the noise. A droplet with momentum cuts smooth curves and can coast across small flats — which is how real water behaves, and it is the difference between drainage that meanders like a river and drainage that jitters like static. The height and gradient under the droplet come from bilinear interpolation, so the droplet feels a continuous landscape even though we only stored a grid.

Rain on the map and watch the paths. Nobody routed them — each droplet only ever sees the slope under its own feet — but they merge into the same channels, because converging is what downhill means. This is the whole phenomenon of drainage in one picture: rivers are not drawn on landscapes, they are computed by them. Crank the inertia slider and the streams swing wide like rivers in flood; set it to zero and they hug every wrinkle.

Step 2 · SedimentThe carrying capacity

Tracing paths is hydrology; changing the ground is erosion. The rule that turns one into the other is carrying capacity — how much sediment a droplet can hold: 

capacity = slope · speed · water · C
if sediment > capacity:  deposit the surplus    // slow, flat, or drying up → drop it
else:                    erode toward capacity  // fast and hungry → dig

Everything geological follows from the product. On a steep face the droplet is fast, capacity is high, and it strips material. When it reaches the valley floor, slope collapses, capacity collapses with it, and the droplet sheds its load — which is why mountain streams run over bare rock while valley floors fill with soft flat alluvium. The simulation doesn't know any of those words. It knows one multiplication, and the words emerge.

Two guardrails keep the digging honest. A droplet never erodes more than the height it is about to descend — otherwise it digs a spike pit below its own path and the terrain fills with needle holes. And it never digs at a single point: the bite is spread over a soft brush a few cells wide, because a real stream undercuts its banks and pulls the walls down after it. Skip the brush and you get channels exactly one cell wide — drainage drawn with a needle instead of carved by water.

Press rain and let the storm run — each second of it is thousands of complete droplet lifetimes. Flip before/after to see what the water did to the raw fBm: ridgelines survive nearly untouched (droplets are born slow and starving up there), slopes grow rills and gullies that join downstream, and the valley floors — watch them with sediment paint on — flatten under pale fans of deposited silt. That last one matters beyond scenery: part one faked flat valleys with a power curve; the rain produces them for the honest reason.

Aside · Rounded by forty years

Eroding fractal terrain is almost as old as fractal terrain. Musgrave, Kolb and Mace's 1989 paper — the same one that gave part one its ridges — already ran hydraulic and thermal erosion over noise, on hardware with less memory than this paragraph. Thermal erosion is the lesser-known sibling: no water at all, just "scree slides downhill until slopes are stable," which rounds everything toward a desert look. Hydraulic carves, thermal relaxes; landscapes you believe tend to need a little of both.

Deep dive: the droplet as a slot machine

Each droplet is randomized only at birth — where it lands. From then on it is fully deterministic, and yet the aggregate looks richly stochastic. That's because the heightfield itself is the random number generator: tiny differences in landing position route droplets into entirely different watersheds. It also means the simulation is reproducible — same seed, same storm, same canyon — which keeps faith with this series' founding rule that every landscape must be able to come back exactly as it was. The droplets come from the same hash as the mountains they erode.

The expensive part is not the math per step (a bilinear sample and a multiply) but the brush — a 5×5-ish stamp on every eroding step. The budget arithmetic: 100,000 droplets × ~60 steps × ~20 brush cells ≈ 10⁸ memory touches, which is why the hero storm takes a few seconds to pass rather than a few frames. GPU implementations run the droplets in parallel and pay instead in collisions — two droplets writing one cell — which mostly averages out, because erosion is already a statistical story.

Step 3 · The ledgerWhat the rain bought

It's worth being precise about what changed, because the next articles will build on each item. Drainage networks: valleys now join downstream instead of wandering — the single strongest "this is real terrain" cue there is. Flat floors: deposition fills the low ground, making the only terrain flat enough to walk on, build on, or — looking ahead — grow a meadow on. Sharpened relief: erosion steepens the middle slopes while sparing the crests, stretching the histogram of slopes toward both ends. And one more, subtle: the sediment map itself. We recorded where every gram was taken and dropped, and that ledger — cut here, fill there — is a soil map. When part three plants grass and part five decides what grows where, "how much silt did the rain leave here" is exactly the question they'll ask.

CodaHistory versus infinity

There is a tension hiding in this article, and it will shape the end of the series. Part one's mountains were a pure function — ask h(x, z) at any coordinate, get the same answer forever, no storage. The rain broke that. Erosion is a process: each droplet changes the ground the next droplet sees, so the carved terrain exists only as a grid of modified heights — bounded, stored, finite. You cannot ask an eroded landscape a question it hasn't already answered. When part six makes the world infinite, it will have to face this honestly: chunks that stream in cannot each carry a hundred-thousand-droplet history that agrees with their neighbors'. The resolution — erode what's near, let the function carry the far — is a story for that article.

Before that, the world needs a skin. Next: a hundred thousand blades of grass, instanced, wind-bent, and rooted exactly where the rain left the soil — while next door in Feather & Bone, the wren who will one day fly over these valleys is growing her skeleton. Her view keeps getting better.
