The Horizon

This flight does not end. There is no far edge of the map under that fog — chunks of world are being built a few milliseconds at a time, just beyond what you can see, and dismantled just behind you. Five articles of functions were the price of this one sentence: the world exists wherever you look, because looking is what causes it.

Every demo in this series so far has had four walls. A patch of mountains 16 units wide, a meadow 56 units wide — generous stages, but stages. Meanwhile, from the very first article, every design decision has been quietly auditioning for today: part one insisted the ground be a pure function with no stored map; part five insisted every tree's existence depend only on integers it can compute locally. Those constraints were never about the demos that carried them. They were about this article, which finally asks: what does it take to never run out of world?

Memory says you can't keep an infinite world; arithmetic says you never need to. If everything visible is a function of coordinates, then the world doesn't have to be stored — it has to be summoned, just-in-time, in the moving window around the camera. The machinery of summoning has three parts: chunks (what to build), levels of detail (how well to build it), and a budget (when to build it). None of the three is glamorous. Together they are the difference between a diorama and a place.

Step 1 · The ringA world keyed by integers

Carve the plane into square chunks and key each one by the integer pair (⌊x/S⌋, ⌊z/S⌋). The rule of existence: a chunk exists if and only if it lies within a few chunks of the camera. Each frame, the manager compares that rule against reality — chunks newly inside the ring get queued for construction, nearest first; chunks that fall outside get dismantled, geometry and all. (One chunk of hysteresis on the way out, so a camera dithering on a boundary doesn't make the same chunk flicker in and out of existence.)

The whole machine from above. The white flashes are construction — watch how they wavefront ahead of the camera's motion, because the queue is sorted by distance and the budget only allows a couple of builds per frame. Notice also what happens at the trailing edge: nothing dramatic. Chunks vanish, and the world doesn't care, because nothing a chunk contained was ever unique to it. That is what all the determinism was for: revisit any coordinate next week and the hash rebuilds the same hill, the same boulder, the same tree leaning the same way.

Step 2 · Resolution by distanceThe pyramid of attention

A chunk three rings out covers the same screen area as a postage stamp; building it at full resolution is generosity nobody sees. So resolution follows distance — full grid density for the ring the camera occupies, half for the next band, a quarter beyond that. The savings are not subtle: in this demo the far rings hold most of the chunks but a small fraction of the vertices, and the vertex total stays roughly constant no matter how far you fly.

The price of mixed resolutions is the seam. Where a 48-segment chunk meets a 24-segment neighbor, their edges sample the same height function at different spacings — and between samples they disagree, leaving pixel-thin cracks of sky. The honorable fix (stitching edge vertices to match the coarser side) is a bookkeeping tax on every border of every LOD pair. The pragmatic fix, used by engines you've played, is the skirt: every chunk wears a short curtain of geometry hanging straight down from its border. Cracks don't show sky anymore; they show skirt, shaded like the terrain above it, and at any distance where LODs differ the eye reads dirt, not deceit. It's a lie with the same defense as the grass normals two articles ago: choose errors that eyes don't audit.

Deep dive: the seam you'd never think of

Cracks are the visible seam problem; lighting is the invisible one. Normals come from central differences on the height grid, and a chunk's border vertex has no neighbor on one side — use a one-sided difference there and every chunk boundary shows up as a faint crease of shading, even with zero geometric cracks. The fix costs almost nothing because the height function is pure: sample one extra row of heights beyond each edge — heights the chunk doesn't own and will never draw — and every border normal becomes honestly two-sided, identical to the one its neighbor computes. Purity pays twice: the margin row is guaranteed to match the neighbor's interior, no communication required.

Step 3 · The budgetMilliseconds, not chunks

At sixty frames a second the renderer owes a frame every 16.7 ms, and a chunk costs real money: thousands of height samples, normals, a scatter query, sometimes ten thousand grass blades. Build everything the wish list wants and the world hitches every time you crest a ridge. The manager's discipline: each frame it builds from the front of the distance-sorted queue until a millisecond budget runs out, then stops mid-list and yields. Whatever didn't get built stays wanted — next frame, the same sort, another few milliseconds. The flight never waits for the world; the world owes no frame more than its budget.

The same thrift decides what far chunks deserve. Trees exist out to ring three (a forest's silhouette carries); grass exists only in the ring you could land in, because at two hundred meters a hundred thousand blades are, measurably, nothing. Population follows attention exactly the way resolution does.

The finished machine. Drag to steer (sideways for heading, up and down for altitude) and pick a direction you like — any direction; they're all infinite. Press freeze streaming and keep flying to do the forbidden thing: catch up with the edge of the world and look over it. Unfreeze and watch the machinery sweep the gap closed in front of you. LOD tint paints each new chunk by its resolution class so you can see the pyramid of attention follow the camera; the readout's "last build" number is the budget at work.

Aside · The rain didn't make it

One passenger from this series is missing. Part two's erosion is a history — each droplet changes the ground the next one sees — and a hundred-thousand-droplet history cannot be replayed per chunk in a 6 ms budget, nor chopped into chunks that agree at their borders (a river is the ultimate neighbor-dependence). So the infinite world fakes maturity the way part one did, with shaping curves, and keeps the honest rain for bounded scenes. This is the standing trade of procedural worlds: functions scale, history convinces, and engineering is choosing which one each feature gets. Big games run erosion offline and ship the result as data; the no-download browser version chooses scale.

CodaGround truth, delivered

Six articles ago this series promised "a world grown from noise," and it's worth tallying what that world now contains: mountains from hashed gradients, valleys that remember rain, meadows planted in simulated silt, trees that grew by competing for light and publish their perches, biomes that follow a climate, and now no edges at all. Every part of it reproducible from a seed and a coordinate; none of it drawn by hand.

And it is, deliberately, a world built for someone. Next door in Feather & Bone, a wren has a body and bones and is working on her wings. The two series were always one story told from two sides: hers ends with flight, and flight needs somewhere worth flying. When she's ready — wingbeats, banking turns, a landing flare onto a branch this series measured for her — the final demo of her series will take place over this ground. The world is finished. It's waiting for its bird.
