The Skeleton

Part one built a static mesh. This article adds the machinery under the fur: eighteen joints, read out of the same table of bones that lofted the mesh, and every vertex assigned which of them to follow. The translucent view above is waving because a few sine waves are turning a few joints — the twelve hundred vertices of his arm were never told anything at all.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part one ended with a useful property: the table of bones that placed every ring of the bear's skin is also, row for row, a skeleton. Each row has a head, a tail, a parent — that is not a modeling convenience; it is also a skeleton. The plan has two halves, shared by every character rig ever shipped: first make the bones move each other (rotate a shoulder and the forearm, paw, and claws must all come along), then make the skin move with the bones — without tearing the mesh apart at the joints.

Neither half touches the vertex buffer. That is the discipline of the whole enterprise: the mesh stays exactly as part one built it, frozen in GPU memory, and everything that looks like motion is a handful of matrices changing on the way in. The bear bends; his triangles never do.

Step 1 · The hierarchyParent-child transforms

Of the twenty-one rows in the bone table, eighteen carry deform: true, and each becomes a joint: a pivot at the row's head point, a parent named by the row, and exactly one animated quantity — a rotation. Nothing else is stored. The skeleton's shape lives in the rest offsets between each head and its parent's head, read once from the table; the pose lives in eighteen quaternions; and the machine that turns one into the other is a single loop of matrix multiplies, parents first: 

// world(child) = world(parent) · T(offset) · R(q) — one pass, parents first
for (let i = 0; i < joints.length; i++) {
  const j = joints[i];
  local.compose(j.restOffset, j.rotation, ONE);
  world[i].multiplyMatrices(world[j.parent], local);  // the root just copies
}

This is forward kinematics, and "forward" is earned: information flows one way, from root to leaf. Because each world matrix is built on the parent's finished world matrix, rotating the shoulder drags the elbow's pivot through space before the elbow's own rotation is even consulted — which is exactly what your skeleton does, and why the hierarchy is the rig. Dial the four angles below and watch the readout: the paw's world position is a number nobody chose, the product of four matrices that each only knew its own joint.

Notice the deal FK offers. Give it angles and it cannot fail: no iteration, no convergence, no impossible inputs — every set of eighteen rotations is some pose, even the embarrassing ones. The cost of that certainty is the steering. You don't get to say where the paw goes; you only get to say how the joints turn, and where the paw goes is news. Hold that thought until the coda.

Aside · Steel under the rubber

The armature is older than the computer. Willis O'Brien's King Kong (1933) was a jointed steel skeleton under rubber muscles and rabbit fur, posed a frame at a time by hand; Ray Harryhausen's creatures ran on the same machined ball joints for forty years. A digital rig is that object, abstracted: quaternions for the ball joints, a matrix pass for the animator's thumbs. The vocabulary even survived — riggers still call the rest pose the bind pose, as if the skin were still being stretched over an armature on a workbench, which, give or take a vertex buffer, it is.

Step 2 · The weightsProximity-based weights

Half one done: bones move bones. But the skin is one welded mesh, not nine separate tubes — when the forearm joint turns, which of the 5,000 vertices should care? In a commercial package an artist answers by painting: brush over the forearm, vertices there get weight. The bear has no artist. It has something better — the table. Every bone is a tapered capsule (two points, two radii), so "is this vertex near the forearm?" is a distance query — point to segment, minus the local radius — against the same silhouette the rings were lofted around. Part one used that distance to build the skin; part two asks it who the skin belongs to.

Per vertex: measure the distance to all eighteen joints, keep the closest four, and convert nearness to loyalty with one sharpening knob — w = 1 / (d + ε)p, then normalize the four to sum to one. The ears and muzzle, which have no joints of their own, are counted as part of the head bone's territory, so an ear vertex reads as "basically zero distance to the head" and rides along. Below, the result as a heatmap: amber is owned, steel-blue is indifferent, and the falloff power p is yours to abuse.

The power is the rig's temperament. At p = 1 loyalty is mushy — half the torso sympathizes with the upper arm and the bear bends like a gummy — while at p = 6 every vertex pledges to one bone and the joints turn to hard creases. The shipped bear sits at 2.4, which is less a number than a taste, arrived at the way all skin weights are: by bending the elbow and squinting.

Step 3 · The blendCorrecting for the rest pose

There is a trap between the weights and the motion. A joint's world matrix maps joint-local points into the world — but the vertices were born in world space, already standing where the rest pose put them. Feed a world-space vertex through a world matrix and it gets the joint's translation a second time; the arm flies off into a parallel arm two shoulder-widths away. Every vertex must first be carried back into the joint's frame — un-positioned, as it were — before being re-posed. That undo is the bind inverse, and the matrix the skin actually uses is the product of both steps: 

// "undo where the bone was born, apply where it is now"
skin[i] = world[i] · inverse(restWorld[i])

In rest pose, world and restWorld cancel and every skin matrix is the identity — the bear stands exactly as built, which is the sanity check rigs live and die by. From there, a posed vertex is a vote among its four bones: run the rest-pose position through each bone's skin matrix and blend the four answers by the weights. The literature calls it linear blend skinning, and it has been the standard since the late eighties for the usual reason — it is four matrix multiplies and an add, and it almost always looks right.

The toggle above is the argument for the blend, compressed. "Rigid" assigns each vertex wholly to its strongest bone — the skeleton's territories made literal — and at a deep elbow bend the mesh cracks open along the border like a poorly mitered pipe joint. "Blended" lets the borderline vertices average the two opinions, and the crack becomes a crease. Almost always right, though: bend far enough and the averaged vertices cut the corner, thinning the elbow — the collapsing joint, linear blending's signature sin, joined by the candy-wrapper collapse wherever a bone twists. Production fixes exist (corrective shapes, dual-quaternion blending); the bear's fix is fur and a forgiving silhouette, which has also shipped many games.

Deep dive: why the bear's bind inverse is just a translation

A production rig exports a full 4×4 inverse bind matrix per bone, because bones in a DCC are born rotated — each has a rest orientation aiming it down its own length. The bear's joints store no rest rotation at all: at bind time every quaternion is identity, and a joint's rest world transform is a pure translation to its head point. The inverse of "translate to the head" is "translate by minus the head", so the entire bind-pose apparatus collapses to makeTranslation(−head) — eighteen vectors the table already knew. The price is that bone rotations are expressed in the parent's axes rather than the bone's own; the rig pays it gladly, and part three's IK will pay it again with a shortest-arc quaternion.

Step 4 · The vertex stageSkinning in the vertex shader

Now the accounting. Where does the blend run? Doing it on the CPU means rewriting every vertex position every frame and re-uploading the buffer — exactly the traffic part one was designed to avoid. So the blend runs where the vertices already live. The weights and indices are baked into the geometry once, as two vec4 attributes per vertex; the eighteen skin matrices ride in as a uniform array; and the vertex stage — the same TSL stage that until now merely stood still — does the voting: 

const boneMats = uniformArray(skin.matrices);  // 18 mat4s — the rig mutates, the GPU re-reads
const idx = attribute("skinIndex");            // vec4: who
const wgt = attribute("skinWeight");           // vec4: how much
const blend = (v) =>
  boneMats.element(int(idx.x)).mul(v).mul(wgt.x)
    .add(boneMats.element(int(idx.y)).mul(v).mul(wgt.y))
    .add(boneMats.element(int(idx.z)).mul(v).mul(wgt.z))
    .add(boneMats.element(int(idx.w)).mul(v).mul(wgt.w));
material.positionNode = blend(vec4(positionGeometry, 1)).xyz;
material.normalNode   = transformNormalToView(blend(vec4(normalGeometry, 0)).xyz.normalize());

Two details earn their keep. Normals go through the same blend with a zero in the fourth component — they are directions, not positions, and must rotate with the bone without inheriting its translation. And the uniform array wraps the very Matrix4[] the rig writes into during its FK pass, so there is no copy step to forget: the CPU computes eighteen matrices, the GPU moves five thousand vertices, and the division of labor is exactly proportional to what each is good at. (three.js ships a SkinnedMesh that does all of this for you — the wren next door uses it. The bear writes the four multiplies out by hand to make the operation visible.) The glossy eyes and nose do not need skinning, so they are simply parented to the head joint's world matrix — three little spheres doing forward kinematics the easy way.

Everything is now wired: table to joints, joints to matrices, matrices to skin. CodaNext: inverse kinematics

Forward kinematics solves only one direction of the problem. It efficiently answers: "if the shoulder turns 40 degrees and the elbow 60, where does the paw end up?" Direction runs opposite. Touch that mark. Look at the firefly. Put the paw on the orb. The place is given; the angles are the problem. Running this article's machine backwards — angles from places — is called inverse kinematics, it is harder in meaningful ways, and for a two-bone limb it is solved by a triangle you met in school.
