The Bear

A new series begins with a new kind of protagonist. Not particles, not light — a character. This bear was never sculpted, never exported from anything: it is one JavaScript function, polygonized in your tab a moment ago. Over four articles it will get a skeleton, learn to want things, and perform qi gong.

Scroll down to read ↓

The previous series on this site simulated stuff: gravity, light, water, sound. This one simulates somebody. The plan, over four parts, is the full character pipeline — modeling, rigging, animation — built from scratch in the browser with three.js and TSL, its shading language that compiles to WebGPU. By the end the bear will perform a qi gong form whose moves you can call out in any order, each one flowing into the next without a single stored animation clip.

But every performer must first exist. The usual way to make a character is interactive sculpting in Blender or ZBrush — hours of artistry, then a file format, then a loader. This article takes the other road, the one this site always takes: state the thing as math and let the computer build it. The entire bear — body, limbs, ears, the slightly self-satisfied belly — is twenty-four capsules and one smooth operator.

Step 1 · The partsA bear kit, in capsules

Look at any stylized animal — a Pixar bear, a Ghibli forest spirit, a claymation dog — and squint. The body resolves into sausages: a fat one for the torso, tapered ones for limbs, little spheres for ears. The capsule (a cylinder with hemispherical caps, optionally tapered) is the atom of character modeling, and it has a property that will pay rent for the whole series: it is defined by two points and two radii. That is also, not coincidentally, the definition of a bone.

So the bear's single source of truth is a table of twenty-four rows: name, parent, head point, tail point, radius at each end. Drag the slider and the kit comes apart.

Two kinds of rows live in the table. Eighteen are deform bones — they have a parent and will become joints in part 2: hips, spine, chest, neck, head, a stubby tail (load-bearing in part 4, when the bear performs Sway the Head and Wag the Tail), and three segments per limb. The other seven are shape-only blobs — ears, muzzle, brows, belly — pure anatomy, no articulation. The split matters later; today everything is just geometry.

Aside · The blobby man of 1980

Building creatures from blended primitives is nearly as old as computer graphics. Jim Blinn, working on the Voyager flyby animations at JPL, modeled molecules as summed Gaussian density fields — "blobby molecules" — and in 1980 bent the same trick into a crouching human figure, the blobby man. Japan's Omura lab and the Wyvills formalized the idea as "metaballs" and "soft objects" through the eighties. The bear is a direct descendant, with one modernization: it blends distances instead of densities, because distances come with a free gift we will collect twice before this page ends.

Step 2 · The glueSmooth minimum

A capsule, as a signed distance function, answers one question: how far is point p from my surface? Negative inside, positive outside, zero exactly on the skin. The sphere-tracing article in the light series used SDFs to march rays; here we use them to be the model. Combining two SDF shapes is one line: min(a, b) — a point is inside the union if it is inside either. But min welds with a crease, and creatures do not have creases where the arm meets the shoulder. They have fillets. The fix is one of the great small functions in graphics, the polynomial smooth minimum: 

function smin(a, b, k) {
  const h = clamp(0.5 + 0.5 * (b - a) / k, 0, 1);
  return mix(b, a, h) - k * h * (1 - h);   // k = width of the handshake
}

Where the two fields disagree by more than k, smin is just min. Inside the disagreement zone it dips below both — and a lower distance means a fatter union, which is exactly a fillet of flesh. Drag a circle into the other and watch the handshake; the bright line is the zero contour, the surface itself.

The bear is this, twenty-four times: fold every capsule in with its own blend width k. Big k where masses merge (thigh into hip, belly into everything — the belly blends at k = 0.12, the most generous handshake in the table, which is why the bear looks well-fed rather than assembled). Small k where anatomy should stay crisp (ears, muzzle). Those two knobs — radius and blend — are the entire sculpting interface:

Slide blend to zero and the bear becomes an obvious bag of capsules — a useful reminder of how little shape information it really contains. Slide it high and the parts melt toward one blob. Character design lives in a surprisingly narrow band in the middle.

Step 3 · The harvestMarching tetrahedra

A field is not a mesh. To render the bear the usual way (and later to deform it, which is the whole point of the series), the zero contour has to be extracted as triangles, once, at load time. The ferrofluid article did this in 2D with marching squares; the textbook 3D answer is marching cubes — visit every cell of a grid, classify its eight corners as in/out, look up the triangle recipe among the 256 cases. But those 256 cases want a famously fiddly lookup table, and there is a humbler relative with no table at all: marching tetrahedra. Slice each cube into six tetrahedra. A tetrahedron has four corners, sixteen sign patterns, and only three honest outcomes: all same side (nothing), one corner odd (a triangle), or two-and-two (a quad). You can write it from scratch and trust it.

Each crossed edge gets one vertex, placed where the field actually hits zero along the edge — t = d₀ / (d₀ − d₁) — and welded: edges are shared between tetrahedra, so vertices are cached by edge identity and the mesh comes out watertight. At a grid of 80 the bear costs about as many triangles as a video-game character; at 14 it is a crystal bear from some lower-polygon afterlife. The readout counts only surface cubes — the empty ones are rejected with eight comparisons, which is why doubling the grid does not even nearly cube the cost.

Aside · The patent dodge, paid off

The ferrofluid article mentioned that marching cubes was patented in 1987, and that "marching tetrahedra" was invented largely to route around the license. The patent expired in 2005, so using the dodge today is an aesthetic choice rather than a legal one — but it is a good aesthetic: no tables, no ambiguous saddle cases (a tetrahedron's quad case has only one reading, where a cube's has two), at the price of a few more, slightly thinner triangles. For a one-time build of a smooth blob, that trade is free money.

Deep dive: the free gift — normals from the gradient

A mesh needs normals for lighting, and the usual recipe — average the face normals around each vertex — inherits every wobble of the triangulation. But this mesh came from a distance field, and a distance field's gradient points exactly away from the nearest surface: the gradient is the normal, analytically smooth no matter how coarse the triangles. Four extra field evaluations per vertex (central differences) buy normals that make the grid-14 crystal bear light like a smooth one wearing a low-poly costume. That is gift number one of blending distances rather than densities; gift number two — "how far is this vertex from that bone?" — is the entire next article.

Step 4 · The furDressing the bear in TSL

The mesh arrives naked and needs a material. This series writes its shaders in TSL — three.js's node-based shading language, where shader code is composed as JavaScript expressions and compiled to WGSL for WebGPU (or GLSL, if a browser must). The bear's coat is built from whatever the geometry already knows: 

const base  = vertexColor();                      // zone color, baked at march time
const grain = mx_noise_float(positionGeometry.mul(scale))  // rest-pose noise: fur glued to the skin
            .add(mx_noise_float(positionGeometry.mul(scale * 3.13)).mul(0.4));
material.colorNode = base.mul(grain.mul(strength).add(1));
material.sheenNode = base.mul(0.5);               // fuzzy backlit edges — fur's tell

Every vertex was stamped at march time with the capsule that owns it — muzzle, belly, paws, ears — and that zone id is baked down to a vertex color, so switching the whole animal from cinnamon to panda is one buffer rewrite, no re-march. (The first draft sent the zone index to the shader and looked the color up there — and learned why you don't: the GPU interpolates attributes across each triangle, and an interpolated index sweeps through every palette slot in between, drawing rainbow stripes along every border. Indices don't interpolate; colors do.) The noise is sampled in rest-pose coordinates (positionGeometry), which matters in two articles: when the bear bends, the grain must bend with the skin, not swim through it. The glossy eyes and nose are confessed cheats — three little spheres parked on the head, because marching a grid fine enough to grow eyeballs is the wrong kind of heroism.

A sheen lobe and a faint fresnel rim do most of the "fur" here — honest strand rendering would be its own article. What matters for this series is on the inside: this is now an indexed triangle mesh with positions, gradient normals, and a zone id per vertex, sitting in GPU buffers. It just doesn't do anything yet.

CodaThe bear is a statue

A statue with a secret, though: the capsule table that sculpted it is also, row for row, a skeleton — heads, tails, parents, a hierarchy. The same distances that decided "this vertex is belly, color it cream" can decide "this vertex is 80% forearm, 20% upper arm" — and that sentence, made precise, is called skinning. Next time: the bear gets joints, the joints get a hierarchy, and the mesh learns to follow them on the GPU, four matrix multiplies per vertex, in the same TSL vertex stage that today merely stands still.
