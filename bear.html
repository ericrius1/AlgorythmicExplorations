The Bear

A new series builds a procedural character rather than a physical simulation. This bear was never sculpted or exported from anything: it is a few hundred triangles, every vertex placed by code in your tab a moment ago. Over four articles it will get a skeleton, inverse kinematics, and a procedural qi gong routine.

Scroll down to read ↓

The previous series on this site simulated gravity, light, water, and sound. This one covers a procedural character pipeline. The plan, over four parts, is the full character pipeline — modeling, rigging, animation — built from scratch in the browser with three.js and TSL, its shading language that compiles to WebGPU. By the end the bear will perform a qi gong form whose moves you can call out in any order, each one flowing into the next without a single stored animation clip.

There are two common procedural modeling approaches. One describes the creature as a field: a function that returns the distance from any point to the surface, which is the approach used for the wren. The bear instead places and connects triangles directly.

Step 1 · The partsA body from tapered tubes

Look at any stylized animal — a Pixar bear, a Ghibli forest spirit, a PS1 mascot — and squint. The body resolves into sausages: a fat one for the torso, tapered ones for limbs, little nubs for ears. Each sausage is fully described by two points and two radii — where it starts, where it ends, how thick at each end. That is also, not coincidentally, the definition of a bone.

So the bear's single source of truth is a table of twenty-one rows: name, parent, head point, tail point, radius at each end. The rows chain into nine tubes — torso through head, two arms, two legs, muzzle, two ears, and a tail. Drag the slider and the kit comes apart.

Two kinds of rows live in the table. Eighteen are deform bones — they have a parent and will become joints in part 2: hips, spine, chest, neck, head, a stubby tail (load-bearing in part 4, when the bear performs Sway the Head and Wag the Tail), and three segments per limb. The other three are shape-only parts — ears and muzzle, pure anatomy, no articulation. The split matters later; today everything is just geometry. Notice what the kit is not: it is not welded into one blob. The arm tube ends inside the shoulder; the ears sink into the skull. Low-poly characters are overlapping low-poly forms arranged to produce a clear silhouette.

Aside · A budget measured in triangles

In 1993 Sega's Virtua Fighter put playable humans on screen at about 1,200 triangles each, and an entire art form grew inside that ration: silhouettes over surfaces, color over detail, five-sided heads that somehow had personality. The budget is long gone — a modern GPU would not notice a million-triangle bear — but the discipline survived as a style. This bear spends about 1,700 triangles, not because it must, but because every one of them is doing visible work.

Step 2 · Mesh constructionStitching vertex rings

A mesh, to the GPU, is two flat lists. A vertex buffer: rows of floats, one row per vertex — position now, normals and colors and (in part 2) bone weights appended to the same row later. And an index buffer: integers, three at a time, each triple naming the corners of one triangle. Modeling is nothing more than filling both lists in a way you can defend.

The loft fills them like a lathe operator. Walk along a chain of bones and drop a station every eleven centimeters or so. At each station, lay a ring of N vertices in the plane perpendicular to the chain, at the bone's radius for that point. Then stitch: every ring pair contributes N quads, every quad two triangles, wound counter-clockwise as seen from outside so their faces point out. The open ends get two shrinking rings and a final apex vertex — a hemisphere from a triangle fan. Repeating this for all nine tubes, then offsetting and concatenating their indices, produces the mesh.

The two sliders control the polygon budget. Sides per ring determines the cross-section resolution; around ten gives the intended shape. Ring density produces smoother bends along the limbs — and almost nothing along the straight stretches, which is why modelers spend rings at the joints and starve them elsewhere.

Deep dive: rings that refuse to twist

Each ring needs two perpendicular directions to draw its circle in. Pick them independently at every station — say, "cross the tangent with world-up" — and the frame snaps 90° wherever a limb bends past the reference axis, candy-wrapping the tube. The fix is parallel transport: choose a frame once, at the first station, then carry it forward, rotating it only by the minimal rotation that takes each tangent to the next. No reference axis, no snap, no twist; each frame derives from the previous one. Hanson and Ma published it as a curve-framing technique in 1995; every tube modeler since has quietly depended on it.

Step 3 · The shapeShaping the mesh

Perfect rings alone produce a generic tube model. Three local shape adjustments give it the intended anatomy. The skull: where the neck's thin rings meet the head bone's fat ones, one extra ring slips in early at full radius, so the head reads as a mass instead of a cone. The paws: the same trick at the wrists, where the radius jumps from 0.058 to 0.08 — a mitt, not a taper. And the belly: every torso vertex whose outward direction faces front, at belly height, gets its radius multiplied by up to 1.45. That is the entire tummy. A sculptor would call it pulling clay; the code calls it one multiply.

The other shape decision is invisible in the buffers and loud on screen: the normals. Shared vertices average their normals and the lighting glides smoothly across edges — the low-poly look dissolves into cheap blobbiness. So after stitching, the bear's vertices are deliberately un-shared: each triangle gets private copies of its three corners, normals computed per face, and the facets light up flat and crisp. It triples the vertex buffer to keep the style. Flat shading therefore depends on topology, not only on the material.

Step 4 · The furTSL fur shading

The mesh then needs a material. This series writes its shaders in TSL — three.js's node-based shading language, where shader code is composed as JavaScript expressions and compiled to WGSL for WebGPU (or GLSL, if a browser must). The bear's coat is built from existing geometry attributes: 

const base  = vertexColor();                      // zone color, stamped at build time
const grain = mx_noise_float(positionGeometry.mul(scale))  // rest-pose noise: fur glued to the skin
            .add(mx_noise_float(positionGeometry.mul(scale * 3.13)).mul(0.4));
material.colorNode = base.mul(grain.mul(strength).add(1));
material.sheenNode = base.mul(0.5);               // fuzzy backlit edges — fur's tell

Every vertex was stamped at build time with the part that owns it — muzzle, belly, paws, ears — and that zone id is baked down to a vertex color, so switching the whole animal from cinnamon to panda is one buffer rewrite, no re-loft. (The first draft sent the zone index to the shader and looked the color up there, which fails because the GPU interpolates attributes across each triangle and an interpolated index sweeps through every palette slot in between, drawing rainbow stripes along every border. Indices don't interpolate; colors do.) The noise is sampled in rest-pose coordinates (positionGeometry), which matters in two articles: when the bear bends, the grain must bend with the skin, not swim through it. The glossy eyes and nose are separate spheres attached to the head, because resolving them in the lofted mesh would require unnecessary ring density.

A sheen lobe and a faint fresnel rim do most of the "fur" here — strand rendering would be its own article. What matters for this series is on the inside: this is now a triangle mesh with positions, face normals, and a zone id per vertex, sitting in GPU buffers. It just doesn't do anything yet.

CodaPreparing the rig

The model already contains the basis of its rig: the bone table that lofted it is also, row for row, a skeleton — heads, tails, parents, a hierarchy. Asking "which bone's tube is this vertex from?" is a distance query, and the same query, softened, can answer "this vertex is 80% forearm, 20% upper arm" — and that sentence, made precise, is called skinning. Next time: the bear gets joints, the joints get a hierarchy, and the GPU deforms the mesh from them using four matrix multiplies per vertex, in the same TSL vertex stage that today merely stands still.
