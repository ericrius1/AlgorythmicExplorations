The Landing

Flying is the easy part. This article is about stopping — arriving at one twig in a whole tree with no speed left, caught by a flare at the edge of the stall — and then, perched, singing with a voice box that has two throats. The wren above is landing on a tree the sister series grew, over and over. Watch her pick her spot.

Part four gave the wren an engine and a sky, and an autopilot that could hold a cruise and wander through lazy turns. But cruising is the easy regime — the forces are gentle, the margins wide. This article tackles the regime where everything is tight: the last few meters of an arrival, where she must hit a precise point in space, pointed the right way, with her speed bled to almost nothing — and do it by flying the same physics, not by snapping to a pose. Then, having landed, she finally gets a voice.

And she lands on something real. The sister series, Ground Truth, finished growing its trees — and those trees publish their branches as perches: a position, a tangent along the twig, a radius, and the headroom above. Her landing controller reads that list like a menu. This is the first place the two series actually touch; in part six they merge entirely.

Aside · Why landing is the boss level

A cruising bird that's off by a meter is still cruising. A landing bird that's off by a meter is in a bush. Landing has no margin: the target is a point, the acceptable speed is near zero, and the approach is flown in the worst corner of the envelope — slow, where lift is weakest and the stall is one twitch away. Every aircraft's trickiest minutes are its last ones, and it's the same physics here. We don't cheat it with a scripted arc; we build a guidance law that flies the forces down to the branch.

Step 1 · The approachGuidance, not a path

The controller never plans a trajectory. Each frame it looks at where the perch is relative to where she is and issues three commands, each a simple feedback law. Steering: measure the angle between her heading and the direction to the perch, and bank in proportion — she rolls toward the target and the part-four physics curves her path there (banking turns, reused). Glide slope: compare her sink rate to the gentle descent the approach wants, and pitch to close the gap. Speed: schedule the throttle by distance, easing off as she nears so she arrives slow.

Launch the approach and watch the phases in the readout: she lines up in cruise, settles onto the dashed glide slope in approach, and at the last moment enters flare. The orange trail is the path the guidance laws actually flew — never drawn, only fed back, frame after frame. Nothing tells her where to be; she's told only where the branch is, and corrects her way onto it.

Step 2 · The flareThe stall, used on purpose

Here is the trick that makes a landing soft, and it's the angle-of-attack cliff from part four turned from hazard into tool. Approaching the branch, she pitches her wings sharply nose-up — toward the stall. For an instant before the stall, lift spikes: she's still moving, and the steep angle wrings every last newton out of the air. That spike arrests her descent — the cushion — while the drag of the steep wing kills her remaining speed. A heartbeat later the wing stalls fully and lets go, but by then she's at the branch with nothing left to give. Spike, cushion, stall, stop.

This is the flare in slow motion, plotted: the blue speed line bleeding toward zero, and the green sink-rate line — watch it lift toward level right in the shaded flare zone, the cushion arresting her fall just before touchdown. No part of those curves is keyframed. They're what the force balance does when you point a slow wing steeply up at the right moment, which is precisely what "flare" means to every pilot and every bird.

Deep dive: a controller is the seed of a brain

The three feedback laws here — bank from heading error, pitch from sink-rate error, throttle from distance — are the simplest members of a family that runs all the way up to animal intelligence. Each is a proportional controller: measure an error, push back in proportion. String a few together with a phase machine (cruise → approach → flare → perched) and you have something that looks goal-directed without any planning at all. Part six will let you supply the errors instead — your steering becomes her heading command — and the very same laws will fly your intentions. The reflex and the pilot share a wire.

Step 3 · The songA voice box with two throats

Perched, she sings — and birdsong deserves its own physics. Birds don't have our larynx. They have a syrinx, sitting deep at the fork where the windpipe splits toward the two lungs, and it has two independent sound sources, one per branch. Suthers proved in 1990 that a songbird can drive the two halves separately — which is how a wren sings two notes at once, and trills faster than any single voice could. We model exactly that: two oscillators, each with its own pitch and volume envelope, summed.

On top of the two voices sits a little grammar. Wren song is built from a handful of gestures — clear whistles, quick rising sweeps, and the machine-gun trills they're famous for — strung into short, repeatable phrases. The generator picks a sequence of those gestures with wren-ish proportions (lots of trills), hands them to one voice or the other, and schedules the whole burst at once.

Tap for sound and she sings a freshly generated phrase up in the brilliant 2–8 kHz register where wrens live. Her beak isn't animated to the song — it's driven by it: the demo reads the actual loudness coming out of the speakers and opens her beak to match, the same reading the music series takes off its master bus. Two systems we built separately, snapped together: a rig that can open a beak, and a synth that can fill it.

Step 4 · The whole actLand, sing, leave

Put it on a loop over a real tree. She circles in, the controller picks a perch from the tree's published list and flies her onto it, she settles and sings a phrase, then launches — a burst of full-power flapping — and lines up for the next branch. Approach, flare, perch, song, takeoff: the whole vocabulary of this series running as one behavior, with nothing scripted but the order of the phases.

That tree is not decoration — it's an actual Ground Truth tree, grown by space colonization, its branches found and ranked as perches by the sister series' own code. The wren is choosing real twigs. This little scene is a preview of the finale: a bird with a full behavioral repertoire, loose in a world that was grown, not drawn.
