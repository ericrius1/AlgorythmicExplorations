The Landing

This article adds landing: approaching one bough in a whole tree, killing thirteen meters per second with a flare at the stall's edge, and then screaming with a voice box that has two throats. The eagle above is landing on a tree the sister series grew, over and over. Watch her pick her spot.

Part four gave the eagle an engine and a sky, and an autopilot that could hold a cruise and wander through lazy turns. But cruising is the easy regime — the forces are gentle, the margins wide. This article tackles the regime where everything is tight: the last few meters of an arrival, where she must hit a precise point in space, pointed the right way, with her speed bled to almost nothing — and do it by flying the same physics, not by snapping to a pose. It also adds a procedural voice.

And she lands on something real. The sister series, Ground Truth, finished growing its trees — and those trees publish their branches as perches: a position, a tangent along the bough, a radius, and the headroom above. Her landing controller reads that list like a menu — filtered for radius first, because four kilograms of eagle does not perch on a twig. This is the first place the two series actually touch; in part six they merge entirely.

Aside · Why landing is difficult

A cruising bird that's off by a meter is still cruising. A landing bird that's off by a meter is in a bush. Landing has no margin: the target is a point, the acceptable speed is near zero, and the approach is flown in the worst corner of the envelope — slow, where lift is weakest and the stall margin is small. Rather than using a scripted arc, the demo uses guidance laws to control the simulated forces through touchdown.

Step 1 · The approachApproach guidance

The controller never plans a trajectory. Each frame it looks at where the perch is relative to where she is and issues three commands, each a simple feedback law. Steering: measure the angle between her heading and the direction to the perch, and bank in proportion — she rolls toward the target and the part-four physics curves her path there (banking turns, reused). Glide slope: compare her sink rate to the target descent rate, and pitch to close the gap. Speed: schedule the throttle by distance, easing off as she nears so she arrives slow.

Launch the approach and watch the phases in the readout: she lines up in cruise, settles onto the dashed glide slope in approach, and at the last moment enters flare. The orange trail is the path the guidance laws actually flew — never drawn, only fed back, frame after frame. Nothing tells her where to be; she's told only where the branch is, and corrects her way onto it.

Step 2 · The flareLanding flare

The landing flare uses the high-angle-of-attack behavior from part four. Approaching the branch, the bird pitches its wings sharply nose-up — toward the stall. For an instant before the stall, lift spikes: she's still moving, and the steep angle wrings maximum lift from the remaining airspeed. That increase arrests the descent while the drag of the steep wing removes the remaining forward speed. The wing then stalls near touchdown.

This is the flare in slow motion, plotted: the blue speed line bleeding toward zero, and the green sink-rate line — watch it lift toward level right in the shaded flare zone, the cushion arresting her fall just before touchdown. No part of those curves is keyframed. They're what the force balance does when you point a slow wing steeply up at the right moment, which is precisely what "flare" means to every pilot and every bird.

Deep dive: proportional control

The three feedback laws here — bank from heading error, pitch from sink-rate error, throttle from distance — are the simplest members of a broad family of control systems. Each is a proportional controller: measure an error, push back in proportion. String a few together with a phase machine (cruise → approach → flare → perched) and you have something that produces goal-directed behavior without trajectory planning. Part six replaces the autonomous commands with manual steering while retaining the same control laws.

Step 3 · The screamA two-source syrinx model

Perched, she calls — and bird sound deserves its own physics. Birds don't have our larynx. They have a syrinx, sitting deep at the fork where the windpipe splits toward the two lungs, and it has two independent sound sources, one per branch. Suthers proved in 1990 that a bird can drive the two halves separately. Songbirds use that to run two melodies at once; an eagle uses it more bluntly — the two voices stack on the same gesture a few hertz apart, and the beat between them is the metallic shimmer that makes a raptor call sound like it's cutting through something. We model exactly that: two oscillators, each with its own pitch and volume envelope, summed.

On top of the two voices sits a little grammar. Eagle calls are built from fewer gestures than song — an opening klee or two, the long descending scream, the staccato kik-kik-kik series, the occasional fast chitter — strung into short phrases with deliberate gaps. The generator picks a sequence with eagle-ish proportions, hands the gestures to one voice or the other (and stacks both on the scream), and schedules the whole burst at once.

Tap for sound and she calls a freshly generated phrase in the 1–3.5 kHz band where the big raptors live. Her beak isn't animated to the call — it's driven by it: the demo reads the actual loudness coming out of the speakers and opens her gape to match (the pinned lower mandible from part two, earning its hinge), with the head thrown back on the loud gestures. The same loudness reading the music series takes from its master bus.

Aside · The Hollywood eagle

The piercing scream every movie dubs over a bald eagle actually belongs to a red-tailed hawk; real bald eagles mostly produce a surprisingly modest chitter. Our grammar splits the difference — the kik series is honest bald eagle, the long scream is the one the movies taught you — because a procedural bird may as well sound the way the audience already believes she does.

Step 4 · AssemblyThe complete behavior sequence

Put it on a loop over a real tree — scaled to eagle country, because the sister series grew its timber for songbirds. She circles in, the controller picks a bough from the tree's published list and flies her onto it, she settles and screams a phrase, then launches — a burst of full-power flapping — and lines up for the next branch. Approach, flare, perch, scream, takeoff: the whole vocabulary of this series running as one behavior, with nothing scripted but the order of the phases.

The tree is generated by Ground Truth's space-colonization model, with branches found and ranked as perches by the sister series' own code. The scene previews the final integration of the bird's behavior with the procedural world.
