The Sorcerer's Apprentice

Part six combines the series' earlier techniques into an interactive fluid toy. The constants are chosen for visual effect rather than physical accuracy. Move your cursor through it, or click ✋ and use your hands.

Scroll down to read ↓

The earlier parts used visual results to explain physical models. This one uses the same advection and Poisson solve for a substance that does not exist, with constants chosen by eye. It is an interactive graphics effect rather than a model of a particular fluid.

There is a respectable name for this move: visual effects houses call it art direction. The moment a director says "make the smoke angrier," the units come off. The solver stays — the solver is what makes the motion feel coherent instead of arbitrary — but gravity becomes a slider, viscosity becomes taste, and color stops pretending to be temperature. This article is about that handoff: what survives when you keep the mathematics and discard the physics.

Step 1 · The same equation, a third timeIncompressible means swirling

Start with what makes fluid read as fluid. It is not splashing — sand splashes. It is that fluid goes around. Push a hand through smoke and the smoke does not pile into a wall in front of your palm; it slips sideways, curls behind your wrist, and closes back in a pair of vortices. That single behavior — flow refusing to pile up — is the visual signature of incompressibility, and it comes from a familiar constraint: a Poisson equation.

Part five solved ∇²φ = 4πG ρ on a mesh because gravity demanded it. Fluids demand the same equation with the names changed. Evolve a velocity field u however you like — advect it, stir it, add wind — and it comes out slightly illegal, with places where flow converges (a sink) or diverges (a source). Real incompressible fluid allows neither: the divergence ∇·u must be zero everywhere. The fix is to find a pressure field whose gradient exactly cancels the illegal part: 

∇²p = ∇·u        // find the pressure that objects to the pile-up
u ← u − ∇p       // and let it push back

Same operator, third appearance, third meaning. In part one's far field it was implicit in every pairwise pull; in part five it turned density into potential; here it turns "almost a fluid" into a fluid, sixty times a second. The other half of the loop moves the field. Do not push values forward (that explodes, the way part one's first integrator exploded); instead stand at each grid cell, ask where would the fluid here have come from?, and walk backward along the velocity to take what you find there: 

u_new(x) = u_old(x − u(x)·dt)   // semi-Lagrangian: fetch your own past

Fetching can never invent a value larger than what exists, so the scheme is unconditionally stable. Even violent, units-free stirring from a hand does not make it diverge. The price is numerical smearing, which softens the motion in a way that suits this demo.

Aside · Stable Fluids, 1999

Jos Stam's 1999 paper Stable Fluids assembled this loop — move the field, then project it back to legality — into the algorithm that still runs underneath most of the smoke you have ever seen in a game.

Deep dive: solving the projection only as well as the eye can see

The toy runs this loop on a 64³ grid — a quarter of a million cells, advected and projected every frame. Part five solved its Poisson equation exactly, with an FFT, in a periodic box. This box is not periodic (the fluid stops at the glass), so the toy runs ten sweeps of Jacobi relaxation instead — each cell repeatedly averaging its six neighbours.

Ten sweeps is nowhere near convergence, and it does not matter. An under-converged projection leaks a small amount of divergence that the next frame's projection reduces again. For this visual effect, solving only to the point where the remaining error is not visible is sufficient.

Step 2 · Wind that never diesNoise, rotated ninety degrees

A projected grid is a fluid, but an empty fluid is a disappointment: stir it, watch the vortices fade, and within seconds the box is still. Films and games keep their volumes alive with ambient wind. The obvious way to fake wind — sample smooth random noise and push particles along its gradient — fails in an instructive way:

Follow the gradient and every tracer slides to the nearest peak of the noise and parks. The picture clots. A gradient field is the most divergent field there is — it is all sources and sinks — and divergence is death for flow. But rotate that same gradient a quarter turn and the flow runs along the contour lines of the noise instead of across them. Every level set becomes a closed racetrack; no point in the plane gains or loses fluid. In 2D the rotation is one swap and a minus sign; in 3D the same construction is the curl of a noisy vector potential: 

v = ∇ × ψ        // ψ: three channels of smooth noise
∇·(∇×ψ) ≡ 0      // divergence-free, by identity, forever

This is curl noise: procedural turbulence that never needs solving, never decays, and never pools. The toy feeds a slow-drifting curl field into the grid as a body force every frame — the breathing you see when you aren't touching anything is six noise lookups per cell, rotated into legality by a vector identity from the eighteenth century.

Aside · Folklore, written down in 2007

Curl noise was production folklore for years before Bridson, Hourihan and Nordenstam wrote it down formally in 2007. It remains a particularly cheap way to generate convincing motion.

Step 3 · Paint, not matterHalf a million tracers

Here the series' particles return — demoted, and better for it. In parts one through five, particles were the simulation: they carried mass, they pulled on each other, losing one would change the answer. The particles in this toy carry nothing. They are massless tracers dropped into the grid's velocity field, advected by trilinear interpolation exactly the way part five's cloud used its mesh forces — the same two-grid exchange, except nothing is deposited anymore. The fluid does not know the particles exist. They are paint on a river.

Everything you actually see is decided after the physics:

Stretch. Each particle renders as a capsule elongated along its velocity — part two's camera-facing billboard, given a tail. Fast paint becomes streaks, slow paint becomes dots, and the eye reads speed without being told.

Persistence. The renderer never clears the screen. It draws into a high-dynamic-range buffer that is dimmed a few percent each frame instead of erased, so every particle leaves a cooling wake — the long-exposure trick, the same reason star-trail photographs feel like motion rather than positions. Trail length is one number: how much of last frame survives into this one.

Palette. Color is speed, mapped through a three-stop gradient chosen the way a colorist chooses. Ink runs abyss-blue to white the way dye blooms in water; embers runs blood-orange to gold and adds buoyancy, so the dye your hand injects actually rises; nebula slows the field and pulls it gently toward the centre — a soft echo of part one's gravity, the only force in the toy with a memory of being real.

Half a million of these tracers cost less than a tenth of what part one's half-million bodies cost, because nothing is n². No tree, no sort, no FFT — every particle reads eight grid cells and writes itself, in perfect parallel. The cleverness all lives upstream, in the 64³ field that tells the paint where to go.

Step 4 · Hand trackingTwenty-one landmarks

A toy wants to be touched, and a cursor is a single dry fingertip on glass. Hand tracking gives the interface more degrees of freedom than a cursor. It now requires only a webcam and seven megabytes of neural network, running on the same WebGPU device as the fluid.

The model (micro-handpose, a compact WebGPU reimplementation of the MediaPipe hand pipeline) finds palms in the frame, then regresses twenty-one landmarks per hand — four joints per finger plus the wrist — at a couple of milliseconds per frame, entirely in the tab. No video leaves your machine; the camera only starts when you click; the inference shares the GPU with a quarter-million-cell fluid solve and neither notices the other.

Landmarks become an instrument the same way the cursor did, just with more strings. Each palm is a stirrer with a radius and a velocity; each index fingertip is a finer, brighter one — a pen for drawing dye. And the distance between thumb-tip and index-tip, normalized by the hand's own scale so it works at any distance from the camera, controls a second gesture: 

pinch = 1 − |thumb_tip − index_tip| / hand_scale
// open hand pushes; pinched fingers pull inward and add spin

Pinch past half-closed and the stirrer flips sign: instead of pushing fluid away it pulls it in and spins it around the view axis. None of this is physics. All of it is mapping — the same job as part two's dome, geometry in the service of where the audience is standing. Tuning the mapping until a new user understands it quickly takes more iteration than the Poisson solve.

Step 5 · The instrumentPlay

Everything above, at full size. Hover to stir; drag to orbit the box (⌘-scroll zooms); the mode buttons re-dye the same fluid; the ✋ button asks for your camera and enables hand control. Pinch for vortices. Draw with your index finger. Try a slow figure-eight in nebula and then stop moving entirely — the projection keeps your gesture circulating long after you leave, demonstrating the persistence of a velocity field rather than a stateless particle effect.

If no hand and no cursor touches the box for a few seconds, an automatic stirrer follows a slow Lissajous path so the demo remains active.

CodaWhat the machines were for

Count what this toy quietly inherits. The grid of cells with values deposited and interpolated — part five's particle-mesh, with the mass turned to dye. The Poisson projection — part five's solver, with gravity turned to pressure. The per-particle parallel update over a shared field — part one's GPU lesson, with the n² removed entirely rather than defeated. The billboard renderer and orbit camera — part two. The conviction that locality is what makes a fluid a fluid — part three. The shameless welding of two solvers that were never introduced — the same composition used in part four.

It also discards physical units and any conservation law that does not help the interaction. The result is still held together by the numerical methods from the earlier posts, but their purpose here is responsiveness and visual continuity rather than physical prediction.
