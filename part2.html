Gravity on a Dome

Part two: the simulation leaves the plane. Quadtrees become octrees, the galaxy gains depth — and then we bend the world it lives on. Drag the dome above to orbit it.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part one ended with hundreds of thousands of bodies in a flat universe: a Barnes–Hut tree with no pointers, rebuilt from scratch on the GPU every step. If you haven't read it, start there — everything below builds on it.

This part answers the two questions everyone asks next. Does it work in 3D? Yes — and the port is surprisingly small. And can the world be a shape other than "everywhere"? That's where it gets fun: above this paragraph, twenty thousand bodies are gravitating while confined to a thin hemispheric shell, like weather on a snow globe. The dome costs exactly four lines of shader code, and the principle behind those four lines is useful for far more than domes.

Step 1 · The dimension taxWhat 3D actually costs

Adding a dimension sounds like it should hurt. Whole textbooks treat the 2D and 3D versions of an algorithm as separate chapters. So it's worth savoring that here is the entire conceptual diff between a quadtree and an octree: 

// 2D: children of (level, ix, iy)        — four of them
(level+1, 2ix+dx, 2iy+dy)                  dx, dy ∈ {0, 1}

// 3D: children of (level, ix, iy, iz)    — eight of them
(level+1, 2ix+dx, 2iy+dy, 2iz+dz)          dx, dy, dz ∈ {0, 1}

That's it. The implicit-pyramid trick never cared about dimension. Children are still arithmetic; levels are still flat slabs in one buffer. Even the Barnes–Hut acceptance test — cellWidth² < θ² × distance² — survives without changing a character, because widths and distances were always just lengths.

So where's the catch? Memory. Each pyramid level now cubes instead of squares. Part one's finest grid was 1024² ≈ a million cells — 16 MB of accumulators, trivial. The same depth in 3D would be 1024³ ≈ a billion cells: 16 GB, which most machines reading this don't have. So the 3D pyramid stops three levels shorter, at 128³ ≈ two million cells, and the solver picks its depth from the body count — log₂(n)/3, clamped — so small demos don't pay for resolution they can't use.

What does a coarser floor cost physically? Resolution at close range. Two bodies sharing a finest cell can't be told apart by the tree; their interaction falls to the softening term. Softening was always a polite lie about short-range physics; the coarser 3D floor just asks the lie to cover a slightly larger neighbourhood. For a galaxy — where no individual encounter matters — the trade is invisible. For planetary close passes, it would be the first thing to fix.

The algorithm changes little in 3D, but grid memory grows from a square to a cube.

Deep dive: the bookkeeping that changes from 2D to 3D

Level offsets. The levels above level l hold 1 + 8 + 64 + … + 8l−1 nodes — a geometric series summing to (8l−1)/7, where 2D had (4l−1)/3. Both denominators are 2d−1. A bonus falls out: the entire hierarchy above the finest level adds only ~one-seventh extra memory. Build the bottom and the rest of the tree is nearly free.

The spare word fills up. Part one's scatter used a stride of four atomic words per cell (power-of-two strides keep indexing cheap) but only used three: mass, Σm·dx, Σm·dy. In 3D the fourth word becomes Σm·dz, and the layout is exactly full: 

atomicAdd(&grid[c],     u32(round(m * FP_SCALE)));        // Σ mass
atomicAdd(&grid[c + 1], …(m * frac.x * FP_SCALE));        // Σ m·dx
atomicAdd(&grid[c + 2], …(m * frac.y * FP_SCALE));        // Σ m·dy
atomicAdd(&grid[c + 3], …(m * frac.z * FP_SCALE));        // Σ m·dz  ← the spare word

Bounds and stack. The bounds reduction now fills six slots (min/max of x, y, z) instead of four. The traversal stack packs four indices into 32 bits — level(4) | iz(9) | iy(9) | ix(9) — and must be deeper: popping a node pushes eight children instead of four, so the worst case is 7·depth+8 entries instead of 3·depth+4. At depth seven that's 57 words — still small enough to live entirely in registers, which is why the stack walk stays fast.

Mass rides in position.w. A 3D position needs three floats, GPU buffers want 16-byte alignment, so a fourth float was going to exist anyway — and the separate mass buffer disappears. A body is one vec4 of position and one of velocity: 32 bytes, one fetch each. Two hundred thousand bodies fit in ~6 MB.

Step 2 · Seeing in depthA renderer with no sort

Before using the octree, we need to see 3D — and 3D rendering of transparent things usually comes with a dreaded word: sorting. Blending is order-dependent: paint a near glow before a far one and the picture lies. Sorting hundreds of thousands of particles every frame is exactly the kind of bill part one taught us not to pay.

We skip it with arithmetic. Our particles add light rather than occluding it, and addition is commutative: a + b = b + a, so draw order cannot matter, by algebra rather than by effort. A million overlapping glows compose to the same picture in any order — no sort, no depth buffer, nothing to maintain. And this isn't a hack that happens to look acceptable: stars genuinely work this way. They emit rather than block, their angular sizes at galactic scales make occlusion a rounding error, and a long-exposure photograph of a star field is a sum of light.

Aside · Fifty years of fighting draw order

The problem is as old as computer graphics. The painter's algorithm of the early 1970s was literally "draw back-to-front, like a painter." Ed Catmull's z-buffer (1974) killed the sort for opaque scenes by testing depth per pixel and is baked into every GPU since — but a depth test can only discard, not blend, so transparency clawed the sort right back. "Order-independent transparency" is a research topic to this day. Space is a rare scene whose physics makes additive rendering a reasonable model.

The geometry is equally frugal. Each body becomes a billboard: a small quad that always faces the camera, built in the vertex shader from the camera's own right and up vectors. There is no vertex buffer — the six corners are hard-coded in the shader, and per-body data is fetched by instance index straight from the same storage buffers the simulation integrates in place. Nothing is copied between physics and pixels. The fragment shader fades each quad from centre to edge; velocity picks the color — slow bodies blue-white, fast ones orange, which is why collapsing clusters glow like embers.

Step 3 · The octree pyramidPart one, cubed

The full 3D pipeline is part one's pipeline with the constants swapped. Every frame, a dozen dispatches rebuild the universe's index from nothing: 

clear     → zero ~8M atomic words, reset the bounds slots
bounds    → min/max reduce over every body          (the root cell)
scatter   → each body bumps its cell's 4 words      (fixed-point atomics)
resolve   → fixed point → (center of mass, mass)    (finest level)
reduce ×7 → each level from its 8 children          (the pyramid)
force     → stack walk, θ test, integrate           (in place)

Nothing here waits on anything. The tree is rebuilt from scratch each step because rebuilding is cheaper than updating — part one's lesson, intact in 3D. The force kernel still reads only its own body's position and velocity, so it integrates in place, and there is still exactly one copy of the universe in memory, shared by physics and renderer.

Deep dive: how a body avoids gravitating toward itself

When the stack walk reaches a finest-level cell, that cell's centre of mass includes the body doing the asking — every body scattered itself into the grid. The fix is two lines of algebra, no flags or exclusion lists: subtract my mass from the cell's total, un-blend my position from its centre of mass, and attract toward what remains. If I was the only occupant, nothing remains and the cell is skipped.

Twenty thousand bodies by default — drag the slider to two hundred thousand — on the same θ knob you've been turning since part one. The initial condition is part one's recipe with thickness: a spinning disk, each body given the circular velocity its enclosed mass demands, plus vertical scatter. Watch long enough and the disk thickens, clumps, throws off spiral arms, sometimes tears into orbiting sub-clusters that merge again. None of that is scripted. It's the one formula from part one, evaluated quickly enough to matter.

Step 4 · Bending the worldThe dome

Now the promised twist. Everything so far simulates gravity in free space — bodies go wherever forces send them. Suppose we want the simulation to live on a surface: a hemispheric dome, with bodies flowing across it like weather over a planet's cap. The mind starts cataloguing machinery: spherical coordinates? Geodesic equations? A different integrator for a curved manifold?

We need a spring. Here is the entire dome, verbatim from the force kernel: 

if (P.shellK > 0.0) {
  let r = length(p);
  let rhat = p / max(r, 1e-6);
  acc -= rhat * (r - P.shellR) * P.shellK;   // radial spring: stick to the shell
  if (p.z < 0.0) {
    acc.z -= p.z * P.shellK * 4.0;           // floor spring: stay in the upper half
  }
}

A body inside the shell radius gets pushed out; outside, pulled in; below the equator, shoved back up (the floor spring is four times stiffer, because the equator is a hard edge and the shell is a soft preference). Each violation produces a restoring force proportional to how far the rule was broken. This is a penalty force. The constraint never edits positions, never special-cases the integrator, never touches the octree — it is one more acc += among the gravitational ones.

Aside · Penalty methods, 1943

Richard Courant described penalty methods in 1943: replace "you must satisfy this constraint" with "you pay a price that grows with violation." The idea has been rediscovered by structural engineering, optimization, robotics, and every physics engine since.

The slider below makes the argument physical. At zero, free 3D gravity. Drag it up and the shell condenses out of the chaos — bodies snap onto the dome and keep flowing, swirling, clustering, but now along a curved two-dimensional world. And here is the detail worth staring at: gravity still acts through 3D space. Two clusters on opposite sides of the dome attract along the straight chord through the dome — watch them pair up and slide toward each other like beads pulled by a thread under the shell. The dynamics live on the surface; the forces never did.

Aside · Archimedes seeds the dome

Sampling points uniformly over a hemisphere needs care — naive latitude-longitude sampling bunches points at the pole — but the care was taken twenty-two centuries ago. Archimedes proved that a sphere and its circumscribing cylinder have equal areas between any two horizontal slices, so a uniform random height z, paired with a random angle, lands uniformly on the sphere. Two random numbers, no rejection: the hat-box theorem, shipping in a WebGPU demo.

Deep dive: why a spring and not a hard projection?

Projection is the tempting fix — p = normalize(p) * shellR, done — and it is everything a constraint shouldn't be. It silently deletes radial velocity (energy vanishes without anything deciding it should), and it happens outside the integrator's view, so it fights damping, fights gravity, and fights any second constraint you add later. A penalty force obeys the integrator's rules: energy-aware, tunable, composable. Note the gentle velocity damping that switches on with the dome so the shell can settle instead of ringing forever.

The cost of the spring is stiffness: an explicit integrator can only tolerate springs so stiff before the spring's own oscillation outruns the timestep and explodes. That's why the dome uses a modest k plus damping rather than an iron wall. When penalty stiffness becomes the bottleneck, the literature offers heavier artillery — Lagrange multipliers, or the position-based dynamics modern game engines run. For a living, flowing system, the spring is enough.

And nothing in the spring cares that the shape is a sphere. length(p) − shellR is a signed distance to the shell, and the spring pushes down its gradient. Swap in any signed distance function — a torus, a saddle, a terrain heightfield, extruded text — and gravity will flow over that instead, with the same four lines doing the holding.

EpilogueThe sequel's lessons

1. Dimensions cost memory, not ideas. The 2D→3D port changed constants — 4 to 8, squared to cubed — and not one algorithm. The only forced design decision was the memory bill for the finest grid, and the response was a trade (a coarser floor, absorbed by softening), not a redesign.

2. Commutativity can remove work. Additive blending made depth sorting simply not exist, because a + b = b + a is true in every draw order. When you can choose the math your problem runs on, choose math with symmetries and cash them in as deleted code.

3. Constraints are forces. The dome is four lines in the force kernel, and the kernel doesn't know they're special. Before reaching for special-case geometry code, ask what spring would express the rule and let the integrator enforce it. Part three leans on this immediately: the walls of a water tank are the dome trick, three more springs.

4. Physics and pixels can share one memory. The renderer never receives data; it reads the integration buffers directly, by instance index, the same frame they were written. On a GPU, the cheapest copy is the one that isn't.

From here, the solver could Morton-order the bodies so neighbouring threads walk the same octree branches; swap the uniform pyramid for an adaptive tree (LBVH) when clustering outruns 128³; add collision impulses and the dome becomes a container for colliding particles. The core solver would not need to change.
