The Bones

Last time the wren was a statue. This article gives her the table's second gift: joints. Skin weights fall out of the same capsules that modeled her, forward kinematics chains seventeen rotations into a pose, and a handful of fidget timers does the rest. She is looking around right now. Part one produced a faceted mesh in a fixed rest pose. This article adds a skeleton and skinning so the same mesh bends: head turning, tail flicking, body breathing, every vertex deciding for itself how much of each motion to follow. The machinery is called skinning, and most of its input already exists in the capsule table from part one.

The implementation has three steps. First the table's deform rows become actual joints — things that rotate, chained parent-to-child. Then every vertex of the mesh gets weights: a short list of joints it cares about, computed by the same distance function that sculpted the body. Then the GPU blends it all together with one formula from 1988. Once a bird can be posed, idle motion requires relatively little state — the hero above uses four timers and two springs, with no keyframes. In parallel, Ground Truth adds hydraulic erosion to its terrain.

Aside · Reusing the capsule table

Nothing downstream of the capsule table stores its own copy of the bird. This article adds zero new numbers describing the wren — no joint positions, no weight maps, no painted influence regions. Joints are the table's rows with deform: true; their pivots are the rows' head points; the weights are queries against the rows' capsules. Make the neck longer in the table and the rig follows before you've saved the file. The bear — this site's other creature — works the same way: same philosophy, heavier animal.

Step 1 · The skeletonBuilding the joint hierarchy

Of the twenty-two capsules, seventeen carry deform: true — the rows used as bones: body, neck, head, beak, tail, three per wing, three per leg. (The other five — breast, brows, halluxes — are shape-only blobs; they'll reappear in step two.) Each deform row becomes a joint: a pivot at the row's head point, a parent from the row's parent column, and exactly one new degree of freedom — a rotation.

Posing is then a chain rule. A joint's transform in the world is its parent's world transform, times a fixed translation (where the child's pivot sits relative to the parent's — measured straight off the table), times the joint's own rotation: 

world(joint) = world(parent) · T(head − parentHead) · R(joint)

That is the whole of forward kinematics. Rotate the neck and the head, beak, brows, and eyes all inherit the turn, because their transforms are multiplied downstream of it; rotate the body and all sixteen descendants come along. The skeleton is a tree, the pose is a product of matrices along each branch, and one pass over the joints — parents first, which is the order the table is already written in — computes everything.

The x-ray shows octahedra for joints, beads for pivots, and the mesh ghosted on top. Rolling the body moves the full hierarchy; yawing the head moves only its subtree. Complex poses, including a full wingbeat, combine several such rotations.

Step 2 · The weightsComputing skin weights

Joints move; now the skin has to follow. Each vertex needs an influence value for each nearby joint. A vertex on the forehead should follow the head completely. A vertex mid-neck should split its influence — half neck, half body — or it will crease like bent cardboard. The answer is a set of weights per vertex, non-negative, summing to one.

Production pipelines often have artists paint these influences by hand. Here they come from a distance function. Every capsule can report how far a point is from it, so for each vertex, ask all the capsules, keep the four nearest joints, and turn distances into weights with a sharpened reciprocal: 

wᵢ = 1 / (dᵢ + ε)^p          // then normalize so the four sum to 1

The shape-only blobs from step one get their purpose back here: each is a proxy for the joint it rides. The breast capsule answers for the body, the brows answer for the head, so a vertex on the eyebrow reads as practically touching the head even though the head's own capsule is millimeters away. The five shape-only capsules therefore still affect skin weights.

Every joint's territory, painted by arithmetic. The falloff power p is the only artistic dial: low p spreads influence wide and the bird goes rubbery; high p snaps each vertex to its nearest bone and joints crease. Around 2.6, blends span exactly the fillets the smooth minimum built — which is not a coincidence; both are reading the same geometry. And use the stress pose button: weights can only be judged while bent. The rest pose hides weighting problems.

Deep dive: weights on a mesh with no shared vertices

Part one un-shared every vertex so each triangle could own one flat normal and one flat color — the low-poly look. That means the three corners of a triangle are distinct vertices and each computes its own weights. Corners at the same position get the same weights (the computation is a pure function of position), so the skin never tears; but within a triangle the GPU interpolates between corner weights as smoothly as on any mesh. The facets stay rigid little plates exactly when their corners agree — near a joint they shear slightly instead of folding, which reads as the plates "giving" at the hinge. It looks deliberate. We'll take it.

Step 3 · The blendLinear blend skinning

One piece is missing. A joint's world matrix says where the joint is, but a vertex needs to know how the joint moved — the change from rest. That's one multiplication: undo where the bone was born, then apply where it is now. With rest-pose world transforms being plain translations to each pivot, the skin matrix for joint i is 

Mᵢ = world(i) · T(−headᵢ)      // "as born" → identity; rest pose moves nothing
v′ = Σᵢ wᵢ · Mᵢ · v             // each vertex: blend of its four bones' motions

The second line is linear blend skinning, and it is what the GPU executes for each vertex of a skinned character. The method is essentially unchanged since Magnenat-Thalmann, Laperrière and Thalmann wrote it down in 1988 for a hand grabbing a ball. Our four weights and four indices ride into the vertex shader as a pair of vec4 attributes; three.js calls the bundle a SkinnedMesh and runs the sum on hardware.

The fully skinned model has seven dials and three preset poses. The eyes require separate handling because they were never part of the field; part one bolted them on as riders at fixed positions. Now they're parented to the head joint, exactly as promised, so they get carried by world(head) without owning a single weight. Turn her head: the face follows, including the glints. The same approach works for any rigid object attached to a joint.

Aside · The candy wrapper

Linear blend skinning has a famous failure: blend two rotations that differ too much and the linear average of matrices is not a rotation — it shrinks. Twist a wrist 180° and the forearm pinches to a point like a candy wrapper; bend an elbow hard and the inner crease loses volume. Wrens have no wrists, use modest joint angles, and have plumage that hides the elbows, so this article uses the 1988 formula. The fix, blending rotations as dual quaternions instead of matrices, was introduced in 2008 and would be the next upgrade if shoulder twisting caused visible collapse.

Step 4 · Idle motionLayered timers and springs

A poseable bird still needs idle behavior. Watch a real wren for one minute and you'll see the repertoire — she fixates on a point, holds, snaps to a new one (birds move their eyes barely at all; they move the whole head, in saccades), the tail flicks up and rings back down, the body never quite stops breathing.

Every item on that list is one line of state. A look target that re-rolls every couple of seconds, approached fast and held — that's a timer and an exponential ease. The tail flick is an impulse into an underdamped spring, so it overshoots and rings down like the real thing. Breath is a slow sine on the body joint, weight-shift a slower one on its roll. No keyframes, no animation clips, no blend trees — the pose is computed fresh every frame from four timers and two springs, and the rig produces the motion.

The curiosity slider controls saccade rate, while tail sass controls flick frequency. Unlike a looping animation clip, the generated behavior can respond to state. In part six, when you're flying her, the same head-saccade system will be looking where you're about to turn.
