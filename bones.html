The Bones

Last time the eagle was a statue. This article gives her the table's second gift: joints. Skin weights fall out of the same bone segments that placed her lofts, forward kinematics chains seventeen rotations into a pose, the feather coat rides along for free, and a handful of fidget timers does the rest. She is glaring around right now. Drag to glare back.

Part one produced a faceted mesh in a fixed rest pose. This article adds a skeleton and skinning so the same mesh bends: head turning, tail pitching, body breathing, every vertex deciding for itself how much of each motion to follow. The machinery is called skinning, and most of its input already exists in the bone table from part one.

The implementation has three steps. First the table's rows become actual joints — things that rotate, chained parent-to-child. Then every vertex of the body gets weights: a short list of joints it cares about, computed by distance to the bone segments — with a few components deliberately exempted and pinned instead. Then the GPU blends it all together with one formula from 1988. Once a bird can be posed, idle motion requires remarkably little state — the hero above runs on five timers and two springs, with no keyframes. In parallel, Ground Truth adds hydraulic erosion to its terrain.

Aside · Reusing the table

Nothing downstream of the bone table stores its own copy of the bird. This article adds zero new numbers describing the eagle — no joint positions, no weight maps, no painted influence regions. Joints are the table's rows; their pivots are the rows' head points; the weights are queries against the rows' segments. Make the neck longer in the table and the rig follows before you've saved the file. The bear — this site's other creature — works the same way: same philosophy, heavier animal.

Step 1 · The skeletonBuilding the joint hierarchy

Seventeen rows, seventeen joints: body, neck, head, beak, tail, three per wing, three per leg. Each row becomes a pivot at its head point, a parent from its parent column, and exactly one new degree of freedom — a rotation.

Posing is then a chain rule. A joint's transform in the world is its parent's world transform, times a fixed translation (where the child's pivot sits relative to the parent's — measured straight off the table), times the joint's own rotation: 

world(joint) = world(parent) · T(head − parentHead) · R(joint)

That is the whole of forward kinematics. Rotate the neck and the head, beak, brows, and eyes all inherit the turn, because their transforms are multiplied downstream of it; rotate the body and all sixteen descendants come along. The skeleton is a tree, the pose is a product of matrices along each branch, and one pass over the joints — parents first, which is the order the table is already written in — computes everything.

The x-ray shows octahedra for joints, beads for pivots, and the mesh ghosted on top. Rolling the body moves the full hierarchy; yawing the head moves only its subtree. Complex poses, including a full wingbeat, combine several such rotations.

Step 2 · The weightsProximity, and the pins

Joints move; now the skin has to follow. Each vertex needs an influence value for each nearby joint. A vertex on the forehead should follow the head completely. A vertex mid-neck should split its influence — half neck, half body — or it will crease like bent cardboard. The answer is a set of weights per vertex, non-negative, summing to one.

Production pipelines often have artists paint these influences by hand. Here they come from a distance function. Every bone segment can report how far a point is from it (minus its flesh radius), so for each vertex, ask all seventeen, keep the four nearest, and turn distances into weights with a sharpened reciprocal: 

wᵢ = 1 / (dᵢ + ε)^p          // then normalize so the four sum to 1

Proximity has blind spots the anatomy refuses to forgive, and this is where part one's component bookkeeping pays off. The upper beak runs far from the skull segment but must follow it rigidly — only the lower mandible rides the beak joint; that hinge is the gape she'll scream with in part five. And a talon that blends weights goes soft like licorice. So a loft can declare a pin: every vertex in that component is nailed to one named bone, weight one, stamped over the proximity result. Beak halves, toes, and talons are pinned; everything fleshy stays blended.

Every joint's territory, painted by arithmetic. The falloff power p is the only artistic dial: low p spreads influence wide and the bird goes rubbery; high p snaps each vertex to its nearest bone and joints crease. Around 2.6 the blends span the shoulder and hip exactly as far as the flesh radii overlap — both are reading the same table. Use the stress pose button: weights can only be judged while bent. The rest pose hides weighting problems. And flip to one joint on the beak or a foot to see a pin: a hard, binary territory with no gradient at all.

Deep dive: weights on a mesh with no shared vertices

Part one un-shared every vertex so each triangle could own one flat normal and one flat color — the low-poly look. That means the three corners of a triangle are distinct vertices and each computes its own weights. Corners at the same position get the same weights (the computation is a pure function of position), so the skin never tears; but within a triangle the GPU interpolates between corner weights as smoothly as on any mesh. The facets stay rigid little plates exactly when their corners agree — near a joint they shear slightly instead of folding, which reads as the plates "giving" at the hinge. It looks deliberate. We'll take it.

Step 3 · The blendLinear blend skinning — and the riders

One piece is missing. A joint's world matrix says where the joint is, but a vertex needs to know how the joint moved — the change from rest. That's one multiplication: undo where the bone was born, then apply where it is now. With rest-pose world transforms being plain translations to each pivot, the skin matrix for joint i is 

Mᵢ = world(i) · T(−headᵢ)      // "as born" → identity; rest pose moves nothing
v′ = Σᵢ wᵢ · Mᵢ · v             // each vertex: blend of its four bones' motions

The second line is linear blend skinning, and it is what the GPU executes for each vertex of a skinned character. The method is essentially unchanged since Magnenat-Thalmann, Laperrière and Thalmann wrote it down in 1988 for a hand grabbing a ball. Our four weights and four indices ride into the vertex shader as a pair of vec4 attributes; three.js calls the bundle a SkinnedMesh and runs the sum on hardware.

And the feathers? They skip all of it. Part one made each one a rigid instance with a bone, an anchor, and a fan angle, so when this article's joints start rotating, the coat re-poses itself from the fresh bone matrices — a hundred and twenty-eight passengers on seventeen vehicles. The eyes work the same way, parented to the head joint without owning a single weight. Turn her head: the face and the nape feathers come along, glints included.

The fully skinned model has seven dials and four preset poses — including a mantle (wings dropped over an imaginary kill) and the scream, which is one pinned mandible and a head throw. Watch the wing droop dial: the bones rotate a few degrees, but the coat's fan angles crack open with them, which is most of what your eye reads as "wing".

Aside · The candy wrapper

Linear blend skinning has a famous failure: blend two rotations that differ too much and the linear average of matrices is not a rotation — it shrinks. Twist a wrist 180° and the forearm pinches to a point like a candy wrapper; bend an elbow hard and the inner crease loses volume. Our eagle bends modest angles at the body, and the joints that do rotate hard — the wrist in a folded wing — live under a coat of rigid feathers that hides the hide. The fix, blending rotations as dual quaternions instead of matrices, was introduced in 2008 and would be the next upgrade if a bare joint ever collapsed on camera.

Step 4 · Idle motionLayered timers and springs

A poseable bird still needs idle behavior. Watch a perched raptor for one minute and you'll see the repertoire — she fixates on a point, holds, snaps to a new one (birds move their eyes barely at all; they move the whole head, in saccades), the tail flicks and rings back down, the body never quite stops breathing, and every so often the whole bird shakes her coat back into place — falconers call it a rouse, and it usually means she's relaxed.

Every item on that list is one line of state. A look target that re-rolls every couple of seconds, approached fast and held — that's a timer and an exponential ease. The tail flick is an impulse into an underdamped spring, so it overshoots and rings down like the real thing. Breath is a slow sine on the body joint, weight-shift a slower one on its roll; the rouse is a third timer driving a half-second shiver through the body roll and a flutter of wing spread. No keyframes, no animation clips, no blend trees — the pose is computed fresh every frame from five timers and two springs, and the rig produces the motion.

The vigilance slider controls saccade rate, while temper controls how often the tail and the coat have opinions. Unlike a looping animation clip, the generated behavior can respond to state.
