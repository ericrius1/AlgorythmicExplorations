The Bones

Last time the wren was a statue. This article gives her the table's second gift: joints. Skin weights fall out of the same capsules that modeled her, forward kinematics chains seventeen rotations into a pose, and a handful of fidget timers does the rest. She is looking around right now. Part one ended with a confession: everything in it was built to be destroyed. The mesh that came out of the polygonizer was frozen in rest pose — a beautifully faceted statue. This article breaks the freeze. By the end, the same mesh bends: head turning, tail flicking, body breathing, every vertex deciding for itself how much of each motion to follow. The machinery is called skinning, and the punchline of the whole article is that we already built most of it — in part one, by accident, when we wrote down the capsule table.

The plan is three steps and a reward. First the table's deform rows become actual joints — things that rotate, chained parent-to-child. Then every vertex of the mesh gets weights: a short list of joints it cares about, computed by the same distance function that sculpted the body. Then the GPU blends it all together with one formula from 1988. And once a bird can be posed, making her act turns out to be embarrassingly cheap — the hero above is four timers and two springs, no keyframes anywhere. Her sister series is on the same arc: while the wren learns to move, Ground Truth's mountains are learning history, one simulated raindrop at a time.

Aside · The contract pays out

Part one made a promise: nothing downstream of the capsule table would ever store its own copy of the bird. Here is the first payout. This article adds zero new numbers describing the wren — no joint positions, no weight maps, no painted influence regions. Joints are the table's rows with deform: true; their pivots are the rows' head points; the weights are queries against the rows' capsules. Make the neck longer in the table and the rig follows before you've saved the file. The bear — this site's other creature, currently backstage while his series is rebuilt — works the same way: same philosophy, heavier animal.

Step 1 · The skeletonRows become joints

Of the twenty-two capsules, seventeen carry deform: true — the rows that are secretly bones: body, neck, head, beak, tail, three per wing, three per leg. (The other five — breast, brows, halluxes — are shape-only blobs; they'll reappear in step two.) Each deform row becomes a joint: a pivot at the row's head point, a parent from the row's parent column, and exactly one new degree of freedom — a rotation.

Posing is then a chain rule. A joint's transform in the world is its parent's world transform, times a fixed translation (where the child's pivot sits relative to the parent's — measured straight off the table), times the joint's own rotation: 

world(joint) = world(parent) · T(head − parentHead) · R(joint)

That is the whole of forward kinematics. Rotate the neck and the head, beak, brows, and eyes all inherit the turn, because their transforms are multiplied downstream of it; rotate the body and all sixteen descendants come along. The skeleton is a tree, the pose is a product of matrices along each branch, and one pass over the joints — parents first, which is the order the table is already written in — computes everything.

The x-ray shows the machinery naked: octahedra for joints, beads for pivots, the mesh ghosted on top. Roll the body and watch the whole tree obey; yaw the head and only its subtree moves. One slider, one joint, one rotation — every complex pose this series will ever strike, including a full wingbeat, is just several of these at once.

Step 2 · The weightsWhich bones own you?

Joints move; now the skin has to follow. The question every vertex must answer is: when joint j rotates, how much do I care? A vertex on the forehead should follow the head completely. A vertex mid-neck should split its loyalty — half neck, half body — or it will crease like bent cardboard. The answer is a set of weights per vertex, non-negative, summing to one.

Production pipelines have artists paint these by hand, brushing influence onto meshes like watercolor. We have something better than a brush: a distance function. Part one already taught every capsule to report how far any point is from it. So for each vertex, ask all the capsules, keep the four nearest joints, and turn distances into weights with a sharpened reciprocal: 

wᵢ = 1 / (dᵢ + ε)^p          // then normalize so the four sum to 1

The shape-only blobs from step one get their purpose back here: each is a proxy for the joint it rides. The breast capsule answers for the body, the brows answer for the head, so a vertex on the eyebrow reads "practically touching the head" even though the head's own capsule is millimeters away. Five capsules that deform nothing, still earning their rows.

Every joint's territory, painted by arithmetic. The falloff power p is the only artistic dial: low p spreads influence wide and the bird goes rubbery; high p snaps each vertex to its nearest bone and joints crease. Around 2.6, blends span exactly the fillets the smooth minimum built — which is not a coincidence; both are reading the same geometry. And use the stress pose button: weights can only be judged bent. Rest pose hides every sin.

Deep dive: weights on a mesh with no shared vertices

Part one un-shared every vertex so each triangle could own one flat normal and one flat color — the low-poly look. That means the three corners of a triangle are distinct vertices and each computes its own weights. Corners at the same position get the same weights (the computation is a pure function of position), so the skin never tears; but within a triangle the GPU interpolates between corner weights as smoothly as on any mesh. The facets stay rigid little plates exactly when their corners agree — near a joint they shear slightly instead of folding, which reads as the plates "giving" at the hinge. It looks deliberate. We'll take it.

Step 3 · The blendOne formula since 1988

One piece is missing. A joint's world matrix says where the joint is, but a vertex needs to know how the joint moved — the change from rest. That's one multiplication: undo where the bone was born, then apply where it is now. With rest-pose world transforms being plain translations to each pivot, the skin matrix for joint i is 

Mᵢ = world(i) · T(−headᵢ)      // "as born" → identity; rest pose moves nothing
v′ = Σᵢ wᵢ · Mᵢ · v             // each vertex: blend of its four bones' motions

The second line is linear blend skinning, and it is what the GPU executes for every vertex of every skinned character in every game you have ever played, essentially unchanged since Magnenat-Thalmann, Laperrière and Thalmann wrote it down in 1988 for a hand grabbing a ball. Our four weights and four indices ride into the vertex shader as a pair of vec4 attributes; three.js calls the bundle a SkinnedMesh and runs the sum on hardware. The arithmetic is ours; the silicon is rented.

The puppet, fully strung. Seven dials, three preset poses, and one detail to catch: the eyes. They were never part of the field — part one bolted them on as riders at fixed positions. Now they're parented to the head joint, exactly as promised, so they get carried by world(head) without owning a single weight. Turn her head: the face simply comes along, glints and all. Free, forever, for anything else we ever glue to a joint.

Aside · The candy wrapper

Linear blend skinning has a famous failure: blend two rotations that differ too much and the linear average of matrices is not a rotation — it shrinks. Twist a wrist 180° and the forearm pinches to a point like a candy wrapper; bend an elbow hard and the inner crease loses volume. Wrens are merciful subjects — no wrists, modest joint angles, plumage hiding the elbows — so this article gets away with the 1988 formula. The fix, blending rotations as dual quaternions instead of matrices, waited until 2008. If the wingbeat's shoulder twist ever candy-wraps, that's the IOU we'll cash.

Step 4 · AliveFour timers, two springs

A poseable bird is still a statue between poses. The final step is the cheapest and the most rewarding: idle behavior. Watch a real wren for one minute and you'll see the repertoire — she fixates on a point, holds, snaps to a new one (birds move their eyes barely at all; they move the whole head, in saccades), the tail flicks up and rings back down, the body never quite stops breathing.

Every item on that list is one line of state. A look target that re-rolls every couple of seconds, approached fast and held — that's a timer and an exponential ease. The tail flick is an impulse into an underdamped spring, so it overshoots and rings down like the real thing. Breath is a slow sine on the body joint, weight-shift a slower one on its roll. No keyframes, no animation clips, no blend trees — the pose is computed fresh every frame from four timers and two springs, and the rig does the acting.

The sliders are personality knobs: curiosity is the saccade rate, tail sass the flick frequency. Turn both up and she's caffeinated; down, and she's contemplating rain. This is the part of character work that data can't sell you — a downloaded animation clip loops; a behavior responds. In part six, when you're flying her, the same head-saccade system will be looking where you're about to turn.
