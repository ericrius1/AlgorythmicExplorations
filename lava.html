The Lava Lamp

A new series begins with a lava lamp. The wax above is a fluid simulation that tracks its own temperature; the glow is real two-dimensional light transport, bouncing off things. Stir it with your cursor. Then wait — waiting is the whole point of a lava lamp.

Scroll down to read ↓

The previous series spent six parts simulating matter: gravity, sand, water, dust, the cosmic web, and finally a toy fluid. In all that time, light was never once simulated. Every pixel you saw was a particle stating its own color — additively blended, artfully graded, but self-reported. No particle ever lit another. The galaxy's core, half a million suns deep, cast no glow on the arms around it.

This series fixes that, and it starts deliberately small: one lamp, one room, two dimensions. A lava lamp is an ideal first subject because it is two coupled problems. The wax is a thermodynamics problem — heat in at the coil, heat out at the cap, buoyancy in between. Its appearance is a light transport problem: the bloom around a rising blob, the amber wash on the cold wax above, and the shadow a sinking blob drags across the glass all come from light leaving one place and arriving at another. The demo therefore simulates both the wax motion and the light it emits.

Step 1 · One scalar per particleWax that knows how hot it is

The fluid is the same smoothed-particle hydrodynamics that part three of the gravity series built for water: particles sorted into a grid, density summed over neighbours, pressure pushing back wherever the crowd exceeds a rest density. One addition changes the species of the fluid: every particle now carries a temperature, and the neighbour loop that already sums density also lets heat conduct between particles that touch — 

rho     += w·w            // density, as before
tFlux   += (T_j − T_i)·w  // conduction: heat flows along the same kernel

Heat enters at the bottom — particles near the coil warm at a fixed rate — and leaks away everywhere, faster near the cool top of the vessel. What makes it a lava lamp is that temperature feeds back into the mechanics twice: 

rest_i = ρ₀ · (1 − β·T_i)        // thermal expansion: hot wax wants more room
acc.y += buoyancy·T_i − gravity   // and the unsimulated water lifts what expanded

The first line is an inexpensive approximation of thermal expansion: raising a particle's temperature lowers the density it is content with, so a warm region pushes its neighbours apart and genuinely takes up more space — blobs swell as they heat, as wax does. The second line is a larger simplification. In a real lamp the wax floats in water, and buoyancy is the water's opinion of the wax's density. We are not simulating the water, so its job is performed by a single force proportional to temperature. Cold wax sinks and hot wax rises.

Watch the cycle develop: heat pools along the floor until a patch of wax expands enough to pinch off and rise. It cools on the way up until gravity overcomes the buoyancy term and the blob turns around. No line of code says "make blobs circulate." The loop emerges from four numbers you can drag: heat in, lift per degree, swelling per degree, heat out.

Step 2 · Particles become a surfaceMetaballs, and a temperature-lit skin

Particles are how the wax moves; they are a terrible way for wax to look. Wax is continuous, and the eye knows it. The fix is the oldest trick in implicit surfaces: every particle splats a smooth radial bump into a coarse field texture, the bumps sum, and a threshold of that sum defines "inside." Two particles drifting together merge into one rounded mass before they touch — which is precisely the surface-tension look that real wax has and raw dots never will.

Aside · Blobby molecules

Jim Blinn introduced the technique in 1982 as blobby molecules, for rendering electron-density surfaces; everyone since has called them metaballs.

The same splat carries temperature along as a second channel, so the surface pass knows the local mean temperature everywhere — and that number decides what the wax is, optically: 

emission = palette(T) · luminance(T)   // cold: near-black plum … hot: amber-white, and bright
occlusion = 1 inside the wax           // hot or cold, wax blocks light

Cold wax is an occluder: nearly black, visible only by what it blocks. Hot wax is a light source. The same blob is both at different moments of its life, which is why a lava lamp cannot be faked with a glow sprite — the scene's lights move, split in two, and cast shadows on each other. We now need what no part of any series so far has needed: actual light transport.

Step 3 · Light transportRadiance cascades

The problem, stated plainly: for every pixel of the canvas we want the light arriving from every direction — with shadows, in real time. The brute-force answer marches a few hundred rays from every pixel, every frame. That is the n² of light transport, and this series opens exactly the way the last one did: with a cost that looks impossible until a structural observation collapses it.

The observation is now called the penumbra condition: the further away a light source is, the softer its contribution varies across space — but the more precisely you need to know its direction. Near light needs dense spatial sampling and barely any angular detail; far light needs fine angular detail and barely any spatial. No single grid of probes can be right for both. So don't use one grid. Use a cascade: 

cascade 0:  probes every 2px,  4 rays each,  reaching   0 →   4px
cascade 1:  probes every 4px, 16 rays each,  reaching   4 →  20px
cascade 2:  probes every 8px, 64 rays each,  reaching  20 →  84px
// each level: ¼ the probes × 4 the rays — every cascade costs the same

Each cascade traces only a thin annulus of ray — short segments that begin where the previous level's rays gave up. Watch one probe do it, ray by ray, as you scroll:

A pixel's final light is assembled top-down: each cascade's rays that hit nothing inherit the radiance of the matching directions one level up, interpolated between the four surrounding coarser probes. The hierarchy meets in cascade 0, which knows, for every 2×2 block of pixels, the light arriving from everywhere on the canvas — noiselessly, because nothing was ever sampled at random. The whole stack costs about as much as 30 rays per pixel; the equivalent brute force would be thousands.

Aside · Shipped in a game before it was a paper

Radiance cascades were published by Alexander Sannikov in 2023 and ship in Path of Exile 2. If the merge — coarse levels answering for whatever fine levels cannot see — feels familiar, it should: it uses the same hierarchical approximation as Barnes–Hut in part one.

Deep dive: distance fields and the jump flood

Two pieces of supporting machinery. The rays sphere-march against a distance field — at every pixel, the distance to the nearest surface, so each step can leap that far with certainty. And the field is built in logarithmic time by the jump flood algorithm (Rong & Tan, 2006): every pixel repeatedly asks neighbours at halving offsets "what's the nearest seed you know about?" Nine ping-pong passes and a 450×280 canvas knows its distance to everything. The halving-offset cascade is the same doubling rhythm as part three's scan and part five's FFT.

This sandbox is the algorithm with nothing in front of it. Paint lights, paint walls, erase, and watch shadows wrap correctly around corners you just drew. The one slider truncates the cascade hierarchy: at one cascade, light reaches four pixels and dies. Each additional level lets light leap four times further for the same per-pixel cost.

Step 4 · AssemblyThe lamp

Everything connects in one direction, no cycles: particles → temperature → metaball field → emission and occlusion → distance field → cascades → pixels. The coil at the base is painted into the same scene texture as the wax, so it is not a special light — it is just a hot object, exactly as a coil is. The metal base and cap are dark occluders; the glass is a faint emissive sliver that never blocks anything.

The view button exposes each stage of the renderer. Scene shows what the rays actually see — a few glowing shapes on black, the entire input to the lighting. Distance field shows the jump flood's contour map, warping in real time as blobs split and merge. Light only drops the emission boost and shows pure transported light: every photon in that view arrived at its pixel from somewhere else. Flip back to final and find the moment a cold blob drifts past a hot one and picks up an amber rim on one side only. Nothing in the code draws that rim directly; it comes from the transported light.

CodaWhat carries over

Much of the earlier series carries over. The grid sort under the wax is part three's, untouched except that particles grew eight bytes of temperature; the deposit-and-threshold field is part five's two-grid exchange with mass swapped for heat; the cascade merge is part one's hierarchy, for photons.

What's genuinely new is the direction of travel. Matter simulation asks where do things go? Light transport asks what do things see? — and it turns out to compress the same way, hierarchically, by being principled about what you can afford not to know. Next in this series: the glow leaves the lamp. Same cascades, harder rooms — moving occluders in number, colored bounce, and the question of what happens when the light source is the thing your hands are stirring.
