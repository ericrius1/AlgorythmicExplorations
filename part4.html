Worlds from Dust

Part four: the series' two machines meet. Gravity from part one's pyramid, collisions from part three's grid, one buffer between them — and a ring of dust that clumps into moons. Stir it and watch it heal.

Scroll down to read ↓

Part one built a structure for the far field — a pyramid of summarized mass, because in a galaxy distant crowds matter in aggregate. Part three built the opposite structure for the near field — a sorted grid of buckets, because water only touches its neighbours. Nothing stops both from living in the same simulation. Gravity from the tree, contacts from the grid — dust attracting, colliding, and sticking. The beginnings of a solar system.

That is this article. The ring above is sixty thousand grains of dust on near-circular orbits around a star. Every grain pulls on every other grain through part one's pyramid; every grain shoves the grains it actually touches through part three's counting sort. Left alone, the ring curdles — clumps condense, collide, and grow into moonlets. It is a simplified, two-dimensional version of planetesimal formation.

Getting there takes three steps: understanding why gravity alone cannot build a planet, freeing part three's grid from its box, and combining the two data structures with dissipative collisions.

Step 1 · GhostsWhy gravity can't finish the job

Part one's softening parameter was introduced as a fix for division by zero. Its real meaning is physical: it declares that bodies have no surface. Below the softening length, gravity fades out instead of diverging, and two particles heading straight at each other slide through like ghosts. For a galaxy this is the correct physics — stars are so small compared to the space between them that when two galaxies collide, essentially no two stars touch. Astronomers call such systems collisionless.

Planets are not made in that world. Watch what gravity alone does to a cold cloud of dust:

The cloud falls inward, as it must. And then it misses. Every grain sails through the centre, out the far side, and back up to nearly the height it fell from — the cloud breathes in and out forever. It cannot settle, and the reason is bookkeeping: gravity is a conservative force. Whatever kinetic energy the collapse gains, the re-expansion spends, exactly. A cloud that interacts only through gravity has no way to get rid of the energy of its own fall. Press the both button and the same cloud does something a galaxy never does — but that's Step 4's story. First the contacts have to survive leaving their box.

Step 2 · Losing the boxA grid with no walls

Part three's grid had a hidden load-bearing wall — four of them. The cell index was computed from position inside a fixed box, and the box made the cell count finite. The tank had walls anyway, so nothing was lost. But a collapsing dust cloud lives in open space, and an orbiting ring flings the occasional grain far away. Cover that with a dense grid and you face a dilemma: make the box huge and waste millions of counters on empty space, or make it tight and watch particles escape it.

The escape hatch: keep the cells, lose the box. A cell is now just a pair of integers — which column, which row, anywhere in the infinite plane — and those integers are hashed into a fixed table of 65,536 buckets: 

fn bucketOf(c: vec2i) -> u32 {
  let h = u32(c.x) * 0x9E3779B1u ^ u32(c.y) * 0x85EBCA77u;
  return h & 65535u;   // which bucket this cell shares
}

Nothing else changes. The histogram counts bucket occupants, the scan finds bucket starts, the scatter groups particles by bucket — part three's machinery runs untouched. The price: unrelated cells, possibly half a world apart, occasionally land in the same bucket, so the 3×3 query dredges up impostors. And the algorithm does not care, because the force pass was always going to measure the distance to every candidate anyway. A real neighbour passes the distance test; an impostor from across the world fails it by a margin of, well, the world. The filter was already installed.

Yellow is your cursor's 3×3 window; red cells are strangers that happen to share its buckets, and red discs are the impostor candidates the distance test throws away. Aside · Hashing was born in a filing cabinet

In 1953, Hans Peter Luhn — an IBM researcher who later gave the world the checksum on your credit card — wrote an internal memo proposing that records be filed not by their full key but by a short scrambled digest of it, with colliding records chained in the same bin. Arnold Dumey published the idea openly in 1956; Knuth traced the lineage back to Luhn. The data structure invented to find insurance records on magnetic drums is exactly what lets twenty thousand dust grains find each other in infinite space.

Step 3 · Combining the solversTwo structures, one buffer

Now combine them. The obstacle is not conceptual — the far field and the near field don't even speak to each other — it is plumbing. Part three's sort permutes the particles every substep: grain #4,071 is somebody else after every shuffle. Part one's solver carried a per-body mass array, indexed by particle number. Bolt them together naively and the masses stay put while the particles dance — every grain inherits a stranger's weight, sixty times a second.

The orthodox fix is to sort the masses alongside (a key–value sort, one more scattered array). The better fix is to delete the assumption that creates the problem: make every grain identical. A speck of protoplanetary dust is not a star; nothing demands variety. If all masses are equal, any permutation of the particles is invisible to gravity — the pyramid sums counts instead of masses — and the mass buffer ceases to exist. Total dust mass is fixed; the particle-count slider only changes how finely it is sampled.

With that, the two structures snap together, every substep, on the same buffer: 

sort     → hash, count, scan, scatter        // part three (4 dispatches + scan)
pyramid  → bounds, mass scatter, reduce ×8   // part one   (11 dispatches)
force    → tree walk + 9 buckets, integrate  // new        (1 dispatch, both answers)

Here is that force kernel's view of the world, one structure at a time:

Note the two structures aren't the same size, and shouldn't be. The pyramid takes its root box from a bounds reduction each substep — it stretches to fit wherever the cloud wanders, because gravity needs to see everything. The hash cells stay locked at one grain diameter forever, because contact physics happens at the scale of a grain. Far field sized to the world, near field sized to the particle. The force kernel is the only place they meet: walk the pyramid for the pull of the whole cloud, walk nine buckets for the shove of the actual neighbours, add, integrate. The star isn't in the buffer at all — a single analytic GM/r² term handles it, the same "constraints are just forces" move as part two's dome.

Aside · The professionals do it this way too

The workhorse codes of planetary science — Derek Richardson and colleagues' pkdgrav (2000) is the standard citation — are tree codes for gravity with a neighbour structure bolted on for collisions, evolving millions of planetesimals per orbit. They run in 3D with better integrators and real collision outcomes, but the skeleton — far field from a tree, near field from spatial bins, every step — is the one on this page.

Step 4 · DissipationLosing energy on purpose

Here is the part that no data-structure cleverness supplies. Go back to the collapsing cloud and try all three buttons:

Gravity only is Step 1's ghost: collapse, miss, re-expand, forever. Contacts only is a handful of marbles tossed in space: they bump, drift, and disperse, because nothing binds them. But both produces something new — the cloud collapses, and this time the fall lands. Grains pile onto grains; the swirl tightens into a dense, slowly-spinning ball with debris settling around it. A planetesimal.

The difference is one term in the contact force. The spring–dashpot contact, unchanged from part three's sand, pushes overlapping grains apart and bleeds off their approach velocity. The dashpot makes collisions inelastic: every bump converts a little ordered falling-motion into nothing (in real dust, into heat). Slide the stickiness slider to zero and the collapse turns into a bouncing swarm that never settles — gravity plus perfectly elastic contact still conserves energy, and conservative systems do not settle. Galaxies remain diffuse because they cannot dissipate collision energy; dust can form compact bodies because it can.

Aside · Newton's pendulums and an unread Russian monograph

Newton measured exactly this property in the Principia — dropping pendulum bobs of steel, glass, and cork against each other and recording how much of the rebound survived. His coefficient of restitution is the dashpot's great-grandparent; the stickiness slider is a restitution knob.

The man who worked out that this is how planets form spent years almost entirely unread. Viktor Safronov, working in Moscow through the 1950s and 60s, built the planetesimal theory — dust settling, colliding, sticking, the survivors sweeping up the stragglers — and published it in 1969 as a slim Russian monograph. A 1972 NASA translation reached a handful of western astronomers, and within a decade his picture had quietly become the foundation of the field. Nearly every planet-formation simulation running today is an elaboration of Safronov's loop: gravity gathers, collisions dissipate, bodies grow.

Step 5 · The diskMoons from a ring

The collapse demo is planet formation with the difficulty turned down — a cloud with nowhere to go but inward. The real nursery is a disk, and a disk fights back. Here is the hero ring again, at full size and with the knobs exposed:

The antagonist in a disk is shear. Orbits are faster on the inside lane — Kepler's third law — so any clump that starts to gather is constantly smeared into a spiral arc by its own halves orbiting at different rates. Watch the early frames: the ring doesn't clot evenly, it grows tight spiral streaks — self-gravity winning locally while shear stretches the winnings. Where enough grains crowd together, a knot condenses out of a streak, collisions kill its internal motion, and a moonlet is born. From then on it sweeps its lane, scattering or swallowing what it meets, and the survivors space themselves out like teeth on a comb.

Aside · Saturn runs this experiment live

Planetary scientists call the end stage oligarchic growth — Kokubo and Ida's simulations of it in the late 1990s used a few thousand planetesimals; the slider above goes to 150,000. And Saturn's A ring is the experiment running where we can watch: a sheet of colliding ice grains whose self-gravity keeps trying to make moons and whose shear keeps tearing them apart. Cassini photographed the stalemate — ephemeral elongated clumps (self-gravity wakes, the spiral streaks above) and hundred-metre "propeller" moonlets that won their local fight but not the war. The stickiness slider moves the front line of that battle.

An important limitation: real planet formation is 3D, its dust is embedded in gas whose drag both saps orbits and — via the streaming instability — actively herds grains into clumps, and real collisions can shatter as well as stick. This demo has none of that; it is a cartoon. But the cartoon's skeleton — a tree for the far field, a sorted grid for the near field, dissipation doing the creating — is the production skeleton, and every refinement is one more force or one more sorted array away.

EpilogueWhat part four teaches

1. Structures compose. The pyramid never learned about the hash grid; the hash grid never learned about the pyramid. Each answers exactly one question — "what does everything far away add up to?", "who is touching me?" — and the force kernel just asks both. Production simulations are not one clever structure; they are libraries of single-purpose ones, composed.

2. Dissipation is where structure comes from. Every force in parts one and two conserved energy, and accordingly nothing in those articles ever became anything — the galaxy was a galaxy at the start and the end. One lossy term in the contact force lets clouds settle into planetesimals and rings into moonlets.

3. Choose data so the structures can't disagree. The sort permutes particles; gravity indexed them by name. The fix wasn't cleverer bookkeeping, it was making the grains identical so the bookkeeping became unnecessary — the same instinct as part one's implicit tree and part three's sort-instead-of-lists: don't manage the hard case, delete it.

There are several clear extensions. A third coordinate folds into both structures and the disk becomes a true 3D nebula. Letting colliding grains merge needs particles to die and masses to vary — which needs stream compaction and a key–value radix sort, both of which are (what else?) scans. Gas drag is one more force. And the moonlets this page grows in seconds took the real disk a hundred thousand years, so larger timesteps or multirate integration would also matter. In each case, the implementation starts by finding a structure that matches the physics, then arranging it for parallel construction.
