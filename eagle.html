The Wren

A new series builds a bird the way this site builds everything: from a function. Twenty-two capsules, one smooth minimum, one polygonizer — and a low-poly wren with a recognizable silhouette. Drag to inspect the model.

This series builds a bird you can fly. It is not a downloaded model with someone else's animations — a creature that exists because a table of numbers says so, rigged by arithmetic, and flapped by physics. The final demo lets you fly it above a procedural world. This first article does the modeling: it turns a description of a wren into an actual mesh, live, in your browser tab.

The world she'll fly over is its own project. A sister series, Ground Truth, starts in parallel with this one and grows the terrain — mountains first, then erosion, grass, and (importantly for a bird) trees. The two series are written to converge: her final article and its final article are the same demo. And the pipeline here is a close cousin of the one that built the bear — this site's other creature — same bones-first philosophy, except he lays his triangles down by hand where the wren grows hers from a field. The approaches differ, but both begin with a table of bones.

Aside · Why a wren

A wren's distinctive silhouette is compact to describe in a table: a round body, no visible neck, a needle of a beak, and a tail held cocked above its back like a raised eyebrow. Every one of those features is one or two lines in the skeleton table below — and the cocked tail, the hardest thing to find in a stock model, is just a capsule with an unusual angle. It also makes her the ideal low-poly subject: you identify a wren by outline alone, which is exactly the part polygons are good at.

Step 1 · The parts listCapsule definition

The wren's single source of truth is a list of capsules — line segments with a radius at each end. A capsule is the ideal primitive for animals because it is exactly what anatomy is: a bone with flesh at some thickness around it. Torso, neck, head, beak, tail, three segments per wing, three per leg — each one is a row with a start point, an end point, two radii, and a few annotations. Fourteen rows are written by hand; left limbs are mirrored by flipping the sign of x, and the bird is twenty-two capsules in all.

Drag the slider and the bird explodes into its rows. Two things are worth noticing while she's apart. First, the folded wing is a Z: humerus back, forearm forward, hand back again, pressed flat against the body — the same three segments that will unfold into a full wingspan in part three. Birds don't have less arm than we do; they keep it folded under the plumage. Second, the wing and tail capsules are squashed — each carries an optional flatten axis that thins it from a sausage into a blade, because feathers are panels, not volumes.

The table is the shared source for the whole series. Part one blends these capsules into a body. Part two will ask, for every vertex of that body, "which capsules are you near?" — and that answer becomes skin weights. Part three reads segment lengths out of the same rows to unfold the wing with inverse kinematics. Change one number — make the beak longer, the body rounder — and the bird is re-sculpted, re-rigged, and re-animated to match, because nothing downstream ever stored its own copy.

Step 2 · The blendSigned-distance composition

Twenty-two capsules drawn as separate meshes look like parts snapped together. An animal needs shoulders that flow into the torso and a neck that isn't a seam. So the capsules are combined not as geometry but as a function: f(p) returns the signed distance from any point in space to the bird's surface — negative inside, positive outside, zero exactly on the skin. Each capsule has such a distance; the bird is all of them folded together with a smooth minimum: 

smin(a, b, k):
  h = clamp(0.5 + 0.5·(b − a)/k, 0, 1)
  return mix(b, a, h) − k·h·(1 − h)    // min(a,b), but with a fillet of width k

A plain min of two distance fields gives the union of the shapes, crease included. The polynomial term adds negative distance wherever the two fields are nearly tied — which is precisely the seam — and the crease becomes a fillet of width k. Every row of the table carries its own k: the belly merges into the torso across a wide soft zone, while the beak keeps a crisp k of about a centimeter, because a wren's beak does not blob into its face.

This is the field itself, sliced like an MRI: a plane of constant x, colored by distance — contour bands outside, warm bands inside, and the bright line at exactly zero, which is the only part the polygonizer will keep. Slide the plane off-center and watch the wing and leg cross-sections appear; drag the blend width down to zero and the anatomy decomposes into hard-edged capsule unions — the fillets are doing more work than you'd guess.

Deep dive: squashing a capsule without breaking the math

The flattened wing panels use an approximation. To thin a capsule along an axis n by a factor s, measure the offset from the segment as usual, then stretch its component along n by 1/s before taking the length: points that approach along n now seem farther away, so the surface sits closer — a blade instead of a tube. The result is no longer an exact distance field (gradients shear a little under the squash), but the polygonizer only needs a reliable sign and the zero to be in the right place, and both survive. The same trick scaled to three axes is how every "ellipsoid" in the SDF literature is actually built.

Step 3 · Surface extractionSurface nets

The field defines the surface; now we need triangles. The ferrofluid article did this in two dimensions with marching squares — sixteen corner cases, segments on cell edges — and the classic 3D versions, marching cubes and marching tetrahedra, scale the same idea up. This article takes the other road, the dual one: surface nets.

Walk every cell of a grid and ask the eight corners for their signs. Marching cubes would now look up which edges the surface crosses and place vertices on those edges, several per cell, stitched by a 256-case table. Surface nets instead places one vertex inside the cell — at the average of all its edge crossings — and builds faces from adjacency: every grid edge that crosses the surface is shared by exactly four cells, and those four cells' vertices form one quad. No tables, no cases, no saddle ambiguities; the entire algorithm averages edge crossings and connects neighboring cells.

The resolution slider is the whole economics of the technique. At 16 the body becomes too rounded, and legs thinner than a cell simply do not exist, because a surface the lattice can't see can't vote. By 48 — the default — she has toes, and the facets are the size that reads as a style rather than an accident. Toggle the wireframe and the dual structure shows directly: mostly quads, flowing in grid-aligned rivers, one vertex per cell, never more.

The smooth ⇄ faceted button is the article's one aesthetic decision, made visible. The same surface can be shaded two ways. Smooth: each vertex asks the field for its gradient — which for a distance field is exactly the surface normal — and the facets melt into soft organic curvature. Faceted: every triangle keeps one flat normal and one flat color, and the bird becomes intentionally low-poly. This series ships the faceted look, and not to save rendering time — at these triangle counts both are inexpensive. Flat shading gives the mesh a crisp silhouette, readable at a distance, that will stay readable when she's a speck banking over a valley in the finale. The smooth gradient normals aren't wasted, though — they're still consulted once per triangle, to tell each face which way is out.

Aside · Surface nets in medical imaging

Surface nets were published by Sarah Gibson at MERL in 1998 — for medical imaging, not graphics. Binary-segmented MRI volumes meshed with marching cubes came out terraced, like contour-line staircases; Gibson's "elastic" nets placed vertices inside cells and relaxed them toward smoothness while keeping each vertex inside its cell so the surface couldn't drift off the data. Four years later, dual contouring added one idea — place the vertex where the field's gradients vote, not at the plain average — and suddenly sharp creases and corners survived polygonization. The ferrofluid article promised that upgrade for spike tips; this wren's beak tip, gently rounded by the averaging, illustrates the same limitation.

Step 4 · PlumageMaterial zones by proximity

The mesh comes out of the polygonizer naked. The paint job reuses machinery we already have: each face asks every capsule "how far is your centroid from me?" and takes the owner — the nearest one — as its color zone. The table's rows carry zone tags (body, breast, wing, tail, beak, brow, leg), so the buff breast and the pale supercilium — the eyebrow stripe that makes a wren look permanently unimpressed — are not painted on; they are capsules, placed in the table, claiming their territory by distance.

On top of the zones, two cheap textures, both applied per-face because per-face is the style: a hashed mottle (every facet dims or brightens itself by a few percent, keyed on its own index — the subtle patchwork that makes low-poly read as crafted rather than unfinished) and dark barring across the wings and tail — a sine wave along the feather direction, clipped into stripes. Real wrens are barred everywhere; ours is barred where it costs three lines.

The eyes are not in the field. An eyeball is a three-millimeter gloss sphere; resolving it would need a lattice fine enough to waste on everything else. So the eyes and their glints are riders — faceted icosahedra parented to where the head capsule says the head is. When part two gives the skeleton joints, they will ride the head joint for free. The fluff slider is the table's leverage demonstrated: it scales one number into every radius before the field is rebuilt, and the wren puffs up against the cold exactly the way the real bird does.

CodaPreparing for rigging

The mesh is currently frozen in rest pose. Part two will use the capsule table a second life as a skeleton — each vertex weighted to the bones it lives near, so the body deforms when joints rotate. Part three unfolds the wing Z with inverse kinematics and finds the wingbeat. Part four stops faking it: lift, drag, and flapping thrust as actual forces, with banking turns falling out of the physics. Part five adds landing — the approach, the flare, the toes-on-branch problem — and to sing, because a syrinx is two oscillators and a grammar, and we have a whole music series of parts to borrow. Part six hands you the controls.

And under all of it, the other series is building the place. Ground Truth starts with mountains from noise this week; by its middle parts there will be grass to skim and trees with actual branches — branches whose positions her landing controller will read like an instrument.
